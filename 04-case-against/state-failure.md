# The State Failure Objection

**Executive summary.** The objection: even granting every word of the diagnosis, the proposal hands the state an enormous new revenue stream, a comprehensive register of private wealth, and the power to value and seize assets at scale — and the historical record of states wielding such powers runs from waste through drift to abuse. This file states the public-choice, historical, and Hayekian versions of that case at full strength, assesses the proposal's structural answers (direct-to-citizen design, independent administration, comparison with existing powers), and concedes the honest residual: this objection is decisive in some polities, which is an argument about *where*, not *whether*.

## The objection at full strength

### 1. Public choice: feeding Leviathan

Brennan and Buchanan's *The Power to Tax* models government not as a benevolent optimiser but as a revenue-maximising Leviathan constrained only by the constitution of its fiscal powers [1]. On this view the question is never "what would an ideal state do with inheritance revenue?" but "what will an actual state, staffed by actual coalitions, do with a new claim on the largest untaxed asset pool in the economy?"

Public-choice predictions are specific:

- the threshold will drift downward in real terms whenever revenue is needed (the ratchet that took UK income tax from a 1799 war measure to a permanent mass tax);
- the endowment hypothecation will be raided;
- the carve-outs will be colonised by whichever lobbies organise best;
- the bureaucracy administering the tax will grow, acquire its own interests, and resist repeal regardless of performance.

Niskanen's budget-maximising bureau and Tullock's rent-seeking analysis both apply with unusual force to a tax requiring discretionary valuation of every large private asset [2]. The objection notes, pointedly, that the manifesto's own section [`../10-opposition/sabotage-after-passage.md`](../10-opposition/sabotage-after-passage.md) assumes bad-faith administration is possible — it cannot then assume good faith when convenient.

### 2. The historical record of confiscatory powers

Where states have held near-total powers over private wealth transfer, the record includes:

- revolutionary expropriations that destroyed productive capacity and fed patronage machines rather than public purposes;
- mid-century exchange controls and capital levies administered with wide official discretion and predictable arbitrariness;
- and — the sharpest cautionary case for a *register* — the use of comprehensive civil registries by occupying and authoritarian regimes to locate and strip target populations (the Netherlands' meticulous pre-war population records, used under occupation to identify Jewish citizens, are the standard example in the surveillance literature [3]).

Less dramatically, every existing estate-tax administration generates a steady stream of valuation disputes in which the state's incentive is systematically upward. Taxpayers facing a 40% rate litigate; at 100% the taxpayer's stake in a valuation dispute is total, and the state's discretionary power correspondingly maximal.

Corruption risk scales with discretion times stakes: a bureaucracy that values, defers, takes equity in, and claw-backs private businesses ([`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md)) holds precisely the portfolio of discretions that, in most of the world, is a machine for bribery and favouritism. The objection does not claim the UK or Denmark is "most of the world"; it claims that institutions should be designed for the polity's worst plausible decade, not its best.

### 3. The register as surveillance infrastructure

The Central Register of Receipts ([`../02-proposal/mechanism.md`](../02-proposal/mechanism.md)) is, structurally, a lifetime ledger of every citizen's capital receipts, linked to identity, updated in real time. The objection: such infrastructure never stays single-purpose. Data built for tax flows to security, immigration, and political uses under later governments of unknown character; the relevant time horizon for evaluating state infrastructure is not this parliament but the worst government the constitution permits. A polity should build wealth-surveillance apparatus only if it would be content for its least trusted future government to inherit it.

### 4. Hypothecation does not survive contact with the Treasury

The proposal's central political safeguard — revenue hypothecated to the citizen's endowment — has a poor survival record as a mechanism:

| Precedent | Outcome |
|---|---|
| UK Road Fund (1920) | Raided by Churchill as Chancellor from 1926; hypothecation formally ended by the Finance Act 1936, effective April 1937 [4] |
| National Insurance Fund | In substance pay-as-you-go; the "fund" framing persists rhetorically only |
| UK National Lottery "additionality" | The 1998 New Opportunities Fund channelled lottery money into health and education objectives traditionally funded from general taxation, in what critics called a direct breach of the additionality principle [5] |
| US Social Security trust fund | Continuous political contestation over the reality of the fund |

Treasuries dislike hypothecation for defensible reasons (it hard-wires misallocation), which is exactly why they dismantle it. The objection predicts: within two decades the endowment will be means-tested, deferred, or quietly de-indexed, while the tax remains — leaving the confiscation without the compensation, the precise configuration the manifesto itself calls indefensible ([`00-executive-summary.md`](../00-executive-summary.md)).

### 5. The Hayekian knowledge problem in the state-equity portfolio

The business carve-out makes the state a preferred-equity holder in thousands of private firms for up to 30 years. Hayek's calculation argument applies directly: the state cannot know when to exit, how to price buy-backs, which claw-backs to enforce against which struggling firms in which regions — and every one of those discretionary decisions is a lobbying surface [6]. The historical analogue is not reassuring: state holding companies began as temporary rescue vehicles and became permanent patronage empires — IRI in Italy, the canonical case, was created in 1933 as an emergency rescue measure, given permanent legal form in 1937, and not dissolved until 2002 [6]. The carve-out designed to save the tax from the family-business objection ([`family-business.md`](family-business.md)) thus imports a standing industrial-policy apparatus through the back door.

## Assessment

### What the design already answers

1. **Direct-to-citizen architecture.** The strongest structural answer: the revenue's destination is a flat, universal, unconditional transfer — the single hardest form of spending to corrupt, means-test quietly, or direct to clients, because every deviation is visible to every 24-year-old in the country. Public-choice logic cuts both ways: universal entitlements (NHS, state pension, Social Security) are precisely the programmes that have proven *most* resistant to raiding, because they create a universal defending constituency ([`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md)). The hypothecation-drift precedents in the table above all concern funds with *no* individually named beneficiaries; a named entitlement at 25, on the Child Trust Fund / state-pension model, is a different political object. The design deliberately converts an appropriable revenue stream into ten million individual claims.
2. **Independent administration and entrenchment options.** Valuation by an arm's-length body with judicial appeal (the existing tribunal system already does this for IHT); statutory indexation formulas rather than annual discretion ([`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md)); the state-equity portfolio held by an independent fund on the Norwegian sovereign-wealth governance model with a statutory non-intervention mandate; sunset-and-review clauses; supermajority locks on threshold reductions. None of these is proof against a determined future majority — nothing is — but the same is true of every fiscal institution, and the specific sabotage vectors are catalogued and countered in [`../10-opposition/sabotage-after-passage.md`](../10-opposition/sabotage-after-passage.md) and [`../15-transition/grandfathering.md`](../15-transition/grandfathering.md).
3. **The marginal new power is smaller than it appears.** HMRC *already* values every taxable estate, art collection, private business, and farm at death; already charges and collects at 40%; already maintains lifetime gift records for the 7-year rule; already litigates valuations before tribunals. The IRS does the equivalent. The delta, stated precisely:

| Power | Status quo (UK) | Under the proposal | Genuinely new? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Valuing estates, businesses, farms, chattels at death | HMRC + Valuation Office Agency, routine | Same machinery, higher stakes | No — stakes only |
| Charging and collecting death-time tax | 40% with reliefs | 100% above recipient threshold | No — rate only |
| Tracking lifetime gifts | 7-year look-back, self-reported | Lifetime cumulation ledger per recipient | **Yes** — the Register of Receipts |
| Deferred payment and charges over assets | 10-year instalment option | 30-year deferral with secured charge | Extension, not novelty |
| State equity in private firms | None (outside crisis rescues) | Optional non-voting preferred stakes | **Yes** — the equity portfolio |
| Wealth information | AML/KYC, Land Registry, Companies House PSC register, CRS exchange | Consolidated receipts ledger | Consolidation, not creation |

   The two genuinely new powers — the ledger and the equity portfolio — are where the objection should concentrate, and where the design must answer ([`../07-implementation/administration.md`](../07-implementation/administration.md), [`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md)). On surveillance specifically: the honest reply to "the worst future government will inherit this" is that the worst future government inherits the existing informational apparatus anyway; the marginal risk is real but incremental, and data-minimisation design (receipts only — no asset census of the living) can bound it.
4. **Rate-discretion is lower, not higher, than the status quo.** A binary 0/100 structure with a formula-indexed threshold leaves *less* room for annual fiscal tinkering than the current system of rates, bands, reliefs, and taper rules — each of which is a lobbying surface today ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) makes the parallel argument on reliefs).

### What the design cannot answer

- The Hayekian critique of the equity portfolio stands as a real, priced cost — acknowledged as such in [`family-business.md`](family-business.md). The mitigation (independent fund, non-voting stakes, statutory buy-back pricing) reduces but does not eliminate discretion.
- No constitutional device binds a future sovereign parliament. The endowment's defence is ultimately political — the universal constituency — not legal. If that constituency fails to form, the raid scenario is live.
- The corruption argument is empirically conditional. Valuation-with-total-stakes *is* a bribery surface; whether it corrupts depends on baseline institutional quality, prosecutorial independence, and pay-and-audit design.

## What would change the assessment

- **Against the proposal:** documented endowment raids or threshold ratchets in any early-adopting jurisdiction; evidence that the independent-fund model fails to insulate the equity portfolio from ministerial direction (the Norwegian fund's governance record — reviewed annually in a white paper to the Storting [8] — is the live test case); a pattern of valuation corruption emerging even in high-capacity administrations once stakes reach 100%.
- **For the proposal:** continued resilience of universal entitlements against retrenchment relative to targeted programmes (the existing comparative welfare-state literature — [Korpi & Palme's "paradox of redistribution"](https://ideas.repec.org/p/lis/liswps/174.html) — already points this way); successful multi-decade operation of the deferral book by an existing administration analogue, e.g. Irish CAT instalments or the UK's post-2024 APR arrangements.

## Verdict

The objection survives as a *conditionality*, not a refutation.

In a polity with strong courts, professional tax administration, low corruption, and functioning audit institutions — the UK, the Nordics, most of the OECD core — the marginal state-failure risk of this design over the status quo is modest, the genuinely new powers are two and bounded, and the direct-to-citizen architecture is a better-than-existing safeguard against the revenue-capture scenario.

In a polity without those things, the objection is close to decisive: a 100% tax administered by a corruptible valuation bureaucracy would be a catastrophe of selective enforcement, and no design feature on paper would prevent it. That is an argument about **where** the policy is adoptable, not **whether** it is sound — and it is treated as exactly that in [`../11-geographies/adoption-criteria.md`](../11-geographies/adoption-criteria.md), which makes state capacity and institutional trust explicit gating criteria.

The residual that stands everywhere: the proposal asks citizens to extend significant new trust to fiscal institutions at a historical moment when such trust is scarce, and the burden of demonstrating trustworthiness — through design, transparency, and sequencing ([`../15-transition/pilots.md`](../15-transition/pilots.md), [`../15-transition/sequencing.md`](../15-transition/sequencing.md)) — sits with the proposal, permanently.
