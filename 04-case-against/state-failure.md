# The State Failure Objection

**Executive summary.** The objection: even granting every word of the diagnosis, the proposal hands the state an enormous new revenue stream, a comprehensive register of private wealth, and the power to value and seize assets at scale — and the historical record of states wielding such powers runs from waste through drift to abuse. This file states the public-choice, historical, and Hayekian versions of that case at full strength, assesses the proposal's structural answers (direct-to-citizen design, independent administration, comparison with existing powers), and concedes the honest residual: this objection is decisive in some polities, which is an argument about *where*, not *whether*.

## The objection at full strength

### 1. Public choice: feeding Leviathan

Brennan and Buchanan's *The Power to Tax* models government not as a benevolent optimiser but as a revenue-maximising Leviathan constrained only by the constitution of its fiscal powers [1]. On this view the question is never "what would an ideal state do with inheritance revenue?" but "what will an actual state, staffed by actual coalitions, do with a new claim on the largest untaxed asset pool in the economy?" Public-choice predictions are specific: the threshold will drift downward in real terms whenever revenue is needed (the ratchet that took UK income tax from a 1799 war measure to a permanent mass tax); the endowment hypothecation will be raided; the carve-outs will be colonised by whichever lobbies organise best; and the bureaucracy administering it will grow, acquire its own interests, and resist repeal regardless of performance. Niskanen's budget-maximising bureau and Tullock's rent-seeking analysis both apply with unusual force to a tax requiring discretionary valuation of every large private asset [2]. The objection notes, pointedly, that the manifesto's own section [`../10-opposition/sabotage-after-passage.md`](../10-opposition/sabotage-after-passage.md) assumes bad-faith administration is possible — it cannot then assume good faith when convenient.

### 2. The historical record of confiscatory powers

Where states have held near-total powers over private wealth transfer, the record includes: revolutionary expropriations that destroyed productive capacity and fed patronage machines; mid-century exchange controls and capital levies administered arbitrarily; and — the sharpest cautionary case for a *register* — the use of comprehensive civil registries by occupying and authoritarian regimes to locate and strip target populations (the Netherlands' meticulous pre-war population records are the standard example in the surveillance literature — to verify framing) [3]. Less dramatically, every existing estate-tax administration generates a steady stream of valuation disputes in which the state's incentive is systematically upward; taxpayers facing a 40% rate litigate, but at 100% the taxpayer's stake in a valuation dispute is total, and the state's discretionary power correspondingly maximal. Corruption risk scales with discretion times stakes: a bureaucracy that values, defers, takes equity in, and claw-backs private businesses ([`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md)) holds precisely the portfolio of discretions that, in most of the world, is a machine for bribery and favoritism.

### 3. The register as surveillance infrastructure

The Central Register of Receipts ([`../02-proposal/mechanism.md`](../02-proposal/mechanism.md)) is, structurally, a lifetime ledger of every citizen's capital receipts, linked to identity, updated in real time. The objection: such infrastructure never stays single-purpose. Data built for tax flows to security, immigration, and political uses under later governments of unknown character; the relevant time horizon for evaluating state infrastructure is not this parliament but the worst government the constitution permits. A polity should build wealth-surveillance apparatus only if it would be content for its least trusted future government to inherit it.

### 4. Hypothecation does not survive contact with the Treasury

The proposal's central political safeguard — revenue hypothecated to the citizen's endowment — has a poor survival record as a mechanism:

| Precedent | Outcome |
|---|---|
| UK Road Fund (1909) | Raided by Churchill as Chancellor from 1926; hypothecation formally abandoned 1937 (to verify dates) [4] |
| National Insurance Fund | In substance pay-as-you-go; the "fund" framing persists rhetorically only |
| UK National Lottery "additionality" | Documented drift of lottery funding into activities previously funded from general taxation (to verify NAO/DCMS sources) [5] |
| US Social Security trust fund | Continuous political contestation over the reality of the fund |

Treasuries dislike hypothecation for defensible reasons (it hard-wires misallocation), which is exactly why they dismantle it. The objection predicts: within two decades the endowment will be means-tested, deferred, or quietly de-indexed, while the tax remains — leaving the confiscation without the compensation, the precise configuration the manifesto itself calls indefensible ([`00-executive-summary.md`](../00-executive-summary.md)).

### 5. The Hayekian knowledge problem in the state-equity portfolio

The business carve-out makes the state a preferred-equity holder in thousands of private firms for up to 30 years. Hayek's calculation argument applies directly: the state cannot know when to exit, how to price buy-backs, which claw-backs to enforce against which struggling firms in which regions — and every one of those discretionary decisions is a lobbying surface [6]. The historical analogue is not reassuring: state holding companies (IRI in Italy is the canonical case) began as temporary rescue vehicles and became permanent patronage empires (to verify characterisation). The carve-out designed to save the tax from the family-business objection ([`family-business.md`](family-business.md)) thus imports a standing industrial-policy apparatus through the back door.

## Assessment

### What the design already answers

1. **Direct-to-citizen architecture.** The strongest structural answer: the revenue's destination is a flat, universal, unconditional transfer — the single hardest form of spending to corrupt, means-test quietly, or direct to clients, because every deviation is visible to every 24-year-old in the country. Public-choice logic cuts both ways: universal entitlements (NHS, state pension, Social Security) are precisely the programmes that have proven *most* resistant to raiding, because they create a universal defending constituency ([`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md)). The hypothecation-drift precedents in the table above all concern funds with *no* individually named beneficiaries; a named entitlement at 25, on the Child Trust Fund / state-pension model, is a different political object. The design deliberately converts an appropriable revenue stream into ten million individual claims.
2. **Independent administration and entrenchment options.** Valuation by an arm's-length body with judicial appeal (the existing tribunal system already does this for IHT); statutory indexation formulas rather than annual discretion ([`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md)); the state-equity portfolio held by an independent fund on the Norwegian sovereign-wealth governance model with a statutory non-intervention mandate; sunset-and-review clauses; supermajority locks on threshold reductions. None of these is proof against a determined future majority — nothing is — but the same is true of every fiscal institution, and the specific sabotage vectors are catalogued and countered in [`../10-opposition/sabotage-after-passage.md`](../10-opposition/sabotage-after-passage.md) and [`../15-transition/grandfathering.md`](../15-transition/grandfathering.md).
3. **The marginal new power is smaller than it appears.** HMRC *already* values every taxable estate, art collection, private business, and farm at death; already charges and collects at 40%; already maintains lifetime gift records for the 7-year rule; already litigates valuations before tribunals. The IRS does the equivalent. The genuinely new elements are the lifetime cumulation ledger and the rate — significant, but the objection's imagery of a wholly novel seizure apparatus overstates the delta. On surveillance specifically: banks' existing AML/KYC reporting, land registries, and Companies House beneficial-ownership registers mean the informational state largely exists; the Register of Receipts consolidates rather than creates it. The honest reply to "the worst future government will inherit this" is that the worst future government inherits the existing apparatus anyway; the marginal risk is real but incremental, and data-minimisation design (receipts only, no asset census of the living) can bound it ([`../07-implementation/administration.md`](../07-implementation/administration.md)).
4. **Rate-discretion is lower, not higher, than the status quo.** A binary 0/100 structure with a formula-indexed threshold leaves *less* room for annual fiscal tinkering than the current system of rates, bands, reliefs, and taper rules — each of which is a lobbying surface today ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) makes the parallel argument on reliefs).

### What the design cannot answer

- The Hayekian critique of the equity portfolio stands as a real, priced cost — acknowledged as such in [`family-business.md`](family-business.md). The mitigation (independent fund, non-voting stakes, statutory buy-back pricing) reduces but does not eliminate discretion.
- No constitutional device binds a future sovereign parliament. The endowment's defence is ultimately political — the universal constituency — not legal. If that constituency fails to form, the raid scenario is live.
- The corruption argument is empirically conditional. Valuation-with-total-stakes *is* a bribery surface; whether it corrupts depends on baseline institutional quality, prosecutorial independence, and pay-and-audit design.

## Verdict

The objection survives as a *conditionality*, not a refutation. In a polity with strong courts, professional tax administration, low corruption, and functioning audit institutions — the UK, the Nordics, most of the OECD core — the marginal state-failure risk of this design over the status quo is modest and the direct-to-citizen architecture is a better-than-existing safeguard. In a polity without those things, the objection is close to decisive: a 100% tax administered by a corruptible valuation bureaucracy would be a catastrophe of selective enforcement. That is an argument about **where** the policy is adoptable, not **whether** it is sound — and it is treated as exactly that in [`../11-geographies/adoption-criteria.md`](../11-geographies/adoption-criteria.md), which makes state capacity and institutional trust explicit gating criteria. The residual that stands everywhere: the proposal asks citizens to extend significant new trust to fiscal institutions at a historical moment when such trust is scarce, and the burden of demonstrating trustworthiness — through design, transparency, and sequencing ([`../15-transition/pilots.md`](../15-transition/pilots.md)) — sits with the proposal, permanently. Quick-fire versions: [`../09-objections/practicality.md`](../09-objections/practicality.md) and [`../09-objections/slippery-slope.md`](../09-objections/slippery-slope.md).
