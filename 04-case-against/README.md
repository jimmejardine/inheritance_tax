# 04 — The Case Against

A serious manifesto must steelman its opposition. Five strongest objections. Each must be answered directly, not dismissed.

**The steelman rule for this section.** Every file gives the objection its strongest possible form *first* — as its best advocates would state it, citing their actual thinkers and evidence — before any response is offered. Engagement with counter-evidence includes evidence against our own thesis, and verdicts are permitted to leave residuals standing: where an objection survives in weakened form, the file says so and states what the proposal's answer costs, rather than defining the conflict away. Quick-fire rebuttals belong in [../09-objections/](../09-objections/README.md); the design answers belong in [../02-proposal/](../02-proposal/README.md) and [../07-implementation/](../07-implementation/README.md). This section is where sceptical readers will judge the whole project, and it is written accordingly.

## 1. The family business / farm objection

The hard case: an illiquid productive business or working farm valued at £20m but with no cash to pay tax. Historical estate taxes have repeatedly failed here, forcing breakups or sales to larger players — accelerating the very consolidation the policy opposes. Every serious proposal needs a robust mechanism (equity stakes, long deferral, operating-asset carve-outs) or it dies politically at the first televised farm auction. This is the single most important objection to engineer around.

## 2. Capital flight and avoidance

Wealth is mobile. High-net-worth individuals can change residence, restructure into discretionary trusts, shift assets into art/crypto/offshore vehicles, or simply give early. Sweden (2004), Norway (2014), Austria (2008), and Canada (1972) abolished their inheritance taxes partly because revenue was low relative to enforcement costs and wealthy emigration. A serious design requires either international coordination or aggressive domestic anti-avoidance (exit taxes, worldwide taxation, look-through rules on trusts).

## 3. Parental motivation and savings

Empirical question: does the prospect of bequeathing to children drive saving, risk-taking, entrepreneurship? The literature is mixed. Bequest motives exist but appear weaker than folk intuition assumes — most estates are not actively planned; most accumulation appears driven by precaution and status rather than dynastic intent. Still, the effect is plausibly nonzero and must be engaged with quantitatively.

## 4. Liberty and autonomy

The libertarian case. What a person has legitimately earned, they should be free to dispose of, including by giving to their children. This is a genuine normative commitment, not a bad-faith rationalisation. The honest response is not that it is wrong but that it conflicts with other legitimate commitments (equality of opportunity, democratic legitimacy) and the trade-off must be argued, not hand-waved. Note the symmetry: the *heir's* liberty to a life of their own choosing is also at stake.

## 5. State failure

Even granting the diagnosis, giving the state the resulting revenue assumes competent, non-corrupt, welfare-enhancing use. Historical record on state management of redistributed wealth is mixed at best. The universal-endowment design partly addresses this by bypassing discretionary spending — the money goes directly to citizens — but does not eliminate the concern.
