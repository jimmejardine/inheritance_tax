# "They'll Just Spend It All": The Depletion Objections

**Executive summary.** The depletion family of objections — the rich will consume everything, divert everything to charity, or deliberately die broke, so the tax collects nothing — is unique in this repo because its premise, taken seriously, describes the policy *succeeding*: capital returned to circulation or to genuine philanthropy, and no dynasty formed, is the objective, with revenue a secondary benefit. The empirical kicker is that the very wealthy demonstrably cannot consume fast enough to matter.

Rules of engagement: concede, one-liner, full answer. Master table in [README.md](README.md). Behavioural evidence lives in [`../16-second-order/savings-consumption.md`](../16-second-order/savings-consumption.md).

A note on why this file exists separately from [economic.md](economic.md): the depletion objections are not claims that the tax will *harm* the economy but that it will *fail on its own terms* — clever framing, because it lets the objector sound sympathetic while predicting futility. The consistent counter-move throughout is to restate the policy's actual terms: dynastic break first, revenue second.

## "The rich will just spend it all before they die"

**Concede first.** A 100% marginal rate above the threshold is the strongest possible incentive to not die holding excess wealth, so late-life consumption should be expected to rise at the margin; any honest model says so.

**One-line answer.** Good — that is substantially the point: wealth consumed returns to circulation as spending, wages, and VAT, and a fortune consumed is a dynasty that never forms.

**Full answer.** Unpack what "spending it all" physically means:

- **Consumption is circulation.** Buying goods and services employs people, pays taxed wages, bears VAT, and transfers the money to counterparties who are overwhelmingly less wealthy than the spender. The Treasury collects along the way; the capital stock changes hands through markets instead of through bloodlines.
- **Carnegie proposed this as the ideal, not the loophole.** His dictum — the man who dies rich dies disgraced — envisioned surplus wealth administered and dispersed within the holder's lifetime rather than entailed on heirs ([`../05-history/carnegie-1889.md`](../05-history/carnegie-1889.md)). The objection imagines a gotcha; it is describing the [*Gospel of Wealth*](https://www.carnegie.org/about/our-history/gospelofwealth/).
- **There is an empirical ceiling.** Saving rates rise steeply with lifetime income — the rich save more, on average and at the margin ([Dynan, Skinner and Zeldes 2004](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/abs/10.1086/381475) [1]) — so consumption's share of resources falls as wealth rises: there are only so many meals, houses, and yachts a household can use, and at the billion-pound scale genuine consumption cannot make a dent within a lifetime.
- **The residual channels** — asset-price effects of decumulation, luxury-sector composition — are second-order questions treated in [`../16-second-order/asset-prices.md`](../16-second-order/asset-prices.md) and [`../16-second-order/savings-consumption.md`](../16-second-order/savings-consumption.md).

**Deep treatment:** [`../16-second-order/savings-consumption.md`](../16-second-order/savings-consumption.md) · [`../16-second-order/asset-prices.md`](../16-second-order/asset-prices.md)

## "They'll give it all to charity and you'll collect nothing"

**Concede first.** The charitable exemption is a genuine diversion channel, it will be used at scale, and history shows foundation structures being used to preserve family control while claiming the relief — the risk is real and specific.

**One-line answer.** Charitable diversion is a designed feature, not a leak — genuine philanthropy is the Carnegie outcome the policy wants — and the exemption comes with anti-cycling rules so that "charity" which quietly keeps the family in the money is pierced and taxed.

**Full answer.** Separate the two things this objection bundles:

- **Genuine charitable giving** — capital irrevocably committed to public purposes — is a policy success: it ends the dynasty (the family no longer owns it) and funds public goods without touching the Treasury. If the tax's main behavioural effect were a philanthropic surge, that would be a strange definition of failure.
- **Fake charity is the actual threat:** the perpetual family foundation with heirs as salaried trustees, assets circling back through benefits in kind, or control (rather than money) as the real bequest.
- **The anti-cycling design meets it:** independence requirements for governance, minimum annual payout rules, prohibition of benefit to the donor's family, and look-through where control functions as ownership ([`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md), [`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md)).
- **The honest residue** — mega-foundations as unaccountable private power even when honestly charitable — is examined in [`../16-second-order/charitable-giving.md`](../16-second-order/charitable-giving.md) and [`../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md`](../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md).

**Deep treatment:** [`../16-second-order/charitable-giving.md`](../16-second-order/charitable-giving.md) · [`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md) · [`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md)

## "They'll die broke and the tax raises nothing"

**Concede first.** It is arithmetically possible: a cohort of wealthy households that fully decumulates leaves the 100% band empty, and any revenue projection must model that behavioural response rather than assume static estates ([`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md) does).

**One-line answer.** "Die broke" is the policy's structural objective achieved at zero enforcement cost — no dynasty, capital dispersed through markets and philanthropy during life — and the endowment's funding plan is stress-tested against exactly this scenario rather than depending on maximal estates.

**Full answer.** Keep the objective hierarchy straight:

- **This is a distributional-primary policy** ([practicality.md](practicality.md#it-raises-trivial-revenue-so-why-bother)). The problem being solved is hereditary concentration of wealth and the power it converts into ([`../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md`](../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md), [`../01-problem/political-capture.md`](../01-problem/political-capture.md)).
- **A wealthy generation that dissipates its fortunes** among employees, vendors, charities, and up-to-threshold family transfers has solved that problem *voluntarily* — the tax worked as a deterrent works, without collecting.
- **Full decumulation is the opposite of observed behaviour.** Under the existing 40% UK IHT — already a strong incentive to die light — aggregate bequest flows keep rising: the real value of inheritances transferred annually in the UK has roughly doubled every 20 years since 1979 and now exceeds £100bn a year [2], and IHT receipts hit a record £8.2bn in 2024–25 [3]. The very wealthy systematically die with large estates because accumulation is driven by control, status, and precaution more than by spending plans ([economic.md](economic.md#it-kills-the-incentive-to-build-businesses)).
- **The realistic revenue case sits between the corners,** and the endowment's fiscal bridge — including phase-in funding while behaviour settles — is designed on those middle scenarios in [`../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md`](../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md) and [`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md).

**Deep treatment:** [`../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md`](../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md) · [`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md) · [`../16-second-order/macro.md`](../16-second-order/macro.md)

## The unifying reply

All three objections assume the policy's goal is to maximise Treasury receipts, then observe that behaviour will reduce receipts. But the goal is to end the untaxed transmission of dynastic fortunes, and each behaviour the objections predict is a *route to that goal*:

| Predicted behaviour | Who ends up with the money | Dynasty formed? |
|---|---|---|
| Spend it all | Workers, vendors, the VAT base | No |
| Give it to charity (genuine) | Public purposes, irrevocably | No |
| Die broke | Some mix of the above, plus up-to-threshold heirs | No |
| Comply and pay | The endowment fund — every 25-year-old | No |

The tax cannot be dodged by depletion, because depletion is compliance. The only true failure modes are avoidance that preserves dynastic transmission in disguise — covered in [avoidance.md](avoidance.md) — and revenue falling short of the endowment's needs, which is a bridging problem ([`../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md`](../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md)), not a refutation.

In debate, this family of objections is best welcomed rather than resisted: the objector has usually just conceded the policy's behavioural power ("it will change what the rich do with their money") and disputes only where the money lands. Every landing spot in the table above is better, on the project's own diagnosis ([`../01-problem/README.md`](../01-problem/README.md)), than the default — intact transmission to heirs. Say so, and let the objector explain why dynastic transfer beats wages, charity, or a universal endowment.

**Common escalation to expect:** "but the spending will be wasteful — superyachts, not schools." Two-part reply:

- Waste was already the counterfactual: the fortune sitting in an heir's portfolio funds nothing public either, and the heir's consumption is subject to no better taste. At least the spender's yacht pays shipwrights, crew, and VAT.
- The comparison the policy cares about is not yacht vs school but *dynasty vs no dynasty* — and on that axis, late-life consumption and the endowment point the same way ([`../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md`](../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md)).
