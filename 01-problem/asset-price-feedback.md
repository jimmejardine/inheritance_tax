# The Asset-Price Feedback Loop

**Executive summary.** The other chapters in this section each describe a segment of a single circuit: cheap money inflates asset prices, asset inflation delivers windfalls to incumbent owners, those windfalls fund heirs' deposits, heir demand pushes asset prices higher still, and each turn of the loop deepens the divide between those inside and outside the asset economy. The corollary is under-appreciated even by sympathisers: inheritance taxation is not merely fiscal policy but macro-financial policy — the missing damper in a system whose monetary stabilisers systematically enrich incumbents.

## Key findings

- The post-1980 decline in real interest rates, accelerated by post-2008 quantitative easing, mechanically raises the present value of all long-duration assets — housing, equities, land — independent of any change in their productive capacity. Asset holders received a valuation windfall for the act of already owning [1][2].
- The Bank of England's own distributional analysis of QE (2012) concluded that asset purchases raised household wealth substantially and that the benefits accrued overwhelmingly to holders of financial assets — the top 5% of households held around 40% of the relevant assets (to verify against the 2012 paper) [1].
- Subsequent BoE staff work (Bunn, Pugh & Yeates, 2018) found monetary loosening's effects on *relative* inequality measures were modest, but confirmed large *absolute* gains concentrated at the top of the wealth distribution — the distinction between percentage and pound effects is central and is often used to talk past the problem (to verify) [2].
- Housing is the loop's main transmission line: UK price-to-earnings ratios roughly doubled after the late 1990s ([housing.md](housing.md)), and the deposit requirement rose with prices, so access to ownership increasingly requires a capital transfer — Legal & General's "Bank of Mum and Dad" research finds family assistance involved in roughly half of under-35 purchases [3].
- The loop closes across generations: HMRC data show residential property is the largest asset class in taxable estates, and gifted deposits are themselves largely extracted housing equity — heirs of owners buy houses with the proceeds of house-price inflation, bidding prices further beyond non-heirs' reach [4].
- Each cycle raises the *determinativeness* of inheritance: as the ratio of asset prices to earnings rises, the fraction of lifetime wealth attainable through labour falls mechanically, and the fraction attributable to transfers rises — the dynamic underlying the Resolution Foundation "inheritocracy" finding ([great-wealth-transfer.md](great-wealth-transfer.md)) [5].
- Piketty's *r > g* is the steady-state version of this argument; this chapter is its cyclical, policy-driven complement — the gap between asset returns and wage growth is not only a structural tendency but the intended first-order effect of the modern stabilisation toolkit (see [../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md](../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md)) [6].
- Long-run data confirm the wedge is structural: Jordà, Schularick & Taylor's 145-year, 16-country series puts real returns on housing and equities at roughly 6–8% against real growth near 2% — the loop predates QE and will outlast it [7].
- No serious proposal exists to run monetary policy *without* asset-price effects; the realistic choice is between recycling the windfall at the point of transfer or letting it compound into dynasty. This reframing matters for audiences who accept central-bank orthodoxy but resist "redistribution" (see [../14-communications/audience-messaging.md](../14-communications/audience-messaging.md)).

## Detailed analysis

**The loop, stated precisely.** Six segments, each independently documented, compose the circuit:

| # | Segment | Mechanism | Evidence base |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Rates → asset prices | Discounting: lower real rates raise present values of long-duration claims | Standard finance; BoE QE studies [1][2] |
| 2 | Asset prices → owner windfalls | Valuation gains accrue to existing holders, untaxed until realisation (and often never — death uplift) | [wealth-concentration.md](wealth-concentration.md) |
| 3 | Windfalls → transfers | Housing equity funds gifts and bequests; transfer flow doubled as share of national income | IFS, Resolution Foundation [4][5] |
| 4 | Transfers → heir demand | Gifted deposits determine who can buy; BoMaD ≈ top-ten mortgage lender | L&G, Savills [3] |
| 5 | Heir demand → prices | Deposit-backed buyers set marginal prices in supply-constrained markets | [housing.md](housing.md) |
| 6 | Prices → deeper divide | Ownership entry cost rises relative to wages; loop returns to (2) with a smaller insider class | ONS, RF cohort data [5] |

Each segment is individually uncontroversial; the argument of this chapter is simply that they connect end-to-end, and that the circuit has run through at least three full turns (late-1990s boom, post-2008 QE era, post-2020 stimulus) with the insider/outsider boundary hardening on each pass.

**Three turns of the loop.** The circuit is not hypothetical; it has observable vintages, each ratcheting the insider/outsider boundary:

| Turn | Trigger | Asset effect | Generational residue |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Late 1990s–2007 | Financial liberalisation, credit expansion, falling inflation premia | UK price-to-earnings from ~4× to ~7×; buy-to-let boom | Boomer/Gen-X owners capture the first windfall; first-time-buyer age begins its climb |
| 2. 2009–2021 | QE, near-zero rates | BoE-documented wealth-channel gains; global equity and housing revaluation [1][2] | "Bank of Mum and Dad" becomes structural; ownership among 25–34s hits its trough ([housing.md](housing.md)) |
| 3. 2020–2022 | Pandemic stimulus, rate floor | Fastest house-price growth in decades across the Anglosphere; everything-rally in equities and crypto | Deposit inflation outruns record saving rates; cash-buyer and heir share of purchases rises (to verify) |

The 2022–23 rate reversal partially deflated turn three's equity component but left housing nominal prices broadly intact while *raising* mortgage costs — worsening, not improving, the position of the non-inheriting buyer relative to the cash-rich heir. The loop is robust to the rate cycle because its binding variable is the stock of transferable equity, not the level of rates in any given year.

**The "just build more houses" rejoinder.** Supply reform is genuinely necessary and genuinely insufficient. Necessary: supply constraints amplify segment 5 of the loop, and Hsieh–Moretti-scale misallocation costs are real ([declining-dynamism.md](declining-dynamism.md)). Insufficient: the loop runs through *all* long-duration assets, not just housing — equities, land, and private business stakes inflated on the same discount-rate fall — and no supply response exists for those; moreover, even a heroic building programme adjusts price levels over decades while the transfer wave ([great-wealth-transfer.md](great-wealth-transfer.md)) capitalises the existing price level *now*. Supply reform without transfer taxation locks in the windfall to date; the two are complements, and the manifesto should say so plainly to avoid a false either/or (see [../09-objections/whataboutery.md](../09-objections/whataboutery.md)).

**The monetary-policy asymmetry.** Modern stabilisation policy responds to downturns by supporting asset prices — that is the explicit transmission mechanism of QE and rate cuts. The BoE's 2012 paper is candid that the wealth channel works *because* asset holders feel richer and spend [1]. The asymmetry: losses in downturns are socialised through this support, while gains in recoveries are privatised and, at death, transmitted with minimal taxation (and with capital-gains forgiveness at death in both the UK and US regimes). Over repeated cycles this is a ratchet, not a cycle: the state underwrites the downside of asset ownership without participating in the compounding upside. Jordà, Schularick & Taylor's long-run return data — housing and equity returning 6–8% real against ~1.5–2% growth — show the wedge is persistent across 145 years and 16 countries, not an artefact of the QE decade [7].

**Why inheritance becomes more determinative each cycle.** Define the problem from a wage-earner's seat: the deposit on a median home is a multiple of annual savings capacity, and that multiple rises with each turn of the loop. When the price-to-earnings ratio was 3–4×, labour income could traverse the gap; at 8–9×, it generally cannot, and the binding variable becomes parental equity. This is why the RF and IFS cohort findings ([great-wealth-transfer.md](great-wealth-transfer.md), [mobility.md](mobility.md)) show inheritance overtaking earnings as the determinant of lifetime wealth for post-1980 cohorts: not because the young earn less in absolute terms, but because the asset side of the ledger inflated away from the income side. Asset inflation converts an income distribution into a caste boundary.

**The corollary: transfer taxation as macro-financial policy.** The framing payoff is significant. If untaxed inheritance is the mechanism by which monetary stabilisation compounds into permanent stratification, then a confiscatory transfer tax above a high threshold is properly understood as the *fiscal counterpart of the central bank put* — the damper that recycles policy-generated windfalls back through the population, via the universal endowment ([../02-proposal/endowment.md](../02-proposal/endowment.md)). Three implications:

1. It answers "why tax at death rather than annually?": death is the one point where the accumulated, unrealised, policy-inflated gain is measurable and the owner beyond distortion — and where current law (basis step-up, CGT death uplift) currently *erases* the gain instead of taxing it.
2. It recruits an unexpected constituency: financial-stability economists concerned that wealth concentration raises the savings glut and depresses equilibrium rates further (Mian, Straub & Sufi's "saving glut of the rich"), making the loop self-tightening — redistribution via endowment plausibly *raises* r* and restores monetary headroom [8]. Developed further in [../16-second-order/asset-prices.md](../16-second-order/asset-prices.md) and [../16-second-order/macro.md](../16-second-order/macro.md).
3. It reframes the political attack: opponents cast inheritance tax as envy; this chapter casts it as closing a loophole in the stabilisation state — asset holders have enjoyed a decades-long implicit subsidy, and the tax is the exit price (see [../14-communications/narratives.md](../14-communications/narratives.md)).

**Honesty about magnitudes.** The relative-inequality effects of QE are genuinely debated — Bunn et al. find small Gini effects because leveraged middle-class homeowners also gained [2]. The claim this chapter needs is narrower and more robust: absolute gains concentrated among large asset holders; entry costs for non-owners rose; and the transfer system converts the first fact into permanent advantage over the second group. None of those three legs is seriously contested.

## Verification checklist

- BoE 2012 Quarterly Bulletin: exact top-5% asset-holding share and the paper's own distributional caveats — quote directly.
- Bunn, Pugh & Yeates (2018): headline relative-vs-absolute findings, and whether later BoE work (post-2020 QE round) updates them.
- Jordà et al. return figures for housing and equity vs. growth, from the published QJE version.
- Cash-buyer and gifted-deposit share of purchases in turn three (2020–22) — UK lender data and NAR cash-purchase series.
- Whether comparable Fed distributional analysis of US QE exists (the BoE was unusually candid; a US-source equivalent strengthens the chapter for American readers).
