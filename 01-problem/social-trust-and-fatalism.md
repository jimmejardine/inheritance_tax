# Social Trust and Fatalism

**Executive summary.** Where [meritocracy-legitimacy.md](meritocracy-legitimacy.md) documents the decline in institutional trust, this chapter follows the damage downstream into individual behaviour: when young people conclude that effort cannot bridge the asset gap, they rationally disinvest from the long game — saving less, gambling more, delaying or forgoing family formation — and become available to political fatalism and its exploiters. This is the manifesto's emotional core: dynastic lock-in is experienced not as a statistic but as the quiet, corrosive knowledge that the outcome was decided before you started.

## Key findings

- Belief that effort determines outcomes has weakened in exactly the countries where asset gaps widened: WVS and Pew series show the US and UK drifting from the "hard work brings success" pole toward "luck and connections" since the 1990s (documented in [meritocracy-legitimacy.md](meritocracy-legitimacy.md)); youth-cohort readings are consistently more fatalistic than older cohorts' (to verify per wave) [1][2].
- The fatalism mechanism has experimental and behavioural support: perceived low mobility reduces willingness to invest in education and long-horizon saving. Alesina, Stantcheva & Teso show mobility perceptions causally shift policy preferences; the behavioural-poverty literature (Haushofer & Fehr; Banerjee & Duflo on aspiration failure) shows that when the ladder appears unreachable, effort withdrawal is a rational response to genuinely poor returns, not a character defect [3][4].
- "Why bother" saving arithmetic is real, not perceived: at an 8× house-price-to-earnings ratio ([housing.md](housing.md)), a renter saving 10% of median income cannot reach a deposit before mid-life; the rational alternatives are consumption, emigration, or high-variance bets.
- High-variance substitution is observable: FCA research on new self-directed investors found younger, more diversity-representative cohorts entering via apps and crypto, with a substantial minority citing thrill/social factors and — critically — the belief that conventional saving cannot deliver ownership; survey work repeatedly finds "only realistic route to wealth" reasoning among young crypto and meme-stock traders (treat quantitative claims cautiously; the evidence is survey-grade) [5][6].
- The lottery has long shown the same gradient — heaviest play among those with least conventional mobility prospect — and gambling-participation growth among UK young men is a regulatory concern per Gambling Commission statistics (to verify current figures) [7].
- Fertility connects through housing and insecurity: US evidence (Dettling & Kearney) finds house-price rises reduce births among non-owners while raising them among owners — an asset-gradient in family formation; UK and Korean survey data consistently list housing costs among top reasons for delayed childbearing. These are correlational-to-quasi-experimental findings; the honest claim is that wealth insecurity is *a* documented drag on fertility, not the sole cause of low birth rates [8][9].
- Populism correlates with blocked mobility: Protzer & Summers argue populist vote share tracks *unfair* inequality (low social mobility) rather than inequality per se; Rodrik and Inglehart–Norris document the economic-insecurity amplifier. The chain from dynastic wealth to populist rupture is suggestive rather than proven, but every intermediate link is separately evidenced [10][11].
- Case & Deaton's "deaths of despair" — rising mid-life mortality from suicide, alcohol, and opioids among non-college white Americans — is the extreme end of the same distribution: cumulative disadvantage plus lost narrative of progress [12].

## Detailed analysis

**From perception to behaviour.** The step this chapter adds to the legitimacy story is behavioural: beliefs about the returns to effort are inputs to effort. The aspiration-failure literature (Appadurai; Ray; empirically Banerjee–Duflo and Genicot–Ray) formalises the intuition: aspirations motivate investment only when the gap between current position and aspiration appears traversable; beyond a threshold, wider gaps *reduce* investment [4]. Asset-price decoupling ([asset-price-feedback.md](asset-price-feedback.md)) is precisely a machine for widening that gap beyond traversability for the non-inheriting. The prediction — effort withdrawal concentrated among the asset-less young — matches the observed pattern of falling youth saving into conventional vehicles alongside rising high-variance speculation.

**High-variance bets as rational mobility strategy.** The speculation evidence deserves careful handling because it is easy to moralise and easy to overclaim. What is defensible:

| Behaviour | Evidence | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| Lottery play skews to low-mobility groups | Long-standing public-finance literature on lottery regressivity | Strong, old [7] |
| Young cohorts over-index in crypto/meme-stock trading | FCA consumer research; broker demographics | Strong on participation, weaker on motive [5][6] |
| Stated motive: conventional routes blocked | FCA/survey self-reports; qualitative studies | Survey-grade; directionally consistent [5] |
| Sports-betting growth among young men | Regulator statistics US/UK post-liberalisation | Strong on growth; link to fatalism inferential [7] |

The correct framing is expected-utility logic, not pathology: when the deterministic route to ownership is closed, a lottery-shaped portfolio is the only one whose upside tail includes a house. Young people did not become irrational; the payoff matrix changed. This framing matters enormously for communications — it treats the audience as reasoners rather than victims (see [../14-communications/audience-messaging.md](../14-communications/audience-messaging.md)).

**Family formation.** Dettling & Kearney's identification — comparing owners and renters facing the same local price shock — is the cleanest evidence that housing wealth position causally shapes fertility timing [8]. Cross-national correlations (lowest fertility in the highest housing-cost, highest-insecurity East Asian economies) are consistent but confounded by work culture and gender norms; the manifesto should claim the housing-insecurity channel specifically and resist the temptation to attribute national birth rates wholesale to wealth concentration [9]. Framed correctly this is one of the proposal's broadest-reach arguments: the endowment at 25 ([../02-proposal/endowment.md](../02-proposal/endowment.md)) arrives at the median age of desired-but-delayed family formation, and pro-natal framing reaches audiences redistribution framing never will (see [../06-palatability/framing.md](../06-palatability/framing.md) and [../12-political-adoption/right-parties.md](../12-political-adoption/right-parties.md)).

**Generalised trust and the inequality gradient.** Beyond institutional trust, *interpersonal* trust — the "most people can be trusted" survey item — correlates negatively with inequality across countries and US states (Wilkinson & Pickett's synthesis; Uslaner's work arguing the causal arrow runs from inequality to distrust via perceived unfairness) [13]. The correlation is robust; causal direction is debated, and the manifesto should present it as a gradient, not a law. What matters here is the composition effect: a society sorting into hereditary insiders and outsiders gives both groups reasons for distrust — outsiders because the game is rigged against them, insiders because their position invites resentment they can sense. Gated communities, private schooling, and security spending are the insider expression of the same trust collapse; the fatalism documented below is the outsider expression. Both are deadweight losses no income statistic captures.

**The civic endgame.** Fatalism scales from private to political. Protzer & Summers' cross-country result — populism tracks low mobility, not inequality per se — is the key bridging finding: people tolerate unequal outcomes they believe were fairly generated and revolt against smaller inequalities they believe were rigged [10]. A society whose young hold, accurately, that ownership is hereditary satisfies the rigged condition *by construction*. The political expression is unpredictable in direction — abstention, exit, populist right, populist left — but reliably anti-system; [elite-overproduction.md](elite-overproduction.md) describes who organises that energy. The stakes claim of this section is therefore conservative in the literal sense: transfer taxation plus universal endowment is a repair to the belief system on which market democracy runs — the belief that the game is worth playing. Restoring that belief is also the movement's core recruitment message ([../13-movement/recruitment.md](../13-movement/recruitment.md), [../14-communications/narratives.md](../14-communications/narratives.md)).

**The behavioural ledger.** Summarising the downstream behaviours and the confidence warranted for each:

| Behaviour | Direction | Evidence quality | Inheritance link |
|---|---|---|---|
| Long-horizon saving among asset-less young | Falling relative to need | Strong on outcomes; motive inferred | Deposit arithmetic makes saving objectively futile at current ratios |
| High-variance speculation (crypto, meme stocks, betting) | Rising in young cohorts | Strong on participation; survey-grade on motive [5][6][7] | Rational response to closed deterministic routes |
| Family formation | Delayed/foregone along asset gradient | Quasi-experimental for housing channel [8] | Direct: tenure predicts fertility response to price shocks |
| Education investment | Bifurcating — arms race at top, disengagement below | Moderate; see [elite-overproduction.md](elite-overproduction.md) | Family capital buys queue position |
| Anti-system voting / abstention | Rising with blocked mobility | Correlational, cross-country [10][11] | Suggestive; mechanism plausible |
| Deaths of despair | Rising in left-behind strata | Strong descriptive [12] | Indirect; cumulative-disadvantage channel |

**What it feels like.** The manifesto should let the phenomenology carry weight the statistics cannot: the graduate couple doing everything right who compute that the deposit grows faster than their savings; the sibling comparison where the inheritance, not the career, sorted the family; the quiet shift from "when we buy" to "if we inherit." None of this is hypothetical — it is the modal experience of the post-1980 renter cohort documented across this section — and it is why the proposal's beneficiaries, unlike its opponents, currently experience the system as weather rather than as policy. Naming it as policy is the first communicative task.

**Uncertainty flags.** Motive evidence for speculation is self-report; fertility channels are partially confounded; populism links are correlational with contested mechanisms; and trust declines have multiple causes (media fragmentation, scandal, secularisation) of which wealth rigidity is one strand. The chapter's argument survives these caveats because it needs only the weak form: perceived and actual blocked mobility change behaviour in documented, socially corrosive directions, and inheritance concentration is a first-order cause of the blockage.
