# Thresholds and Indexation

**Executive summary.** A £500,000 / $750,000 per-recipient lifetime threshold, indexed annually to the greater of national wage growth or median house-price growth, is calibrated so that the overwhelming majority of heirs transmit and receive unaffected, while concentrated dynastic transfers face the 100% marginal rate. This document grounds the number in house-price and wage multiples, shows the distribution of inheritances received, estimates revenue, and defends the indexation rule against the nominal-erosion failure mode seen in abolished Nordic regimes.

## Key findings

- UK median house price is approximately £290,000 (ONS HPI, 2024), so a £500k threshold is ~1.7× median housing wealth — enough to fully absorb a typical inherited home in almost every region outside inner London [1].
- £500,000 equals ~14× UK median gross annual earnings of ~£35,000 (ONS ASHE 2023) [2]; $750,000 equals ~13× US median earnings of ~$59,000 (BLS) [3].
- Inheritance receipt is rare and typically small: only 4% of GB adults received an inheritance of £1,000+ over 2014–16, with a median receipt of £11,000 (ONS, 2018) [9]; among recipients, half got £10,000 or less and only 10% got over £100,000 (Resolution Foundation, *Passing on*, 2018) [4].
- On IFS lifetime projections for the 1980s-born (the highest-inheriting cohort yet), the median lifetime inheritance is ~£136,000, the top 25% exceed ~£284,000, and only the top 10% exceed ~£495,000 — for the 1960s-born the top-10% cut-off is ~£269,000 [5]. A £500k per-recipient threshold therefore sits at roughly the 90th percentile of projected receipts even for the 1980s cohort.
- Aggregate annual inheritance flow in the UK is projected to roughly double from ~£100bn to ~£200bn by the 2040s (IFS) [5]; the US SCF transfer module shows a similarly top-heavy distribution [6].
- CPI-only indexation would erode the threshold because UK house prices have outpaced CPI by ~1.5–2 percentage points annually over four decades (ONS) [1] — the same drift that neutered Sweden's and Norway's nominal thresholds before abolition (see `../05-history/abolitions.md`).
- A £500k threshold rewards distributive estate planning: four children inherit £2m tax-free; two inherit £1m tax-free.

## 1. Why a per-recipient threshold at all

A threshold (as opposed to a flat 100% rate on the first pound) exists because the policy target is dynastic wealth concentration, not ordinary family transmission of a home, savings, or a modest business share. A *recipient-based* threshold (vs. the UK's current estate-based nil-rate band) aligns the tax with the principle that the morally relevant quantity is what an individual receives unearned, not what a deceased person leaves. It also mechanically creates the incentive central to the design: break up large estates across more beneficiaries to avoid the 100% rate. An estate-based exemption produces no such incentive and privileges single-heir dynasties.

## 2. Why £500,000 / $750,000

Three anchors converge on this range:

**(a) Median house price.** UK median residential property: ~£290,000 (ONS HPI, 2024) [1]. A £500k threshold absorbs a typical home plus typical pension/savings wealth in one transfer. US median home sale price: ~$420,000 (NAR) [7]; $750,000 gives equivalent headroom given higher US housing dispersion.

**(b) Wage multiples.** £500,000 ≈ 14× UK median full-time earnings (ONS ASHE) [2]; $750,000 ≈ 13× US median earnings (BLS) [3]. Both are well above typical lifetime savings capacity for a median household, so the threshold cannot credibly be characterised as targeting the middle class.

**(c) Recipient distribution.** IFS projections put the 90th percentile of lifetime inheritance receipts for the 1980s-born at roughly £495,000 (median ~£136,000; 75th percentile ~£284,000), and lower for earlier cohorts (1960s-born 90th percentile: ~£269,000) [5]. Actual receipts to date are far smaller still: the median inheritance among 2014–16 recipients was £11,000 (ONS) [9]. A £500k lifetime threshold therefore leaves roughly 90% of even the highest-inheriting projected cohort entirely untaxed — and materially more of every earlier cohort — before any behavioural redistribution.

**Tradeoff variants.**

- *£250k (tighter):* captures roughly the top quarter of projected 1980s-born lifetime receipts (75th percentile ≈ £284,000) [5]; politically riskier because it bites into ordinary inherited homes in the South East.
- *£1m (looser):* sits well above the projected 90th percentile of receipts (~£495,000 for the 1980s-born [5]); the precise share of recipients above £1m is unpublished but plausibly only the top few per cent (unverified). Revenue and dynastic-break effect substantially weaker; acceptable only if paired with a larger citizen's endowment.

## 3. How many people would pay

**UK.** Only 4% of GB adults received an inheritance of £1,000+ in the two years to mid-2016, and the median receipt was £11,000 [9]; even on IFS lifetime projections for the 1980s-born, a fifth will inherit less than £10,000 [5]. IFS projections imply around 10% of the 1980s-born would cross £500k in lifetime cumulative receipts (top decile cut-off ~£495,000), with smaller shares for earlier cohorts [5]; precise figures *require modelling* on a cumulative lifetime basis rather than single-event basis.

**US.** The Federal Reserve SCF transfer module and CBO analysis show ~20–25% of households receive any transfer, with the top 1% of heirs receiving more than 40% of the aggregate [6][8]. Roughly the top 3–5% of recipients would exceed $750k cumulatively — again, exact figure *requires modelling*.

"Partially affected" (cross the threshold at all) therefore plausibly sits in the low-single-digit percent of recipients; "materially affected" (receive multiples of the threshold) is a fraction of that.

## 4. Revenue implications

UK aggregate annual inheritance flow: ~£100bn currently, projected to ~£200bn by 2040s (IFS) [5]. If ~30–40% of aggregate flow sits above £500k per recipient (IFS distributional work [5]), the notional pre-behavioural base is £30–80bn/year.

Actual revenue will be materially lower because: (i) business/farm deferral over 30 years smooths large receipts out of a single tax year; (ii) spousal and charitable exemption removes a substantial slice; (iii) behavioural response (lifetime giving, distribution across more recipients, charitable redirection) is exactly the intended outcome and will shrink the base. A plausible steady-state revenue figure is in the £10–25bn/year range — *requires HMRC/OBR micro-simulation* to refine. US equivalent using CBO/JCT methodology scales proportionally [8].

The policy's success metric is not revenue; it is the re-dispersion of dynastic wealth.

## 5. Indexation: max(wage growth, median house-price growth)

CPI indexation is inadequate because the goods the threshold must "cover" — housing wealth, pension pots, business equity — do not track consumer prices. UK house prices have outpaced CPI by ~1.5–2 pp/year over four decades (ONS HPI) [1]. Wage growth is the better floor because the threshold's legitimacy rests on its relation to lifetime earning capacity. Taking the maximum of the two prevents either a housing boom or a wage boom from silently tightening the policy.

This is precisely the failure mode documented in `../05-history/abolitions.md`: Swedish and Norwegian inheritance taxes lost political legitimacy partly because nominally-fixed thresholds captured ordinary middle-class homes as property prices rose, generating the coalition that ultimately abolished them.

## 7. Distributional framing

The per-recipient design has a deliberate incentive. Under a £500k threshold:

- An estate split equally between **4 children** transmits **£2,000,000** tax-free.
- An estate split between **2 children** transmits **£1,000,000** tax-free.
- An estate left to a **single heir** transmits **£500,000** tax-free; the remainder faces 100%.

Combined with the unconditional £50,000 citizen's endowment at 25, the policy ensures everyone starts with a floor while removing the ceiling-less transmission of dynastic fortunes. Wide distribution is rewarded; concentration is taxed away.
