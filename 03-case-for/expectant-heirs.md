# The Expectant Heir: Harms That Arrive Before the Money

**Executive summary.** The harms of dynastic inheritance do not begin at receipt — they begin at the *forecast*. Decades before any death, the credible expectation of a fortune distorts the heir's career, mortgages their autonomy to the donor's approval, suspends succession inside family firms, and — at the pathological margin — curdles into "inheritance impatience" and elder financial abuse. [heirs-burden.md](heirs-burden.md) makes the case that received fortunes harm their recipients; this file makes the companion case that *expected* fortunes harm them earlier, and adds the design point: because the tax binds at announcement, its liberation of expectant heirs arrives immediately, a generation before its revenue does.

## Findings

- The empirical anticipation literature is thin and this file says so throughout: the best panel evidence finds behavioural effects strongest for *unexpected* inheritances [1], so the case here rests on mechanism, clinical observation, family-firm data, and the abuse record — flagged accordingly.
- The expectation is a lever. The strategic-bequest literature models bequests as payment for the heir's compliance and attention — Bernheim, Shleifer & Summers found testators with more than one child use the threat of disinheritance to extract attention [2]; Japanese data show heirs expecting bequests provide measurably more parental care, an exchange motive stronger in Japan than the US [3] ([`../16-second-order/family-and-culture.md`](../16-second-order/family-and-culture.md) §1).
- The family-firm heir waits, visibly: 39% of next-generation members in PwC's Global NextGen Survey 2024 report resistance within the business to embracing leadership change [4]; when the wait ends, the average heir-run outcome is poor (operating profitability falls ≥4pp around family-CEO successions [5]).
- Waiting curdles. Australia's Law Reform Commission documented "inheritance impatience" — adult children treating parental assets as already theirs — as a driver of elder financial abuse [6], now amplified by the scale of the pending wealth transfer [7]; in England & Wales, probate caveats hit records in 2024–25 (11,300+ per year, up 79% in fifteen years) and High Court inheritance disputes reached an all-time high of 1,217 in 2025 [8].
- The cultural record is centuries deep and unusually unanimous: from "waiting for dead men's shoes" to *Great Expectations* — a novel about a fortune that corrupts its beneficiary entirely in prospect — the expectant heir is one of literature's stock tragedies (§5).
- The design implication is distinctive: a legislated cap changes expectations *at announcement*. A 30-year-old who learns the backstop tops out at £500k re-plans their career, their independence, and their relationship with their parents now — the benign announcement effect ([`../16-second-order/heirs-and-work.md`](../16-second-order/heirs-and-work.md) §3).

## 1. The waiting room: what the forecast does to a life

The psychological harms catalogued for recipients in [heirs-burden.md](heirs-burden.md) — purpose deficit, competence never provable, relationships shadowed by instrumental suspicion — do not politely wait for probate. Self-determination theory's three conditions for flourishing (autonomy, competence, relatedness [9]) are pressured by the *prospect* as much as the fact:

- **Autonomy** is compromised by the donor's living veto: the expectant heir's large choices — career, spouse, location, politics — are made under the shadow of a revocable will (§2).
- **Competence** is unprovable in advance: every achievement of the heir-apparent is discounted, by others and by themselves, against the fortune everyone knows is coming. The trust-fund child's question — "would I have got this on my own?" — is asked from adolescence, not from receipt.
- **Relatedness** is contaminated early: the heir-apparent learns young which friendships and courtships arrive pre-interested.

The honest caveat: unlike the post-receipt record, which has decades of clinical and journalistic documentation, anticipation-specific clinical literature is sparse — the "sudden money" practice by definition studies arrivals [10]. The mechanism claims above are theory plus practitioner observation, not measured effects, and the manifesto should present them as such.

## 2. The backstop problem: careers chosen in the fortune's shadow

An expected seven-figure backstop rationally lowers the cost of not trying. The aristocratic pattern — sons who never entered a profession because the estate would provide — is the historical exhibit; the modern version is subtler: safer careers, shorter horizons, less human-capital investment, geographic anchoring to the family asset rather than the labour market ([`../16-second-order/heirs-and-work.md`](../16-second-order/heirs-and-work.md) §3).

The evidence here must be handled honestly, and cuts both ways:

- Post-receipt effects are well-identified and large — the Carnegie-conjecture and lottery literatures ([`../16-second-order/heirs-and-work.md`](../16-second-order/heirs-and-work.md) §1).
- The direct anticipation test is weaker: US Health and Retirement Study panel data show inheritance *receipt* raises early retirement by 4.4 percentage points, with the effect strongest for *unexpected* inheritances [1] — implying expectations were already partly priced into behaviour, but not directly measuring the mid-career effort margin.
- There is also a genuine counter-mechanism: an expected inheritance is implicit insurance, and insurance can *enable* risk-taking — the same logic by which the universal endowment is defended in [equal-start.md](equal-start.md). The difference the design exploits is timing and universality: the endowment delivers its insurance at 25 to everyone, while the dynastic expectation delivers it to a few, in late middle age (UK inheritance receipt peaks at 55–64 [11]), after the career-formation window it distorts.

Carnegie made the anticipation claim explicitly — the parent who leaves enormous wealth "generally deadens the talents and energies of the son, and tempts him to lead a less useful and less worthy life than he otherwise would" — a corruption operating through upbringing and expectation, not through the cheque ([`../05-history/carnegie-1889.md`](../05-history/carnegie-1889.md); the line is from *The Advantages of Poverty*, 1891).

## 3. The leash: expectation as parental power

The strategic-bequest literature formalises what every family knows: a revocable promise of wealth is an instrument of control. Bernheim, Shleifer and Summers' classic model and evidence show testators using potential disinheritance to purchase attention and compliance from adult children — with the effect concentrated where there are multiple children to play against each other [2]. Japanese microdata find children who expect bequests provide significantly more care and contact, an exchange motive stronger in Japan than in the US [3]. The deep treatment of what this does to family culture — including the argument that below-threshold families, with nothing dynastic to leverage, may end up with *cleaner* intergenerational relationships — is [`../16-second-order/family-and-culture.md`](../16-second-order/family-and-culture.md).

For this file the point is the heir's side of the ledger: the expectant heir of a large estate spends their entire adult life on a leash whose length is set by someone else's approval. That is a liberty harm in exactly the register the proposal's liberal critics care about ([`../04-case-against/liberty-autonomy.md`](../04-case-against/liberty-autonomy.md) argues the donor's side; this is the corrective).

## 4. The prince-in-waiting: family firms

Family businesses institutionalise the expectation. The heir-apparent's career is decided in adolescence; the incumbent's reluctance to retire suspends them in deputy roles through their prime — 39% of next-gens report resistance within the firm to leadership change, and readiness of the incumbent generation to retire recurs as a named succession difficulty in the same survey series [4]. The wait is not benign for the firm either: the succession-outcome literature ([`../04-case-against/family-business.md`](../04-case-against/family-business.md)) finds heir-managed firms underperform on average [5] — the waiting room selects for patience and loyalty, not managerial talent, and forecloses the outside career in which the heir could have discovered what they were actually good at.

The proposal's carve-out machinery ([`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md)) keeps genuine operating businesses transmissible in economic terms; what it removes is the *automatic* dynastic assumption — heirs above the threshold hold their stake alongside a state preferred interest or deferral obligation, making "run it because you're the eldest" a choice with an opportunity cost rather than a default.

## 5. When waiting curdles: impatience, disputes, and abuse

The darkest corner of the anticipation channel is documented, current, and growing:

- **Inheritance impatience.** The Australian Law Reform Commission's *Elder Abuse* report (2017) identified the pattern of adult children treating parental assets as presumptively theirs — pressuring gifts, interfering in asset management, extracting "early inheritances" [6]. Australian practitioners and reporting describe it as a leading driver of elder financial abuse, sharpened by the A$5+ trillion transfer now pending [7].
- **Probate conflict at record levels.** In England & Wales, caveats blocking probate exceeded 11,300 in both 2024 and 2025 (from ~6,400 in 2010 — 79% growth in fifteen years), and contested probate claims at the High Court hit an all-time record of 1,217 in 2025 (law-firm-compiled Ministry of Justice data) [8]. Rising stakes — frozen thresholds, property values — and blended families are the drivers practitioners name.
- The proposal does not abolish this pathology — sub-threshold impatience survives, and the £50k endowment could itself attract predation, a risk [`../02-proposal/endowment-delivery.md`](../02-proposal/endowment-delivery.md) must design against. What it removes is the *scale* of the prize: above £500k per recipient there is nothing to extract, contest, or pressure a parent over, because there is nothing to receive. Caveat wars over eight-figure estates have no object.

## 6. The oldest testimony: history and literature

Long before panel data, the expectant heir was a stock figure of moral commentary — and the verdict has been remarkably stable across centuries and ideologies. "Waiting for dead men's shoes" is proverbial in English by the sixteenth century. The eighteenth- and nineteenth-century novel is a sustained study of the type: *Great Expectations* is precisely a story of a fortune that corrupts its beneficiary — snobbery, idleness, abandonment of loyalties — entirely in *prospect*, before and without receipt; Austen's plots turn on entailed expectations warping courtship and character; Trollope's parliamentary novels run on heirs waiting; Mann's *Buddenbrooks* gave its name to the sociology of dynastic decay. Adam Smith's attack on entails ([`../00-capitalism/capitalist-supporters.md`](../00-capitalism/capitalist-supporters.md)) is the same observation in legal-economic register: arranging the present around the presumed claims of unborn or unproven successors is "the most absurd of all suppositions."

Cultural evidence is not causal evidence, and the manifesto should deploy it as testimony, not proof. But testimony this old, this cross-cultural, and this politically unaligned is worth something: nobody wrote the great novel in which waiting for a fortune improved anyone.

## 7. What the tax changes, and when

The distinctive design point — developed from [`../16-second-order/heirs-and-work.md`](../16-second-order/heirs-and-work.md) §3 — is that expectation harms respond to *legislation*, not to deaths:

| Harm channel | When relief arrives |
|---|---|
| Career distortion under the backstop | At announcement: the ceiling is known, the career must carry the life |
| The parental leash above the threshold | At announcement: a revocable promise capped at £500k is family support, not a life-controlling instrument |
| Family-firm succession-by-default | At first post-commencement transmissions, anticipated from announcement |
| Impatience, caveat wars, extraction pressure | Progressively, as the above-threshold prize disappears |

Revenue waits for mortality ([`../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md`](../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md)); the expectant heir's liberation does not. The transition literature treats announcement effects as an avoidance risk to be managed ([`../15-transition/announcement-effects.md`](../15-transition/announcement-effects.md)); this is the same phenomenon read from the other side of the ledger — and it belongs in the manifesto's offer to the young alongside the endowment: *your start will be your own, and so will your life.*

## Honest limits

1. **Identification is weak.** No natural experiment randomises expectations; the strongest direct test cuts partly against the mid-career claim [1]. This file's confidence ordering: abuse/dispute record (documented) > strategic-bequest mechanism (modelled and evidenced) > family-firm waiting (surveyed) > psychological development claims (clinical/theoretical) > career-distortion magnitudes (inferred).
2. **The insurance counter-mechanism is real** (§2) and unquantified in both directions.
3. **Most expectant heirs expect modest sums** and are untouched — as everywhere in this project, the claims are about the dynastic tail, where the money, the leverage, and the distortion concentrate.
