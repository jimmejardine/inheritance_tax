# The Civic Case: Restoring Meritocratic Legitimacy

**Executive summary.** Capitalism's moral authority rests on the claim that reward tracks contribution, yet dynastic wealth transmission severs this link and hollows out the system's own self-justification. A 100% inheritance tax above a per-recipient threshold is therefore not an attack on meritocratic capitalism but the means of making its foundational promise empirically true — a conservative repair dressed as a radical measure.

(The *diagnostic* of declining legitimacy sits in [`../01-problem/meritocracy-legitimacy.md`](../01-problem/meritocracy-legitimacy.md). This file is the normative/prescriptive counterpart.)

## Key findings

- Capitalism's claim to legitimacy is desert-based; inherited wealth is the single largest structural violation of that claim, and taxing it aligns the system with its own stated values [1][3].
- Andrew Carnegie — a self-made industrialist, not a socialist — argued in 1889 that "the man who dies thus rich dies disgraced" and defended heavy estate duties as a civic good [1].
- Markovits and Sandel diagnose meritocracy as rigged and contemptuous; their prescription is *more* actual meritocracy, not less, which requires dismantling the inherited head-start [2][3].
- Wooldridge mounts the same case from the right: genuine talent-based mobility is incompatible with dynastic capture [4].
- Democratic stability depends on perceived fairness; when voters conclude the game is fixed, they defect to populist-authoritarian alternatives (Inglehart & Norris; Rodrik) [6][7].
- The top 10% own 76% of global wealth and the top 1% captured 38% of all wealth growth since 1995 — dynasty, not effort, now drives the distribution [8].
- A high inheritance tax plausibly *raises* dynamism by shifting capital composition from passive heir-stock to active founder-stock — Carnegie's own argument [1].
- The "self-made" national narrative central to US and UK political identity is falsified by heritable wealth; restoring it is the conservative position, properly understood.

## 1. Capitalism's self-justification problem

The moral case for market capitalism has never been that it maximises output — planners could in principle do that — but that rewards track productive contribution. Hayek, Friedman, and the entire neoclassical tradition concede that the system earns its legitimacy through this link. Inherited wealth is the cleanest possible violation: a recipient who contributed nothing receives a windfall that in many cases exceeds what a productive lifetime of labour can earn. Piketty's empirical finding that *r > g* makes this structural rather than incidental — absent correction, inheritance necessarily dominates earnings as the source of wealth over time [5]. An inheritance tax is therefore not hostile to capitalism's moral architecture; it is the load-bearing wall that architecture requires.

## 2. Carnegie's founder-visibility argument

Andrew Carnegie's *Gospel of Wealth* (1889) is the locus classicus and deserves to be read against its author: a Scottish immigrant who built US Steel and then demanded the state take most of his peers' estates. His reasoning is explicitly civic. A society visibly populated by *founders* of fortunes is legitimate in a way that a society dominated by *heirs* cannot be. "The man who dies thus rich dies disgraced" is not moralism but political sociology [1]. Carnegie further argued that heavy estate duties would force the living rich to deploy capital productively or give it away, and would spare heirs the "drone" status he considered corrosive to both character and nation. His position — a self-made industrialist arguing for near-confiscatory estate taxation — is historically remarkable and rhetorically indispensable here.

## 3. Markovits, Sandel, Wooldridge: diagnosis flipped to prescription

Daniel Markovits's *Meritocracy Trap* (2019) documents that elite status in the US is now largely transmitted as inherited human capital — private schooling, tutoring, networks, unpaid internships — dressed up as individual achievement [2]. Michael Sandel's *Tyranny of Merit* (2020) argues that meritocratic rhetoric has generated contempt among winners and humiliation among losers, corroding solidarity [3]. Adrian Wooldridge's *Aristocracy of Talent* (2021) makes the parallel case from the right: genuine talent-sorting is a civilisational achievement that dynastic wealth actively undermines [4]. All three converge: the answer is not to abandon the meritocratic ideal but to make it real, which is impossible while inheritance compounds across generations.

## 4. Democratic stability

Inglehart and Norris's *Cultural Backlash* (2019) shows that support for populist-authoritarian parties rises where voters conclude the economic system is rigged against them [6]. Dani Rodrik's work on the globalisation backlash reaches the same finding through a different route: unaddressed distributional unfairness turns into institutional rejection [7]. A visible, dynasty-disrupting tax is therefore *stability-enhancing* — it is the conservative case for radical redistribution. The genuine radicals are those who would preserve dynastic wealth at the cost of democratic legitimacy itself.

## 5. Entrepreneurial dynamism

The standard right-wing objection — that inheritance taxes kill enterprise — inverts the empirical relationship. Heir-capital is disproportionately passive: it sits in index funds, land, and rent-seeking family offices. Founder-capital is active: it starts firms, hires, and takes risks. A high inheritance tax shifts the composition of the capital stock toward the latter by forcing each generation to re-earn its position. It also opens competitive space: markets dominated by dynastic incumbents crowd out new entrants, and Carnegie himself argued that estate taxation was pro-dynamism for precisely this reason [1]. The *World Inequality Report 2022* shows the current trajectory is the opposite of dynamism — concentrated, heritable, and stagnant [8].

## 6. The founding national narrative

The United States and United Kingdom both tell themselves a story of self-made success — the log cabin, the barrow-boy, the garage start-up. Dynasty falsifies that story in practice while leaving its rhetoric intact, producing the worst of both worlds: a society that believes itself meritocratic and behaves hereditarily. Removing dynasty restores coherence. Many voters who identify as "conservative" would, on reflection, be defending the genuinely conservative case — bourgeois virtue, earned reward, generational renewal — by supporting this proposal. Opposing it means defending aristocracy, which is what "conservative" used to mean only before Burke's heirs made peace with commerce.
