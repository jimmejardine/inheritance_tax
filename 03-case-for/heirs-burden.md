# Liberating for the Heirs

**Executive summary.** The case for a 100% inheritance tax above a generous per-recipient threshold is usually cast as a redistribution from heirs to non-heirs, but a long tradition — from Andrew Carnegie to contemporary clinicians and heirs themselves — argues that large unearned fortunes actively harm their recipients. Capping inheritance at £500k per lifetime therefore reframes the policy as positive-sum: heirs are released from the curse of dynastic wealth, and the wider cohort of citizens gains a real start through the universal endowment [1][5].

## Findings

- Andrew Carnegie, the archetypal self-made industrialist, condemned the transmission of great fortune in explicitly moral terms: "the parent who leaves his son enormous wealth generally deadens the talents and energies of the son, and tempts him to lead a less useful and less worthy life than he otherwise would" [1].
- Clinical and journalistic work on heirs documents identity diffusion, guilt, and "affluenza"-type symptoms; this literature is largely qualitative and should not be read as settled empirical science [2][3][4].
- Self-determination theory predicts that unearned mega-wealth undermines autonomy, competence, and relatedness — the three conditions Deci and Ryan identify as necessary for intrinsic motivation and sustained well-being [6].
- Empirical labour-economics work (Holtz-Eakin, Joulfaian & Rosen 1993) shows that large inheritances sharply reduce labour-force participation, which the authors frame as direct evidence *for* Carnegie's psychological claim [7].
- Heir-led organisations — Resource Generation, Patriotic Millionaires, Abigail Disney's public advocacy, Chuck Collins' "Wealth for the Common Good" — treat redistribution of inherited fortunes as a matter of personal liberation, not altruistic sacrifice [3][5][8].
- The proposed design preserves ordinary family transmission below the threshold; the "liberation" claim applies only to the portion of inheritance that crosses from generous provision into dynastic entrenchment [9].

## 1. Carnegie's curse of great wealth

Carnegie's 1889 *Gospel of Wealth* is the founding text of the "heir-harm" argument. He wrote that "by far the most fortunate heirs" are those whose parents leave them only a modest competence, and warned against "the thoughtless donor" of inherited millions. His prescription — that a man who dies rich "dies disgraced" — rests not on egalitarian ethics but on a conviction that inherited fortune "deadens the talents and energies" of the recipient [1]. This is a conservative, character-based argument; it belongs squarely inside the case for the policy, not outside it.

## 2. The clinical and journalistic record

Robert Frank's *Richistan* interviews, Paul Sullivan's "Wealth Matters" columns, Dennis Pearne's clinical practice, Susan Bradley's "sudden money" research, and Williams & Preisser's *Preparing Heirs* converge on a consistent set of themes: loss of purpose, arrested development, relational suspicion ("do they love me or my money?"), and paralysing guilt [2][4][10]. Chuck Collins' *Born on Third Base* documents his own decision, as an Oscar Mayer heir, to give away his inheritance, and describes the relief that followed [3]. This literature is clinical and anecdotal rather than randomised or representative — we should not pretend it is more than that. It is nonetheless a striking pattern, reported by observers with no shared political agenda.

## 3. Agency, competence, and eudaimonia

Self-determination theory holds that human flourishing depends on autonomy (acting from one's own values), competence (demonstrable mastery), and relatedness (authentic connection) [6]. Large unearned wealth pressures all three: the heir has not chosen their position; they cannot prove competence in any market that would have admitted them without the fortune; and their relationships are shadowed by suspicion of instrumental motive. Aristotle's *eudaimonia* — flourishing through the exercise of earned virtue — points the same way: a good life is *achieved*, not inherited.

## 4. Labour-force evidence

Holtz-Eakin, Joulfaian and Rosen (1993), in the paper they pointedly titled "The Carnegie Conjecture," found that a single person receiving an inheritance of around $150,000 was roughly four times as likely to leave the labour force as someone receiving under $25,000 [7]. Edward Wolff's later work on inheritance replicates the pattern. We read this not as an argument that heirs are lazy, but as quantitative confirmation of what Carnegie and the clinicians describe: large transfers dissolve the structuring pressure of productive work.

## 5. Universalisation, not confiscation

The design does not abolish inheritance. Below £500k lifetime per recipient, transmission proceeds untouched: the family home, the modest business, the education fund, the cushion against misfortune — all preserved [9]. Only the portion that would tip a recipient into dynastic territory is redirected, and it is redirected in part to a universal citizen's endowment that gives *every* young adult the kind of launch that is currently the preserve of the lucky few. The heir loses a curse; the cohort gains a start.

## 6. Heirs' own voices

The most persuasive witnesses are heirs themselves. Resource Generation organises young people with inherited wealth around redistribution; Patriotic Millionaires lobbies openly for higher taxes on their own class; Abigail Disney has written and spoken repeatedly about the psychic damage of the Disney fortune; Chuck Collins has made the auto-biographical case in book length [3][5][8]. Their testimony is not decisive on its own, but it breaks the assumption that this policy is something done *to* heirs rather than *for* some of them.

## 7. A virtue-ethics framing

The manifesto's central move, and this section's contribution, is to reframe inheritance tax from zero-sum redistribution to positive-sum liberation. The non-heir gains a fair start; the heir gains the conditions of a flourishing life — work that matters, relationships untainted by suspicion, a self built rather than inherited. On this reading, a 100% tax above the threshold is not a punishment of the fortunate. It is, in Carnegie's own idiom, the removal of a curse.
