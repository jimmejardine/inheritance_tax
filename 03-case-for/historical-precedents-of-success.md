# The Empirical Case: Precedents of Success

**Executive summary.** "It has never been tried" is false in the only way that matters: every component of the proposal — forced dynastic breaks, near-confiscatory transfer taxation, and mass capital grants — has been tried at scale, and in each case the feared catastrophe did not occur while the intended structural effect substantially did. This file assembles five precedent families, states honestly what each proves and what confounds prevent it from proving, and concludes that the burden of historical proof now sits with the proposal's opponents.

(The detailed histories live in [`../05-history/`](../05-history/README.md); this file extracts their *evidentiary force* for the case. Methodological honesty is load-bearing throughout: these are natural experiments with confounds, not randomised trials, and the file says so at every step.)

## Key findings

- The American abolition of primogeniture and entail (1776–c.1800) deliberately dismantled hereditary wealth mechanics; the predicted ruin of family and property never materialised, and no landed aristocracy of the European type formed in the United States [1][2].
- The US-imposed Japanese (1946–50) and Korean (1949–50) land reforms and zaibatsu dissolutions were among history's most abrupt dynastic expropriations — and were followed by two of history's fastest sustained growth accelerations and decades of exceptionally equal, high-mobility development [3][4][5].
- The high-estate-tax decades in the US and UK (top rates 70–80%, roughly 1940s–1970s) coincided with the Great Compression, the fastest productivity growth on record, and — per Lindert's "free lunch puzzle" — no detectable growth penalty from high progressive taxation [6][7].
- The GI Bill and the Homestead Acts show the endowment side: mass capital and asset grants to ordinary citizens produced measurable, in some cases multi-generational, gains in education, homeownership, and mobility — and where groups were excluded (Black veterans, Black homesteaders), the wealth gap that opened is still visible, which is the same natural experiment run in reverse [8][9].
- None of these cases is a clean trial of the proposal; every one of them falsifies a specific catastrophe prediction that opponents of the proposal still make (see [`../09-objections/`](../09-objections/README.md)).

## 1. America abolishes hereditary wealth mechanics (1776–c.1800)

**What happened.** Beginning with Jefferson's Virginia bills (entail 1776, primogeniture 1785), every American state abolished the two legal institutions that had kept European estates intact down a single line of descent; the process was complete by roughly 1800 (to verify) [1]. Contemporary opponents predicted the destruction of family continuity and agricultural capital. The full civic-republican argument is in [`democratic-renewal.md`](democratic-renewal.md) §2; the historical detail belongs to [`../05-history/`](../05-history/README.md).

**What it proves.** A liberal commercial society can deliberately destroy hereditary transmission machinery, by ordinary legislation, with elite consent, without economic disruption — the US economy of 1800–1860 was among the world's fastest-growing. Tocqueville rated partible inheritance a first-order cause of American social equality [2].

**What it doesn't prove.** The reform regulated the *form* of transmission, not the *amount*: fortunes could still pass entire by will. It did not prevent the Gilded Age — which is precisely why Carnegie and Roosevelt ([`../05-history/carnegie-1889.md`](../05-history/carnegie-1889.md), [`../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md`](../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md)) demanded the quantity-side instrument a century later. Land abundance on the frontier is a serious confound for the equality outcome.

## 2. Japan and Korea: imposed dynastic breaks, then miracles (1946–1960s)

**What happened.** Under the US occupation, Japan dissolved the zaibatsu holding companies, purged their controlling families from management, and executed a land reform that transferred roughly a third of Japan's farmland from landlords to tenants at prices inflation rendered near-confiscatory (to verify) [3]. Occupation-era tax reform also introduced steep progressive wealth and inheritance levies (to verify). South Korea's 1949–50 land reform was comparably sweeping [4]. In both countries, centuries-old patterns of dynastic landholding were broken within half a decade.

**What it proves.** The two most catastrophic predictions — capital strike and growth collapse — were falsified as completely as history ever falsifies anything. Both countries entered the fastest sustained growth of the twentieth century within years, from starting points of exceptional post-reform equality; Rodrik and others treat that initial equality as a plausible *contributor* to subsequent growth via education, stability, and broad-based demand [5]. Japan's postwar decades produced a society in which great fortunes were rebuilt by *founders* (Honda, Sony, Panasonic) rather than inherited — a live demonstration of the founder-vs-heir composition argument in [`meritocratic-legitimacy.md`](meritocratic-legitimacy.md) §5.

**What it doesn't prove.** Causality is genuinely uncontaminatable here and must be handled with care: postwar reconstruction, US aid and market access, technology catch-up, and high global growth all confound. The reforms were imposed by an occupier, not adopted democratically — the political lesson transfers weakly (the democratic-adoption problem is [`../12-political-adoption/`](../12-political-adoption/README.md)'s brief). The most defensible claim is directional: history's largest deliberate dynastic breaks were followed by prosperity, not ruin, and the initial-equality channel plausibly helped. Note also the sequel: chaebol dynasties re-formed in Korea where transmission was not policed — evidence that the *break* works but requires a *maintenance instrument*, which is what the recurring tax provides (see [`../11-geographies/korea-japan.md`](../11-geographies/korea-japan.md)).

## 3. The high-estate-tax decades and the Great Compression (c.1940–1976)

**What happened.** The US federal estate tax carried a 77% top rate from 1941 to 1976; UK estate duty peaked around 80% in the late 1960s; comparable rates held across much of Europe [6] ([`../05-history/us-estate-tax.md`](../05-history/us-estate-tax.md), [`../05-history/uk-estate-duty.md`](../05-history/uk-estate-duty.md)). These decades contain the Great Compression of wealth and income inequality and the strongest broad-based productivity growth in the modern record.

**What it proves.** Correlation honesty first: this is coincidence-in-time, not identified causality, and the compression owed much to war destruction, inflation, and regulation (Piketty's own account — [`piketty-r-vs-g.md`](piketty-r-vs-g.md) §1). What the period *does* establish is compatibility: near-confiscatory top transfer taxes coexisted for a generation with full employment, high investment, rapid growth, and flourishing entrepreneurship. Lindert's systematic cross-country work generalises the point — the "free lunch puzzle" is that high-tax welfare states show no measurable GDP penalty relative to low-tax peers, in part because they choose relatively efficient tax mixes [7]. The claim "such rates would wreck the economy" is not a prediction; it is a retrodiction already falsified by thirty years of data. The converse experiment — the abolition wave after 2000 — produced no growth dividend either ([`economic-efficiency.md`](economic-efficiency.md) §5, [`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md)).

**What it doesn't prove.** That the estate tax *caused* the compression (avoidance meant effective rates sat well below statutory ones — a design lesson taken up in [`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md)), or that mid-century growth conditions can be re-run at will.

## 4. The endowment side: GI Bill and Homestead Acts

**What happened.** The Homestead Acts (1862–) transferred roughly 10% of US land to ~1.6 million households in ~160-acre capital grants (to verify) [8]. The GI Bill (1944) financed university education, vocational training, and zero-down-payment home loans for a majority cohort of returning veterans — the largest asset-building programme in US history [9].

**What it proves.** Mass capital grants to ordinary citizens are administrable, popular, and productive. The GI Bill's education provisions raised college completion for eligible cohorts and its loan provisions helped drive homeownership from under half to two-thirds of households (to verify); homesteading regions show persistent effects on landholding breadth (to verify) [8][9]. Politically, both programmes were framed as *earned entitlement*, not welfare — the exact grammar the citizen's endowment adopts ([`equal-start.md`](equal-start.md) §3, [`../06-palatability/framing.md`](../06-palatability/framing.md)). The dark mirror strengthens the inference: Black veterans were largely excluded from GI Bill housing benefits in practice, and the racial wealth gap that entrenched itself over the following decades is measurable today (to verify) [9] — a demonstration that *asset access at the start of adult life compounds for generations*, which is the endowment's entire theory.

**What it doesn't prove.** Neither programme was universal or funded by transfer taxation; the Homestead grants came from expropriated Indigenous land, which is a moral disanalogy, not a fiscal model. Selection into military service and homesteading complicates the causal estimates.

## 5. Nordic capital formation under universalism

**What happened.** The Nordic countries built universal-welfare capitalism combining high taxation, universal services and pensions, and — through pension funds and cooperative and state holdings — broad socialisation of capital *access*: education, housing finance, and income security that function as a diffuse in-kind endowment [7][10].

**What it proves.** The world's most consistent top performers on intergenerational mobility, entrepreneurship-per-capita measures, and life satisfaction are the societies that most fully guarantee every citizen a funded start (to verify) [10]. The "great Gatsby curve" association between equality and mobility is exactly what the equal-start theory predicts ([`../01-problem/mobility.md`](../01-problem/mobility.md)).

**What it doesn't prove.** Sweden and Norway *abolished* inheritance taxation while remaining equal and mobile — the standing counterexample this manifesto must answer honestly: small, cohesive, resource- or institution-rich countries with compressed pre-tax distributions and universal services could afford to drop the instrument, at the cost (visible in Swedish billionaire wealth-concentration data, to verify) of renewed dynastic accumulation at the very top [11] (full treatment in [`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md) and [`../11-geographies/nordics.md`](../11-geographies/nordics.md)).

## 7. What the record adds up to

No single case is the proposal; together they cover it. Rows 1–2 show dynastic breaks are survivable and arguably generative; row 3 shows the tax rates are compatible with prosperity; row 4 shows the grants work and compound; row 5 shows universal starts and dynamism co-exist durably. Each case also teaches a design lesson the proposal incorporates: form-regulation without quantity-limits fails (row 1 → the threshold design, [`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md)); one-off breaks without maintenance re-concentrate (row 2 → a recurring tax, not a levy); statutory rates without enforcement architecture leak (row 3 → [`../07-implementation/`](../07-implementation/README.md) and [`../08-capital-flight/evidence.md`](../08-capital-flight/evidence.md)); exclusion from grants compounds as surely as inclusion (row 4 → universality). The rhetorical conclusion for [`../14-communications/debate-toolkit.md`](../14-communications/debate-toolkit.md): the burden of proof has been quietly inverted. The proposal's components have been field-tested; the catastrophe predictions have been field-falsified. It is the *opponents* of the proposal who are arguing against the historical record.
