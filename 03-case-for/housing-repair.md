# The Housing Case: Repairing the Ownership Ladder

**Executive summary.** Housing is where inherited wealth now most visibly decides life outcomes: the deposit, not the salary, is the binding constraint on ownership, and family money already finances roughly half of young-adult purchases while the children of renters wait. This file makes the consolidated housing argument for the proposal — stated throughout as an *argument*, since the magnitudes are uncertain: the tax-plus-endowment pair is the only deposit-side intervention yet proposed that carries its own supply offset, because the same act that hands every 25-year-old a deposit also pushes housing stock onto the market (estate sales instead of intact transmission, earlier downsizing, the unwinding of tax-shelter demand for land and property) rather than bidding up a fixed stock, as deposit subsidies to date have done.

(The diagnostic evidence lives in [`../01-problem/housing.md`](../01-problem/housing.md); the price-effect analysis this file draws on is [`../16-second-order/asset-prices.md`](../16-second-order/asset-prices.md) §2 and [`../16-second-order/endowment-effects.md`](../16-second-order/endowment-effects.md) §5; the endowment's design is [`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md).)

## Key findings

- The problem is a deposit problem. UK house prices rose from ~4× average earnings in 1997 to ~8× by 2022 (9×+ in London); the median UK first-time buyer is now 34, the US median first-time buyer 38 — an all-time high [1][2][3].
- Ownership is already substantially inherited: Legal & General's "Bank of Mum and Dad" research finds family assistance involved in roughly half of purchases by under-35s — lending volumes that would rank it among the top ten UK mortgage lenders in some years [4]. The endowment does not introduce family capital into housing; it universalises access to what is currently a hereditary channel.
- Pure demand-side subsidies fail predictably: Carozzi, Hilber & Yu find Help to Buy raised new-build prices with no detectable construction effect in supply-constrained Greater London, and raised construction only near the elastic English/Welsh border [5]. Any deposit policy must answer this finding, and most cannot.
- The pair's supply offset (the argument of this file): above-threshold estates are sold into the market rather than transmitted intact; downsizing advances; buy-to-let and second homes receive no deferral at death ([`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md) §3) and so reach the market each generation; and shelter-driven demand for land and property unwinds ([`../16-second-order/asset-prices.md`](../16-second-order/asset-prices.md) §§2–4) [6][7].
- The endowment leaks less into prices than a housing-earmarked subsidy: it is general capital — education, debt clearance, and business formation divert part of the demand away from housing, unlike Help to Buy, which tied the money to a property purchase by construction [5][8].
- Honest bounds, stated up front: no credible model produces a national price crash from these channels; housing *levels* remain dominated by planning, credit, and construction policy; partial capitalisation of the endowment into entry-level prices is still likely. The claim is about access, turnover, and *who* gets to buy — not cheapness [7][8].

## 1. The deposit is the gate, and the gate is hereditary

The post-1990s doubling of price-to-earnings ratios converted the deposit from a savings problem into a capital problem: at 8× earnings, a 10–15% deposit is one to two years of gross pay, saved from post-rent income — arithmetic that closes for most young adults without a transfer [1][2]. The market has already produced the "solution": parental capital. Family assistance now sits behind roughly half of under-35 purchases [4], and Resolution Foundation's projection follows: the single biggest determinant of whether a young person owns their home is becoming whether their parents did [9]. This is the clearest live mechanism by which advantage transmits across generations — documented in full in [`../01-problem/housing.md`](../01-problem/housing.md) — and it is why housing is the terrain on which the proposal's offer is most legible to the median young voter.

## 2. Why existing demand-side policy inflates

Governments repeatedly reach for demand-side instruments — deposit subsidies, mortgage guarantees, credit expansion — because supply-side reform is slow and politically costly. The evidence on what happens next is unusually clean. Carozzi, Hilber and Yu's study of Help to Buy finds the scheme's equity loans capitalised into new-build prices in severely supply-constrained Greater London with no detectable increase in construction, while near the elastic English/Welsh border the same money produced construction without a noticeable price effect [5]. The general lesson: money pointed at housing, in an inelastic market, becomes price, and the subsidy's incidence lands on sellers and landowners rather than buyers. A universal £50k endowment *viewed alone* is exposed to exactly this critique, and [`../16-second-order/endowment-effects.md`](../16-second-order/endowment-effects.md) §5 treats it as one of the two serious failure modes of the grant.

## 3. What the pair does that no deposit subsidy can

The argument — and it is an argument about a package, not a finding about a precedent, because no precedent exists ([`../04-case-against/model-risk.md`](../04-case-against/model-risk.md) owns that caveat): the proposal is not a demand subsidy but a *simultaneous* demand-and-supply operation on the same asset class.

| Channel | Mechanism | Direction for entry-level access |
|---|---|---|
| Universal deposit at 25 | £50k of unrestricted capital at the age the ownership ladder is boarded | Demand up at entry level — the risk channel [5][8] |
| Estate sales replace intact transmission | Above-threshold housing is sold to pay tax or settled in-specie rather than passing to heirs; supply concentrated in under-occupied family homes | Supply up, top-end first, filtering down [6][7] |
| Downsizing advances | Owners with no above-threshold transmission motive realise housing wealth earlier ([`../16-second-order/savings-consumption.md`](../16-second-order/savings-consumption.md) §4) | Supply up |
| Rental stock recycles | Buy-to-let portfolios and second homes get no residence deferral; they crystallise and largely sell at each death ([`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md) §3) | Supply of former-BTL stock to owner-occupiers up |
| Shelter demand unwinds | Property held or structured for death-tax sheltering loses its tax premium ([`../16-second-order/asset-prices.md`](../16-second-order/asset-prices.md) §§3–4) | Speculative/shelter demand down |
| Demand leakage | The endowment is not housing-earmarked: education, debt, business uses divert part of the flow | Capitalisation muted relative to Help to Buy [5][8] |

The net is what [`../16-second-order/asset-prices.md`](../16-second-order/asset-prices.md) calls a *rotation*: top-end prices soften, entry-level prices firm, and the ratio between them — the ladder's rung spacing — compresses. Flow arithmetic keeps the demand shock modest: roughly 700k endowed 25-year-olds a year against ~1.1m annual UK residential transactions, phased in over a decade [8].

## 4. The honest bounds

Three concessions are load-bearing, and the communications strategy depends on making them before opponents do ([`../14-communications/inoculation.md`](../14-communications/inoculation.md)):

1. **Partial capitalisation is still likely.** Sellers of starter homes will capture some of the endowment; the leakage and supply channels mute this, they do not abolish it. A staged-drawdown variant is the flagged fallback [8].
2. **The timing is asymmetric.** Shelter-premium effects reprice fast, but estate-sale supply builds over a mortality cycle; the housing offer is a decade-scale promise, not an electoral-cycle one [7].
3. **This is not a substitute for supply policy.** Planning, credit, and construction still set the level of prices; the proposal changes who holds the stock and who can bid for it. The defensible public claim is "more family homes reaching the market each year, and every buyer arriving with a deposit" — not "cheap houses" [7].

## 5. The distributional core

Strip the mechanics and the housing case is the equal-start case ([equal-start.md](equal-start.md)) applied to the asset that matters most: the current system runs a capital test at the ownership gate and administers it through parents; the proposal keeps the gate but issues every citizen the entry fee, funded by the very transmissions that currently rig the test. For the pillar-level framing — the tax as the repair of capitalism's own legitimacy claim — this is the concrete instance most voters will actually feel; the polling treatment of the "deposit dividend" framing is at [`../06-palatability/framing.md`](../06-palatability/framing.md).
