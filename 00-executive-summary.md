# Executive Summary

**Thesis.** Concentrated inherited wealth is a primary engine of the pathologies we label "late-stage capitalism": runaway inequality, collapsing social mobility, a housing market decoupled from labour income, democratic capture by dynastic interests, and the erosion of meritocratic legitimacy. A 100% inheritance tax — specified as a 100% *marginal* rate above a generous per-recipient lifetime threshold, with proceeds funding a universal citizen's endowment — would address these pathologies at the source without requiring the state to micromanage the rest of the economy.

**Claim of reasonableness.** This is not a fringe proposal. Thomas Paine, John Stuart Mill, Andrew Carnegie, and Theodore Roosevelt all argued for heavy or near-total death duties; twentieth-century estate taxes in the US and UK briefly approached the levels this thesis proposes; and Thomas Piketty's recent work has revived the intellectual case.

## Three questions answered by this project

1. **Is the thesis reasonable?** Yes, within a well-specified variant (recipient-based, lifetime-cumulative, paired with a universal endowment, with credible family-business carve-outs). See [03-case-for/](03-case-for/) and [04-case-against/](04-case-against/).
2. **What has been proposed or achieved historically?** Paine (1797), Mill (1848), Carnegie (1889), US Estate Tax with peak rate ~77% (1941–76), UK Estate Duty with peak rate ~80% (1969), Piketty's "inheritance for all" (2019). See [05-history/](05-history/).
3. **How do we make it palatable?** Reframe from "death tax on grieving families" to "universal inheritance funded by extreme fortunes" and pair with a visible individual benefit (a five-figure grant to every young adult). See [06-palatability/](06-palatability/).

## The single most important design choice

Every serious historical proposal pairs the tax on large inheritances with a universal benefit to those currently inheriting nothing. Pure confiscation without the redistribution half has no credible intellectual proponent and no political future. The manifesto should lead with the universal endowment, not the tax.

## Beyond the argument: engineering and strategy

The case standing alone wins debates, not elections. The project therefore extends into two further layers:

- **Engineering** — every concrete difficulty has a designed answer: valuation, gifts, trusts, schooling, and nepo jobs in [07-implementation/](07-implementation/); the "wealthy will leave" question in [08-capital-flight/](08-capital-flight/); the path from today's law to the full scheme in [15-transition/](15-transition/); and honest analysis of what changes after adoption in [16-second-order/](16-second-order/).
- **Strategy** — the quick-fire rebuttal armoury in [09-objections/](09-objections/); the opposition's attack playbook and its mitigations in [10-opposition/](10-opposition/); which societies adopt first in [11-geographies/](11-geographies/); the self-interested case for each party family in [12-political-adoption/](12-political-adoption/); movement-building doctrine in [13-movement/](13-movement/); and communications operations in [14-communications/](14-communications/).

The strategic through-line: the universal endowment is not only the policy's moral half — it is its political engine (a concrete benefit every young voter can see), its coalition builder (grandchildren of swing voters), and, once enacted, its bodyguard (700,000 new stakeholders every year).
