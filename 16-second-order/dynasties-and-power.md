# Dynasties and Power

**Executive summary.** The policy's long-run political economy is the point of the whole project: firm control passes to managers, markets, and foundations rather than heirs; media-ownership dynasties break; the inherited donor class thins over one generational turnover; and the institutions of hereditary status lose their economic base. But power abhors a vacuum — this file also maps, honestly, where concentration re-forms: managerial capture, foundation executives, fame-based political dynasties, and the state itself.

## 1. Firm control: from heirs to managers, markets, and foundations

Above the threshold, a controlling stake cannot pass intact to children. Options at succession: sale to the market or trade buyers; management/employee buyout; transfer to a foundation (within the [charitable-giving.md](charitable-giving.md) guardrails); in-specie settlement with the state fund; or the deferred-payment route for genuine operating businesses ([`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md) §5, which defers *payment*, not the end of dynastic control).

**The performance evidence favours this.** Pérez-González (2006) and Bennedsen, Nielsen, Pérez-González & Wolfenzon (2007, Danish registry data with an instrument from the first child's gender) both find family-CEO succession causes significant declines in firm performance relative to professional-CEO succession (to verify magnitudes) [1][2]. Heir succession is, on the evidence, a productivity tax the economy currently pays for dynastic continuity; [`../04-case-against/family-business.md`](../04-case-against/family-business.md) steelmans the counter-case (stewardship, long horizons, trust capital) — the honest reconciliation is that family *ownership* has virtues the evidence partly supports, while family *management succession by birthright* is where the damage concentrates.

**The German preview.** Foundation-linked ownership already runs major firms: Robert Bosch GmbH (~94% owned by Robert Bosch Stiftung, with voting rights held by a separate industrial trust); Carl Zeiss (wholly foundation-owned since 1889); ZF Friedrichshafen; in Denmark, Novo Nordisk and Maersk under enterprise foundations (to verify structures) [3]. The literature on Danish enterprise foundations (Thomsen et al.) finds they perform comparably to conventionally owned firms with longer horizons (to verify) [3]. Cautionary exhibit: IKEA's Stichting INGKA architecture shows foundation form used as dynastic-control *avoidance* — exactly what the control test in [`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md) §4 and [`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md) must catch.

## 2. Media ownership succession breaks

Hereditary media control — Murdoch, Rothermere, the former Barclay holdings — is a special case of §1 with a democratic multiplier: [`../10-opposition/elite-media-capture.md`](../10-opposition/elite-media-capture.md) documents why dynastic media are also the policy's most motivated opponents. Under the tax, each proprietor's death forces dispersal of control above the threshold: to markets, plural ownership, foundation/trust models (the Guardian's Scott Trust as the domestic precedent). Over one mortality cycle, the hereditary press baron becomes structurally impossible. This is simultaneously a democratic gain and the reason the policy's passage must be sequenced against media opposition ([`../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md`](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md)).

## 3. The donor class thins — slowly

Political finance drawn from inherited fortunes declines only as those fortunes' holders die: living donors are untouched, so the effect lags adoption by decades. Expect the inherited share of the donor class to fall with each mortality cohort, shifting political money toward earned fortunes (first-generation founders) and organised small-dollar/institutional channels. That is a *change in the composition* of political money, not its end — campaign-finance rules remain a separate and necessary instrument ([`../01-problem/political-capture.md`](../01-problem/political-capture.md) makes the diagnostic case; the tax is one lever there, not the whole repair).

## 4. Aristocratic institutions lose their base

Hereditary landed estates, the honours-and-season complex, and the boarding-school pipeline all rest economically on transmissible capital. Under the proposal, estates above threshold pass to the state fund, charity, or market on each death (deferral for working farms delays payment, not transmission limits). Over two generations the economic substrate of hereditary aristocracy — already reduced by the 20th-century estate duties before their relief-riddled retreat ([`../05-history/uk-estate-duty.md`](../05-history/uk-estate-duty.md)) — winds down. Institutions adapt or shrink: schools broaden intake as the hereditary client base thins; heritage land plausibly consolidates into trust/National-Trust-type ownership (an in-specie settlement channel worth designing deliberately).

## 5. New elite formation — the honest map

| Old power channel | Post-tax successor | Risk level | Watch / response |
|---|---|---|---|
| Owner-dynasty control of firms | Professional managers | Medium–high | Manager-capture: agency costs, pay ratcheting, entrenchment — the Berle–Means problem returns at scale; governance and shareholder-power policy becomes *more* important, not less |
| Dynastic private wealth | Foundation sector | Medium | Foundation-executive class; answered by payout floors and sunsets ([charitable-giving.md](charitable-giving.md) §4) |
| Family political funding | Fame- and network-based political dynasties | Medium | Name recognition, not money, already carries political families (multiple US and UK examples); the tax does not touch this channel and should not claim to |
| Private concentrated capital | The state fund itself | High — the serious one | A fund receiving in-specie stakes across the economy is a power concentration; [`../04-case-against/state-failure.md`](../04-case-against/state-failure.md) steelmans this. Design responses: independent trustee governance on the Norwegian GPFG model, voting-rights neutrality rules, statutory distribution mandate to the endowment (to verify GPFG governance details) |
| Financial capital transmission | Human-capital and network transmission | Certain, weaker form | [`../07-implementation/human-capital.md`](../07-implementation/human-capital.md) and [`../07-implementation/nepotism-and-networks.md`](../07-implementation/nepotism-and-networks.md) |

Two honest framing points. First, elite circulation is the realistic goal: the policy makes elite status *contestable each generation* rather than heritable, which is Pareto's circulation done by statute instead of by crisis. Second, the manager-capture and state-fund risks are not reasons to prefer the status quo — hereditary concentration plus managerial agency costs — but they do mean the policy raises the stakes of corporate-governance and fund-governance design, which the manifesto should own rather than discover later.

## 6. Timeline of effects

| Horizon | What has changed |
|---|---|
| 0–10 years | Announcement-driven restructuring; foundation formations; first contested successions; media opposition peaks ([`../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md`](../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md)) |
| 10–30 years | One mortality cycle: major firm and media successions resolve to non-heir forms; donor-class composition visibly shifts; state fund reaches systemically significant scale — governance stress-tests arrive |
| 30–60 years | Hereditary economic elite substantially wound down; new-axis elites (managerial, credential, fame, state) fully formed and requiring their own countervailing policy |
