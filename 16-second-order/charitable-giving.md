# Charitable Giving After the Tax

**Executive summary.** With a 100% marginal rate above the threshold and a full charitable exemption, the effective price of a charitable bequest for a large estate falls to zero relative to leaving money to heirs — a large surge in charitable giving is therefore one of the most confident predictions in this entire section. The policy consequently converts dynastic wealth into philanthropic wealth as much as into public revenue; this file argues that is still a categorical improvement, but only if the anti-cycling, payout, and sunset guardrails hold.

## 1. Why a surge is near-certain

The mechanism is a price effect. Under current UK IHT (40%), £1 bequeathed to charity "costs" the heirs 60p of forgone inheritance; under the proposal, £1 given to charity above the threshold costs the family nothing at all — the alternative is 100% tax. The estate's choice above the threshold collapses to: state, charity, or lifetime consumption.

The US estate-tax literature on the charitable deduction consistently finds charitable bequests are responsive to this tax price, though the estimated strength varies: Joulfaian's work on estate-tax returns puts the price elasticity at about −0.7 (1992 decedents), while Bakija, Gale & Slemrod's study of tax-rate variation across states and time finds a large and significant elasticity of about −2.1 [1][2]. Estimates of repeal point the same way: Bakija & Gale calculate that abolishing the estate tax would cut charitable bequests by 22–37%, or roughly $3.6–6bn a year at early-2000s giving levels [6]. At a 100% marginal rate the price of giving hits its floor, so these elasticities imply the strongest giving response the literature has ever had occasion to measure.

Corroborating behavioural evidence: even under the current 40% UK regime, the reduced-rate incentive (36% IHT for estates leaving 10%+ to charity, FA 2012) is heavily used — HMRC data show over £2bn a year passing to charity through the IHT charitable exemption, just under half of it from estates paying the 36% reduced rate [3].

## 2. Scale scenarios

The share of above-threshold wealth diverted to charity rather than paid in tax is the single largest uncertainty in the revenue arithmetic of [`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md).

| Scenario | Share of above-threshold estate value to charity | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Low | ~15–25% | Modest hit to revenue; endowment funding intact |
| Central | ~35–50% | Material revenue diversion; philanthropy roughly co-equal with Treasury as destination of dynastic wealth |
| High | ~60%+ | The policy is primarily a philanthropy mandate with a revenue side-effect; endowment needs the revenue-bridge in [`../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md`](../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md) |

For calibration: US estates above $50m already left about 20% of gross estate value to charity under a 40% top rate (year-of-death 2019) [7]; a 100% rate should push the share far higher. Planning conservatively means budgeting on the central-to-high scenarios.

## 3. Bug or feature? The two readings

| | Reading A: bug | Reading B: feature |
|---|---|---|
| Core claim | Plutocratic power persists, relabelled | Carnegie's intended outcome achieved |
| Mechanism | Perpetual foundations extend donor control past death; unaccountable, tax-advantaged, self-perpetuating boards | Surplus fortunes administered for public benefit during and shortly after life, rather than transmitted as private dynastic power |
| Key text | Rob Reich, *Just Giving* (2018): foundations are "institutional oddities" in a democracy — donor-directed, perpetual, unaccountable [4] | Carnegie, [*The Gospel of Wealth* (1889)](https://www.carnegie.org/publications/the-gospel-of-wealth/): the man who dies rich "dies disgraced"; Carnegie explicitly endorsed near-confiscatory death duties as the enforcement mechanism (see [`../05-history/carnegie-1889.md`](../05-history/carnegie-1889.md)) |
| Exhibits | Gates Foundation endowment $89bn (Dec 2025) [8]; Wellcome Trust portfolio £39.9bn (Sept 2025) [9]; US foundation sector ~$1.5tn in assets (end-2023 estimate) [10] — each a power centre no voter can remove | Carnegie's libraries, Rockefeller's universities, Wellcome's science funding: durable public goods no legislature would have funded |

Both readings are partly right, which is why the design cannot be laissez-faire about the charitable channel.

## 4. Guardrails: recap and extension

[`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md) §4 already specifies the first three; the fourth is this file's addition to the design agenda.

1. **Anti-cycling rule.** Assets that flow to a charity and back to the donor's family or controlled entities trigger full crystallisation plus penalty.
2. **Control test.** Foundations with donor-family board majorities, family-adjacent grant-making, or material family employment income are recharacterised as private vehicles and taxed.
3. **Payout floor.** Minimum 5% annual qualifying distribution, mirroring US [IRC §4942](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/4942) — endowment hoarding is not public benefit.
4. **Sunset requirement (proposed).** Foundations receiving exempt bequests above a size threshold must adopt a spend-down horizon — full distribution within a fixed period (candidate range: 30–80 years after the founder's death). Precedents: the Gates Foundation's May 2025 commitment to spend more than $200bn and close permanently in 2045 — replacing its earlier charter rule of closing 20 years after Bill Gates's death [5]; Julius Rosenwald's deliberately terminating fund (fully spent by 1948); Atlantic Philanthropies' completed spend-down (2020). Perpetuity should be the regulated exception (operating charities, land trusts), not the default.

## 5. The perpetuity problem, honestly stated

Even with all four guardrails, the policy grows the foundation sector substantially. Foundation executives become a more significant elite; grant-making priorities of the dead constrain the living; charity law (UK cy-près doctrine, Charity Commission capacity) comes under load it was not built for. Reich's democratic objection is not fully answerable by governance rules — a society that routes a large share of surplus fortunes through philanthropy has chosen donor-directed public goods over legislature-directed ones at the margin. This is a real cost and should be conceded, not spun. The mitigations are the payout floor and sunset rule (which convert stock-power into time-limited flow), plus Charity Commission / IRS capacity funding as a named implementation cost in [`../07-implementation/administration.md`](../07-implementation/administration.md).

## 6. Why it is still a categorical improvement

The decisive distinction is between *wealth* and *heritable control*:

- **A foundation cannot be consumed, bequeathed, or collateralised** by anyone. The founder's descendants may sit on a board (until the control test bites) but they cannot sell the endowment, spend it on themselves, or pass board seats as property.
- **Foundations are subject to public-benefit law** — reporting, regulators, payout duties. Dynastic private wealth is subject to essentially nothing.
- **Control decays by design.** Boards turn over; the sunset rule terminates the institution; the family's grip weakens each generation. Dynastic wealth compounds; foundation influence amortises.
- **The founder chose the public purpose while alive.** That is Carnegie's whole argument: society's claim is enforced at death either through the Treasury or through binding dedication to public benefit — and either destination breaks the hereditary transmission of private economic power that [`../01-problem/political-capture.md`](../01-problem/political-capture.md) identifies as the core pathology.

The honest formulation for the manifesto: *the policy does not abolish big philanthropy — it makes big philanthropy compulsory for fortunes that would otherwise be dynasties, and time-limits the institutions it creates.*

## 7. Monitoring indicators

- Share of above-threshold estate value flowing to (a) Treasury, (b) charity, (c) lifetime consumption — annual, from the Central Register of Receipts ([`../02-proposal/mechanism.md`](../02-proposal/mechanism.md)).
- Number and asset size of foundations failing the control test; anti-cycling prosecutions.
- Effective payout rates versus the 5% floor; sector-wide spend-down compliance.
- Concentration of grant-making (share of total foundation disbursements by top 10 foundations).
