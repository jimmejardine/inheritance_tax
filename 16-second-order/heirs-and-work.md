# Heirs and Work: The Carnegie Effect

**Executive summary.** A well-replicated empirical literature — inheritance studies and lottery natural experiments — shows that large windfalls measurably reduce recipients' labour supply, confirming Carnegie's 1889 conjecture that the parent who leaves enormous wealth "deadens the talents and energies" of the child. The tax removes this distortion precisely where it is largest, and it removes it *decades early*, because people who merely expect large inheritances already behave differently mid-career.

## 1. The Carnegie conjecture and its empirical confirmation

Carnegie's claim ([`../05-history/carnegie-1889.md`](../05-history/carnegie-1889.md)) was behavioural, not moral: enormous bequests damage the recipient's industry. A century later the econometrics agreed.

| Study | Design | Headline finding |
|---|---|---|
| Holtz-Eakin, Joulfaian & Rosen (1993, *QJE*) | US tax records, inheritances matched to labour-force behaviour | Larger inheritances significantly raise the probability of exiting the labour force: a single person inheriting ~$150k was roughly four times more likely to exit than one inheriting under $25k [1] |
| Imbens, Rubin & Sacerdote (2001, *AER*) | Massachusetts lottery winners vs non-winners | Unearned income reduces earnings — marginal propensity to earn out of unearned income approximately −0.11 (11 cents less earned per dollar of windfall) [2] |
| Cesarini, Lindqvist, Notowidigdo & Östling (2017, *AER*) | Swedish lottery administrative data | Immediate, persistent earnings reductions — about 1.1% of the prize per year, lasting at least ten years; implied lifetime marginal propensity to earn out of unearned income ≈ −0.11 [3] |
| Bø, Halvorsen & Thoresen (2019, *JHR*) | Norwegian inheritances, full-population registry data | Significant labour-supply reductions for recipients of large inheritances; effect varies with transfer size, recipient age, and eligibility for other transfers [4] |

The pattern is one of the more robust findings in applied public economics: the income effect of windfalls on labour supply is real, persistent, and increasing in windfall size. Per pound the effect is modest — which matters for the endowment's £50k ([endowment-effects.md](endowment-effects.md)) — but inheritances at the dynastic scale the tax targets are hundreds of multiples of the endowment, and the labour-supply damage scales accordingly.

## 2. What the tax changes

Under the proposal, no one can receive more than £500k/$750k in lifetime capital transfers ([`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md)). The would-have-been heirs of dynasties therefore work — like everyone else. Concretely:

- The population currently receiving £1m+ windfalls (small in number, large in aggregate wealth) shifts from partial labour-force exit toward normal careers.
- The aggregate labour-supply gain is small in FTE terms (few people are affected) but concentrated among people with above-average education and networks — arguably high-productivity labour currently withdrawn.
- The distortion removed is exactly the one the current system maximises: the bigger the inheritance, the bigger both the windfall and the work disincentive.

This is the labour-supply side of the argument made on welfare grounds in [`../03-case-for/heirs-burden.md`](../03-case-for/heirs-burden.md): the same literature that shows heirs stop working supports the claim that unlimited inheritance is bad *for the heirs* — purpose, mastery, and autonomous achievement are what large windfalls crowd out. The two files should be read together: this one establishes the behavioural facts, that one draws the welfare conclusion.

## 3. The expectation effect: behaviour changes decades before the death

The under-studied half of the mechanism. People who *expect* substantial inheritances behave differently long before receiving them:

- **Career selection and effort.** An expected seven-figure backstop rationally reduces investment in hard, high-variance careers — or, in the aristocratic pattern, in any career. "Waiting for dead men's shoes" is a medieval phrase for a modern phenomenon. US Health and Retirement Study panel evidence shows inheritance receipt raises the probability of retiring earlier than planned by 4.4 percentage points (about 12% relative to baseline) over an eight-year window — though the effect is strongest for *unexpected* inheritances, so direct evidence that mere expectation reduces mid-career effort remains thin [5].
- **Housing and location choices** anchor to the expected family asset rather than to the labour market.
- **Intra-family dependency.** The expectation is a lever the older generation holds over the younger — the control dynamic examined in [family-and-culture.md](family-and-culture.md) §1.

Because the tax is legislated for the future, its behavioural effect arrives at *announcement*, not at first deaths: a 30-year-old who learns they will receive at most £500k re-plans their career now. The transition literature treats announcement effects mainly as an avoidance risk ([`../15-transition/announcement-effects.md`](../15-transition/announcement-effects.md)); this is the benign announcement effect on the other side of the ledger.

## 4. Honest counterpoints

- **Inheritance as entrepreneurship finance.** Holtz-Eakin, Joulfaian & Rosen's companion papers found inheritance receipt raises entry into and survival of self-employment — evidence of liquidity constraints [6][7]. The tax closes this channel above the threshold. The design answer: the endowment re-opens it at scale — capital arrives at 25 for *everyone*, rather than in late middle age (UK inheritance receipt peaks at ages 55–64) for a few [8]. Whether the swap is net positive for entrepreneurship is genuinely contested — see [macro.md](macro.md) §4.
- **Selection, not causation?** Lottery studies answer this: winners are randomly assigned, and the effect persists.
- **Below-threshold inheritances continue.** Most heirs' behaviour is untouched; the file's claims are about the tail, which is where the money and the distortion both live.

## 5. Net assessment

| Effect | Direction | Confidence | Verdict |
