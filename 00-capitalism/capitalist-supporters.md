# Capitalist Supporters of Inheritance Scepticism

**Executive summary.** Scepticism about inherited wealth is not a socialist import into capitalism but a continuous native tradition running from Adam Smith through Bentham, Mill, Carnegie, and Theodore Roosevelt to Buffett, Bill Gates Sr., and the Giving Pledge — the system's founders, richest practitioners, and staunchest theorists. This file organises that lineage, states the two great pro-inheritance counter-authorities (Hayek and Friedman) fairly, and shows why their arguments — aimed at estate confiscation by the state — do not reach a recipient-capped, universally redistributed design.

## The lineage, organised

| Figure | Credential as capitalist | Position on inheritance | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Adam Smith (1776) | Founding theorist of the market economy | Entails — laws locking estates into bloodlines — "founded upon the most absurd of all suppositions," that "every successive generation of men have not an equal right to the earth" (*Wealth of Nations* Bk III, ch. 2) | [1] |
| Jeremy Bentham (1795) | Utilitarian architect of legal-liberal reform | Escheat over taxation: windfalls to distant heirs are the least-burdensome revenue source ever identified — "supply without burthen" | [../05-history/mill-1848.md](../05-history/mill-1848.md) |
| James Mill (1821) | Classical economist | Endorsed heavy succession taxation on Benthamite grounds (unverified — not located in *Elements of Political Economy*) | [../05-history/mill-1848.md](../05-history/mill-1848.md) |
| J. S. Mill (1848) | Canonical philosopher of liberty; author of the era's standard economics text | Cap what any person may *receive* over a lifetime; bequest is a right, inheritance is not | [../05-history/mill-1848.md](../05-history/mill-1848.md) |
| Andrew Carnegie (1889) | The era's richest industrialist | Heavy graduated death duties morally required; great fortunes left to heirs harm the heirs and the republic | [../05-history/carnegie-1889.md](../05-history/carnegie-1889.md) |
| Theodore Roosevelt (1906) | Republican president | Graduated inheritance tax on big fortunes, on anti-aristocratic grounds | [../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md](../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md) |
| Warren Buffett | Most admired living investor | Estate tax defender; dynastic wealth as membership in "the lucky sperm club" / winning the "ovarian lottery" (the latter elaborated at the 1997 Berkshire meeting [8]); leave children "enough money so that they would feel they could do anything, but not so much that they could do nothing" (*Fortune*, 29 September 1986 [2]) | [2][6][8] |
| Bill Gates Sr. (2002–) | Co-chair, Gates Foundation; father of Microsoft's founder | Led the campaign *against* US estate-tax repeal; co-authored *Wealth and Our Commonwealth* — society's investment precedes every fortune | [3] |
| The Giving Pledge signatories (2010–) | More than 250 of the world's wealthiest individuals and families, from 30 countries (as of 2025) | Revealed preference: the majority of the fortune should not go to heirs | [4] |

The pattern is diagnostic. These are not the system's critics; they are its founders (Smith), its philosophers (Bentham, the Mills), its most successful practitioners (Carnegie, Buffett, the Pledge signatories), and its political guardians (Roosevelt). Each reached inheritance scepticism *from* capitalist premises: Smith because entails throttled land markets; Mill because desert attaches to earning; Carnegie because heirs are harmed and fortunes are socially co-produced; Roosevelt because republics cannot coexist with hereditary economic aristocracy; Buffett because the "ovarian lottery" allocates nothing efficiently. The socialist tradition arrived at the same target later and for different reasons — the capitalist lineage is older ([../05-history/README.md](../05-history/README.md)).

## Three entries developed

**Smith.** The *Wealth of Nations* attack on entails is not incidental moralising but load-bearing economics: laws that bound estates to bloodlines in perpetuity kept land — the era's dominant capital — off the market, in the hands of owners selected by birth rather than by willingness to improve it. Smith's charge that entails "are founded upon the most absurd of all suppositions, the supposition that every successive generation of men have not an equal right to the earth, and to all that it possesses; but that the property of the present generation should be restrained and regulated according to the fancy of those who died, perhaps five hundred years ago" (Book III, ch. 2 [1]) is this project's argument in eighteenth-century dress: dynastic transmission is a *market restraint*, and removing it is deregulation, not confiscation. The American founders read him and legislated accordingly ([../03-case-for/democratic-renewal.md](../03-case-for/democratic-renewal.md)).

**Carnegie.** "The Gospel of Wealth" supplies the practitioner's twin arguments no theorist could: that the man who dies rich "dies disgraced" because the fortune was socially co-produced and owed back, and that great bequests injure the recipients — the observation behind the modern "Carnegie effect" literature on heirs' labour-force withdrawal ([../05-history/carnegie-1889.md](../05-history/carnegie-1889.md), [../16-second-order/heirs-and-work.md](../16-second-order/heirs-and-work.md)). Carnegie endorsed graduated death duties as the state's proper backstop where private virtue failed — the exact structural role this proposal assigns the tax.

**Roosevelt.** TR's case was neither fiscal nor egalitarian but *constitutional*: a tax "aimed merely at the inheritance or transmission in their entirety of those fortunes swollen beyond all healthy limits" (speech of 14 April 1906 [7]) — fortunes which, transmitted intact, would recreate in economic form the hereditary aristocracy the republic was founded to escape ([../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md](../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md)). He is the direct ancestor of this project's claim that transfer taxation is order-maintenance for a free polity, and the standing proof that the position belongs naturally on the political right ([../12-political-adoption/right-parties.md](../12-political-adoption/right-parties.md)).

## The other side, stated fairly: Hayek and Friedman

Honesty requires the two heaviest pro-market authorities who defended inheritance, at full strength:

**Hayek** (*The Constitution of Liberty*, 1960, ch. 6, "Equality, Value and Merit" [5]) argued that the family is a legitimate steward of capital and of the cultural transmission of standards; that passing on material advantage is continuous with passing on values and education; and that channelling parental ambition into wealth transfer is *less* socially harmful than channelling it into nepotistic placement of heirs into positions of power. Inequality of starting points, on his account, is part of the price of a free society, and the alternative — state allocation of starts — is worse.

**Friedman** (*Capitalism and Freedom*, 1962, ch. 10, "The Distribution of Income" [5]) argued that the distinction between inherited property and inherited talent is ethically arbitrary — no one deserves their genes either; that a person is entitled to dispose of what they have legitimately earned, and the freedom to give is part of the freedom to use; and that attempts to equalise outcomes destroy the incentive structure freedom requires.

These are serious arguments and this project does not caricature them ([../04-case-against/liberty-autonomy.md](../04-case-against/liberty-autonomy.md) carries the full steelman).

## The answer

Three responses, in ascending order of force:

1. **Both were answering a different proposal.** Hayek and Friedman wrote against estate confiscation *to the state* — the mid-century model in which death duties fed general government expenditure. The design here is receipt-capping with universal private redistribution: every pound above the cap becomes private capital in the hands of young private citizens ([../02-proposal/endowment.md](../02-proposal/endowment.md)), and assets sell to private buyers, not to ministries ([what-this-is-not.md](what-this-is-not.md)). The state is a conduit, not a destination. The "state allocation of starts" Hayek feared is exactly what the design avoids: the endowment is unconditional, uniform, and unallocated.
2. **Hayek's nepotism trade-off cuts the other way at scale.** Below the threshold — the family home, education, a genuine start — his stewardship argument wins, and the design concedes it: £500k per child flows untaxed ([../02-proposal/thresholds.md](../02-proposal/thresholds.md), [../07-implementation/boundary-problem.md](../07-implementation/boundary-problem.md)). Above it, "family stewardship" means multi-generational command of firm-scale capital by untested stewards — the documented underperformance case ([markets-vs-dynasties.md](markets-vs-dynasties.md)), not the transmission of standards.
3. **Friedman's own commitments strain against dynasty.** Friedman championed equality of opportunity against equality of outcome, school vouchers to break accident-of-birth schooling (*Capitalism and Freedom*, ch. 6), and the negative income tax as a floor without behavioural strings (ch. 12) [5]. A compounding hereditary capital aristocracy fails his opportunity test more comprehensively than any policy he attacked; and his genes-and-property parity argument, taken seriously, proves too much — no one proposes redistributing talent *because it cannot be done*, whereas financial inheritance is the one inherited advantage a liberal order *can* neutralise without touching persons ([../03-case-for/ethical.md](../03-case-for/ethical.md), [../07-implementation/human-capital.md](../07-implementation/human-capital.md)).

Note also what both concede: neither defends inheritance as *earned* by the heir. The desert claim — the moral core of capitalism's self-justification — is surrendered even by inheritance's best defenders; their case rests entirely on the giver's liberty and the family's function, which the threshold and lifetime-freedom design substantially honours.

## The modern revealed preference

The most striking twenty-first-century evidence is behavioural. The people who best understand large fortunes — those who built them — increasingly decline to transmit them. Buffett has pledged that "more than 99%" of his wealth will go to philanthropy during his lifetime or at death [6]; the Giving Pledge institutionalised the same choice across hundreds of founders; Gates Sr. organised billionaires *against* the repeal of the tax on their own estates [3][4]. When the arch-capitalists voluntarily do to their own fortunes roughly what the proposal would require of everyone's, the claim that the policy is anti-capitalist loses its last footing. What the Pledge does by private virtue, the proposal does by public rule — with the proceeds flowing not to foundations chosen by the wealthy but to every citizen equally ([../03-case-for/equal-start.md](../03-case-for/equal-start.md), [../16-second-order/charitable-giving.md](../16-second-order/charitable-giving.md)).

## Honest caveats on the lineage

Three qualifications, so the lineage is used accurately rather than talismanically. First, none of the historical figures proposed *this* design: Smith attacked entails, not bequests generally; Mill's cap had no endowment; Carnegie and Roosevelt endorsed heavy graduation, not a 100% marginal rate — the lineage supplies the *direction* and the *premises*, while the specific architecture is modern, with its own named modern proponents: Haslett's lifetime-quota-plus-100% (1997) and Robeyns' accessions cap with a citizen's stake (2024) are near-identical mechanisms ([../02-proposal/mechanism.md](../02-proposal/mechanism.md), [../05-history/has-this-been-proposed.md](../05-history/has-this-been-proposed.md)). Second, the revealed-preference argument from the Giving Pledge has a known limit: voluntary philanthropy preserves the donor's control and prestige in a way a tax does not, so signatories cannot simply be scored as supporters of compulsion — some are on record against estate taxation. Third, quotation discipline: several canonical lines circulate in paraphrase; the quotations here have been checked against primary or contemporaneous sources (Smith's Book III text [1]; Buffett's 1986 *Fortune* interview [2] and Giving Pledge letter [6]; Roosevelt's April 1906 speech [7]), and lines that could not be so checked are marked unverified. The lineage's strength survives all three caveats: what it establishes is not that these figures endorsed this statute, but that inheritance scepticism is native to capitalism's own intellectual tradition — which is all the premise of [README.md](README.md) requires.

## Verification checklist

- Smith's entails passage: **verified** — the phrase is "the most absurd of all suppositions" (not "manifest absurdity"), *Wealth of Nations* Book III, ch. 2 [1].
- Hayek ch. 6 ("Equality, Value and Merit") and Friedman ch. 10 ("The Distribution of Income"): **verified** — chapters confirmed, including Hayek's placement/nepotism passage ("placing them in positions which might bring them the income and the prestige that a fortune would have done") and Friedman's earned-vs-passed-on argument [5].
- Buffett quotations: **verified** — "enough money so that they would feel they could do anything, but not so much that they could do nothing" (Kirkland, *Fortune*, 29 Sept 1986 [2]); "ovarian lottery" (1997 Berkshire meeting [8]); ">99% to philanthropy" (Giving Pledge letter [6]).
- Giving Pledge signatory count: **verified/updated** — more than 250 signatories from 30 countries as of 2025 [4].
- Gates Sr. / Chuck Collins, *Wealth and Our Commonwealth* (2002) — the "society's investment" argument (book premise; detailed reconstruction still open).
- Friedman's voucher and negative-income-tax positions: **verified** — vouchers in *Capitalism and Freedom* ch. 6; NIT in ch. 12 [5].
- James Mill's endorsement of succession taxation in *Elements of Political Economy*: **unverified** — not located; flag retained in the table.
