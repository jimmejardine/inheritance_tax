# Grandfathering: Existing Trusts, Completed Gifts, and Settled Expectations

**Executive summary.** The transition must draw a line between the past it cannot tax and the future it must: completed pre-commencement transfers stand untaxed, but *distributions* made after commencement are receipts on the recipient's ledger regardless of when the trust that pays them was settled — otherwise existing trust wealth enjoys a fifty-year exemption and the regime is stillborn. This position is legally defensible under both legitimate-expectation doctrine and ECHR A1P1, precisely because it taxes future events, not past ones.

## The problem stated

At commencement there will exist, in the UK alone, trust structures holding very large aggregate wealth (order of hundreds of billions; to verify against HMRC trust statistics), plus completed lifetime gifts, life-insurance arrangements, and family understandings formed under the old law. Three questions arise:

1. **Legal**: what does the state *lawfully* owe to expectations formed under prior law?
2. **Ethical**: what does it *fairly* owe?
3. **Practical**: which grandfathering choices leave a base worth taxing?

The tension is real: maximal respect for settled expectations exempts precisely the dynastic wealth the policy targets; minimal respect invites credible legal challenge and hands opponents their best fairness argument ([`../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md`](../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md), [`../09-objections/fairness.md`](../09-objections/fairness.md)).

## The legal frame

**Legitimate expectation.** In UK public law, no one holds a legitimate expectation that tax law will remain unchanged; Parliament may alter prospective tax treatment freely. The doctrine bites only on *specific assurances* and, more weakly, on *retroactive* imposition — charging a past, completed event. The design principle follows directly: tax only events (receipts, distributions, deaths, exits) occurring after commencement.

**ECHR A1P1.** Article 1 Protocol 1 protects possessions but expressly reserves states' right "to secure the payment of taxes." Strasbourg jurisprudence gives a wide margin of appreciation to tax measures, striking them down only where retrospective, arbitrary, or devoid of reasonable foundation (the line of cases on retrospective tax legislation, e.g. *National & Provincial Building Society v UK*, supports even some retrospection where anti-avoidance justified; to verify citations). A prospective charge on future distributions from existing trusts sits comfortably inside the margin; an attempt to levy on trust *corpus* as of commencement day, without any triggering event, would be the vulnerable variant. Full litigation-risk analysis: [`../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md`](../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md).

**US parallel.** Due-process retroactivity doctrine (*United States v. Carlton*) tolerates even modestly retroactive tax legislation with a rational legislative purpose; prospective taxation of future distributions from pre-existing trusts raises no serious constitutional issue. Constitutional questions specific to the US design are in [legislative-path-us.md](legislative-path-us.md).

## The options

| Option | Treatment of pre-commencement trusts | Base integrity | Legal risk | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A. Full grandfathering | Distributions from trusts settled before commencement are permanently outside the base | Catastrophic: a 50+ year hole; triggers a settlement rush before commencement | Minimal | Reject |
| B. Deemed receipt on distribution | Any distribution *after* commencement is a receipt on the beneficiary's ledger, whenever the trust was settled | Sound: trust wealth is taxed as it reaches human hands | Low: taxes a future event | **Recommend** |
| C. Periodic charge on corpus | Ten-yearly charge on trust assets (extending the existing UK relevant-property regime) at a rate calibrated to the 100% receipt rate | Sound but blunt; taxes wealth no one has received | Moderate: closer to a wealth levy; valuation-heavy | Adopt as backstop only |
| D. Day-one deemed distribution | Entire corpus of existing trusts deemed received by beneficiaries at commencement | Maximal | High: functionally retroactive confiscation; the case opponents want | Reject |

## The recommendation: distributions after commencement are receipts

**Option B is the load-bearing transition rule.** The logic:

- **Completed gifts stand.** An outright gift finished before commencement (subject to the lookback headroom-consumption rule in [announcement-effects.md](announcement-effects.md)) is untouched. The past is the past.
- **But a trust is not a completed transfer to a person.** Wealth in a discretionary trust has left the settlor and reached *no one*. The recipient-based logic of the whole scheme ([`../02-proposal/mechanism.md`](../02-proposal/mechanism.md)) says the taxable moment is *receipt by a human being* — and for trust wealth, that moment is the distribution, which lies in the future. Taxing it is not retrospective in any legal or ethical sense: no beneficiary had possession, and many had no fixed entitlement at all.
- **Without this rule the regime is optional for old money.** Full grandfathering (Option A) would mean the tax binds only wealth that has not yet reached a planner — new fortunes — while every existing dynasty distributes tax-free for two generations. The result inverts the policy's purpose and repeats abolition-era error #4 (incidence inversely correlated with wealth; [`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md)). It would also trigger the largest trust-settlement rush in history during the announcement window.

**The backstop.** Option B alone invites the *accumulation* strategy: never distribute, let the trust compound as a perpetual shadow-owner (companies held, houses occupied under licence, expenses paid). Three supplementary rules answer this, extending machinery the UK already runs:

1. **Benefit-in-kind as distribution.** Occupation of trust property, use of trust assets, or trust settlement of a beneficiary's expenses is a deemed distribution at market value ([`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md)).
2. **Periodic charge retained** (Option C) on undistributed relevant property, at a rate set so that indefinite accumulation is no cheaper than distribution — the existing ten-year-anniversary charge, recalibrated.
3. **Trust-termination and migration events** are deemed distributions ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md)).

## The 'dry trust' endgame

Under this package, a pre-commencement trust faces a closed set of futures: distribute (taxed as receipts against each beneficiary's threshold — note that wide distribution across many beneficiaries is tax-efficient, which is the intended dispersal incentive); accumulate (bleeding periodic charges with no exit); or wind up into charity (exempt, with the anti-cycling rules in [`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md); second-order effects on giving in [`../16-second-order/charitable-giving.md`](../16-second-order/charitable-giving.md)). Over a generation, private dynastic trusts either **run dry into taxed human receipts, convert to philanthropy, or dwindle under the periodic charge**. No cliff, no confiscation event, no day-one seizure to photograph — just a slow, lawful drainage of the trust form's dynastic function. The projected end-state for dynastic structures generally is treated in [`../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md`](../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md).

## Settled expectations honestly weighed

The residual ethical claim is the heir who, under old law, reasonably expected an untaxed inheritance and ordered their life accordingly. Three mitigations are built into the transit:

- **The ladder gives notice.** Under the sequencing in [sequencing.md](sequencing.md), the 100% rate arrives 10–20 years after the direction of travel is public — longer notice than most pension-age or tax-relief reforms have offered.
- **The threshold preserves the ordinary case.** £500k per recipient means the expectation of inheriting a family home or a comfortable sum survives intact; only expectations of *dynastic-scale* receipt are defeated, and the ethical weight of that expectation is exactly what [`../03-case-for/ethical.md`](../03-case-for/ethical.md) contests.
- **The endowment replaces expectation with entitlement.** Every 25-year-old, including the disappointed heir's children, holds a new settled expectation of £50k ([`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md)).

Transition relief beyond this — e.g. honouring pre-commencement *contractual* obligations funded from trusts (maintenance orders, disabled-dependant provision) — belongs in the carve-out architecture ([`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md)), not in blanket grandfathering.

## Summary of the transition boundary

| Event | Before commencement | After commencement |
|---|---|---|
| Outright gift, completed | Untaxed; consumes headroom if within the lookback window ([announcement-effects.md](announcement-effects.md)) | Taxable receipt on the ledger |
| Bequest (death) | Old law applied | Taxable receipt, whenever the will was written |
| Trust settled | Settlement itself untouched | Settlement is a nothing for the ledger — but see next rows |
| Trust distribution | Old law applied | **Receipt on beneficiary's ledger, regardless of settlement date** |
| Benefit-in-kind from trust (occupation, use, expenses paid) | Old law | Deemed distribution at market value |
| Trust ten-year anniversary | Existing relevant-property charge | Recalibrated periodic charge (accumulation backstop) |
| Trust winds up to charity | Old law | Exempt, subject to anti-cycling rules ([`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md)) |
| Emigration of recipient | Old law | Exit tax; 10-year tail ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md)) |

One line to hold in every drafting and litigation argument: **the regime taxes no event that was complete before commencement, and exempts no event that occurs after it.**
