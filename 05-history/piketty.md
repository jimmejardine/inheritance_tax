# Thomas Piketty: *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* (2013) and *Capital and Ideology* (2019)

**Executive summary.** Piketty rebuilt the empirical foundation under inheritance politics — documenting that inherited wealth's economic weight collapsed mid-century and has been returning toward Belle Époque levels — and then, in *Capital and Ideology*, proposed the closest published cousin of this project: steeply progressive inheritance and wealth taxes funding a universal capital grant at 25. This project adopts his empirics and his endowment, but departs from him on rate structure (a single 0/100 kink rather than a progressive schedule) and instrument (inheritance receipts only, no annual wealth tax).

## The empirical contribution

### r > g and the mechanics of patrimonial return

*Capital in the Twenty-First Century* (French 2013; English 2014, trans. Goldhammer) assembled two centuries of tax-record data — much of it built with Atkinson and Saez in the World Top Incomes project ([atkinson.md](atkinson.md)) — around a simple engine: when the net return on capital *r* persistently exceeds the growth rate *g*, existing wealth compounds faster than the economy that hosts it, and *inherited* wealth tends to dominate *saved* wealth [1]. The mid-twentieth-century compression (1914–70s) appears in his data not as capitalism's natural maturation (contra Kuznets) but as the one-off product of wars, depression, inflation and — directly relevant here — confiscatory top tax rates, including the death duties documented in [us-estate-tax.md](us-estate-tax.md) and [uk-estate-duty.md](uk-estate-duty.md). The full theoretical treatment is in [../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md](../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md).

### The inheritance-flow measurements

For this project the load-bearing empirics are Piketty's inheritance series for France (the 2011 *QJE* paper underlying the book's Chapter 11) [2]:

| Measure (France; figures to verify against Piketty 2011) | 19th c. / 1900–10 | ~1950 | ~2010 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Annual flow of inheritances and gifts, % of national income | 20–25% | ~4–5% | ~15%, trending up |
| Share of total wealth attributable to inheritance | ~80–90% | (trough mid-century, ~45–50%) | ~60–70%, projected toward 80–90% by 2050–2100 under central scenarios |

The rhetorical force is the U-shape: the mid-century world in which self-made wealth predominated — the world the post-war generations mistook for capitalism's normal state — was the anomaly. On current trends the twenty-first century reverts to a society of *rentiers* and well-placed heirs; Piketty's coinage "patrimonial capitalism" names the destination [1][2]. This is the master empirical warrant for [../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md](../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md) and [../01-problem/rentier-economy.md](../01-problem/rentier-economy.md).

## The proposal: *Capital and Ideology*

Where *Capital21* offered a "utopian" global wealth tax, *Capital and Ideology* (French 2019; English 2020) is concrete. Its "participatory socialism" package includes [3]:

- **Universal capital endowment ("inheritance for all"):** ~€120,000 to every citizen at age 25 — calibrated as 60% of average adult wealth (~€200,000 in France at writing; figures to verify) [3].
- **Funding: twin progressive taxes** on inheritance and on annual wealth holdings, each with rates rising from ~0.1–5% at average-wealth multiples to **60–90% on the largest fortunes** (schedule to verify) — together yielding on the order of 5% of national income for the endowment [3].
- **Temporary ownership** as the governing philosophy: beyond modest scale, private property is a *social relation held on licence*; large holdings should circulate back to society each generation rather than persist. "Private property becomes temporary rather than permanent" (paraphrase; quote to verify) [3].
- Accompanying measures — employee board representation, "educational justice," reversal of what he documents as the left's capture by graduates ("Brahmin left" vs "merchant right") — that place the tax inside a broader political sociology relevant to [../12-political-adoption/left-parties.md](../12-political-adoption/left-parties.md).

## Reception and criticism

*Capital21* sold over 2.5 million copies (figure to verify) — an unprecedented event for a data-dense economics tome — and moved inheritance from the seminar room to the front page. The serious criticisms, which this project must carry rather than wave away:

| Critique | Content | Status |
|---|---|---|
| **Measurement (income):** Auten & Splinter vs Piketty–Saez–Zucman | Allocating untaxed income differently, Auten–Splinter find much smaller rises in US top income shares [4] | Live dispute; but it concerns *income* series — the *wealth* concentration and inheritance-flow findings are less contested, and French inheritance data are unusually direct |
| **Measurement (wealth):** Smith–Zidar–Zwick | Lower top-wealth estimates from capitalisation refinements [4] | Narrows but does not reverse concentration findings |
| **Housing:** Rognlie | Post-war capital-share rise is mostly housing, not corporate capital [5] | If anything strengthens the *inheritance* case in high-house-price economies — see [../01-problem/housing.md](../01-problem/housing.md) |
| **Growth theory:** Acemoglu–Robinson, Krusell–Smith | r>g is not a "general law"; institutions dominate; the savings assumptions overstate accumulation [5] | Fair against grand-law rhetoric; this project's case rests on the measured inheritance flows, not on r>g as iron law |

The strategic lesson of the reception — one book moved the Overton window a decade — is developed in [../14-communications/overton-strategies.md](../14-communications/overton-strategies.md) and [../13-movement/precedents.md](../13-movement/precedents.md).

## Where this project differs from Piketty

| Dimension | Piketty (2019) | This project ([../02-proposal/README.md](../02-proposal/README.md)) |
|---|---|---|
| Rate structure | Progressive schedule to ~90% | **Binary kink: 0% then 100%** above the lifetime threshold — simpler to administer, impossible to nibble rate-band by rate-band, and rhetorically clean: "no one inherits more than £500k" |
| Instruments | Inheritance tax **plus annual wealth tax** | **Inheritance receipts only** — avoids annual-valuation burden, liquidity strain and the wealth-tax abolition record; accepts slower de-concentration in exchange |
| Base | Progressive on transfer size | Recipient-based, **lifetime-cumulative** receipts ledger (the Mill–Atkinson line: [mill-1848.md](mill-1848.md), [atkinson.md](atkinson.md)) |
| Endowment | ~€120k at 25 | £50k/$60k at 25 — deliberately more modest, sized to the inheritance-tax yield alone ([../02-proposal/endowment.md](../02-proposal/endowment.md)) |
| Philosophy | Temporary ownership; participatory socialism | Narrower: dynastic circulation without the wider programme — by design, to keep the coalition broad ([../06-palatability/coalition.md](../06-palatability/coalition.md)) |

The differences are strategic, not doctrinal: a single-instrument, single-kink design presents a smaller attack surface ([../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md](../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md)) and a clearer story ([../14-communications/naming-and-language.md](../14-communications/naming-and-language.md)).

## What this lends the present proposal

- **The empirical spine.** The inheritance-flow U-curve is the single most important chart in the entire case; every quantitative claim in [../01-problem/](../01-problem/README.md) should trace to Piketty's series or its replications.
- **Contemporary respectability.** A best-selling, Nobel-shortlisted-calibre economist proposing 90% inheritance-tax rates means this project's 100%-above-threshold is an increment, not a leap.
- **The endowment calibration.** €120k at 25 brackets our £50k from above; Ackerman–Alstott's $80k ([ackerman-alstott.md](ackerman-alstott.md)) brackets from the side — the design space is charted.
- **A reception playbook** — and a warning that measurement disputes will be weaponised; pre-emption in [../09-objections/whataboutery.md](../09-objections/whataboutery.md) and [../14-communications/inoculation.md](../14-communications/inoculation.md).

## Verification checklist

- The French inheritance-flow series (20–25% of national income c. 1900; ~4–5% c. 1950; ~15% c. 2010) and the inherited-share-of-wealth series, against Piketty (2011) and the *Capital21* technical appendix.
- The *Capital and Ideology* endowment figure (€120,000), its calibration (60% of ~€200,000 average adult wealth), the 60–90% top-rate schedule, and the ~5%-of-national-income funding envelope, against Ch. 17 and its tables.
- The "temporary ownership"/"private property becomes temporary" formulation — exact wording and location.
- Sales figures for *Capital21* (~2.5m copies is widely reported; source it or drop it).
- The current state of the Auten–Splinter vs PSZ exchange, which has continued past both original papers — check for post-2024 replies before quoting either side as settled.
- Replications of the inheritance-flow U-curve outside France — Atkinson's UK series, Schinke for Germany, Ohlsson–Roine–Waldenström for Sweden (citations to verify) — since the manifesto should not rest the empirical case on one country.
- Piketty's own estimates of what his 90% top rate would raise, for comparison against the revenue arithmetic in [../02-proposal/thresholds.md](../02-proposal/thresholds.md).
- Whether *Capital and Ideology*'s endowment is recipient-lifetime-cumulative on the tax side or transfer-by-transfer — the structural comparison table depends on it.
