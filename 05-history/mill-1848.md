# John Stuart Mill, *Principles of Political Economy* (1848)

**Executive summary.** Mill supplied the single most important structural idea this project uses: cap what any individual may *receive* by gift or inheritance over a lifetime, rather than what any estate may *leave* — thereby preserving the right of bequest while destroying the mechanics of dynasty. The modern proposal in [../02-proposal/mechanism.md](../02-proposal/mechanism.md) is, in its architecture, Millian.

## Context: the classical economist as reformer

Mill published the *Principles* in 1848 — the year of European revolutions — and revised it through seven lifetime editions. His treatment of inheritance sits at the junction of two commitments: a classical-liberal defence of private property as the reward of "exertion and abstinence," and a growing conviction that existing property law protected much that exertion had never earned [1]. The *Principles* famously distinguishes the laws of production (natural) from the laws of distribution (chosen): "The Distribution of Wealth... is a matter of human institution solely. The things once there, mankind, individually or collectively, can do with them as they like" (quote to verify) [1]. Inheritance law is the paradigm case of a distributional institution open to redesign.

## The family lineage: Bentham and James Mill

The recipient-based idea did not begin with Mill. Jeremy Bentham's pamphlet *Supply without Burthen; or Escheat vice Taxation* (written 1795) proposed that the state take, by escheat, estates of persons dying without close relatives, and levy heavily on collateral successions — on the utilitarian ground that a windfall never expected is a loss never felt, making it the least burdensome of all possible taxes [2]. James Mill, John Stuart's father, endorsed heavy taxation of inheritances in his *Elements of Political Economy* (1821) on similar grounds (to verify against the text) [2]. J. S. Mill's contribution was to move from Bentham's *who-is-related* test to a general *how-much-received* test — from limiting succession by kinship to limiting acquisition by quantity.

## The core distinction: bequest is a right; inheritance is not

Mill separates two things that ordinary debate fuses:

| Concept | Mill's position |
|---|---|
| **Bequest** (the owner's power to give at death) | An attribute of ownership; part of the liberty of the proprietor; should be broadly respected |
| **Inheritance** (the heir's claim to receive) | *Not* a natural right; the heir has done nothing; society may limit it freely |

From this follows the celebrated proposal in Book II, Chapter II of the *Principles*: each person should be free to dispose of their whole property, "but not to lavish it in enriching some one individual, beyond a certain maximum, which should be fixed sufficiently high to afford the means of comfortable independence" (quote to verify) [1]. No one is prevented from *giving*; everyone is limited in *getting*. A testator who wishes his wealth to survive him intact must spread it across many recipients — which is precisely the de-concentrating outcome sought.

Mill also grounds the limit in his theory of desert. Property earns its justification "in the fruits of their own labour and abstinence"; it is illegitimate when it guarantees "that some should be born to riches and the vast majority to poverty" (quotes to verify) [1]. Large inherited fortunes are the clearest case of *unearned advantage* — the same category as the "unearned increment" of land value that Mill pursued through his Land Tenure Reform Association in the 1870s [3]. The earned/unearned line, not the rich/poor line, is where Mill draws the moral boundary — the framing this project adopts in [../03-case-for/ethical.md](../03-case-for/ethical.md) and [../03-case-for/meritocratic-legitimacy.md](../03-case-for/meritocratic-legitimacy.md).

## Reception and influence

The proposal was widely read — the *Principles* was *the* economics textbook of the English-speaking world for half a century — but never legislated in Mill's form. Its influence ran through three channels:

- **Liberal fiscal politics.** Mill's legitimation of graduated death duties (he opposed graduated *income* tax as a penalty on saving, but endorsed graduation on inheritance precisely because it taxed the unearned) helped clear the intellectual ground for Harcourt's 1894 Estate Duty — though Harcourt taxed the estate, not the recipient, a structural wrong turn examined in [uk-estate-duty.md](uk-estate-duty.md) [3][4].
- **The accessions-tax tradition.** Economists returned repeatedly to Mill's structure: Eugenio Rignano's generation-skipping scheme (1901), Josiah Wedgwood's *The Economics of Inheritance* (1929), the Meade Report's progressive accessions proposals (1978), Atkinson's lifetime capital receipts tax (see [atkinson.md](atkinson.md)), and the Mirrlees Review (2011) [4]. Ireland's Capital Acquisitions Tax (1976) is the closest living statutory descendant — see [../11-geographies/ireland.md](../11-geographies/ireland.md).
- **The socialist-liberal borderland.** Fabians and New Liberals (Hobhouse's *Liberalism*, 1911) cited Mill's inheritance chapters as proof that limiting inherited wealth was continuous with, not opposed to, the liberal tradition [3].

## Why the modern proposal is Millian

Point-by-point correspondence with [../02-proposal/mechanism.md](../02-proposal/mechanism.md):

| Mill (1848) | This project |
|---|---|
| Limit on lifetime acquisition by gift or inheritance | Recipient-based, lifetime-cumulative ledger of capital receipts |
| Maximum set at "the means of comfortable independence" | £500k / $750k threshold — several multiples of median lifetime savings ([../02-proposal/thresholds.md](../02-proposal/thresholds.md)) |
| Bequest free below the cap; testator may spread wealth widely | 0% below threshold per recipient; wide distribution is untaxed by construction |
| Gifts inter vivos counted, else the cap is trivially avoided | Gift aggregation in [../02-proposal/avoidance.md](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) |
| Earned/unearned distinction as the moral basis | Ethical case in [../03-case-for/ethical.md](../03-case-for/ethical.md) |

The one element Mill lacks is the universal endowment — his cap redistributes by dispersion, not by grant. Fusing Mill's mechanism with Paine's endowment ([paine-1797.md](paine-1797.md)) is precisely the synthesis this project proposes.

## What this lends the present proposal

- **Rhetorical inoculation.** The recipient-based structure defeats the "death tax on the dead" attack at the level of design, not spin: nothing is taken from anyone at death; a living recipient's windfall is capped. See [../14-communications/inoculation.md](../14-communications/inoculation.md).
- **Liberal pedigree.** The idea's author is the canonical philosopher of liberty. Against "this is socialism," the answer is a citation — deployed in [../09-objections/slippery-slope.md](../09-objections/slippery-slope.md) and [../12-political-adoption/centrists-and-liberals.md](../12-political-adoption/centrists-and-liberals.md).
- **A 175-year-old warning.** Mill saw that any receipts cap must count lifetime gifts or die by avoidance — the failure that later destroyed UK Estate Duty ([uk-estate-duty.md](uk-estate-duty.md)).
