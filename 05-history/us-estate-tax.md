# The US Federal Estate Tax, 1797–Present

**Executive summary.** The United States ran a statutory top estate-tax rate of ~77% for thirty-five years in the mid-twentieth century — the strongest existence proof that very high transfer taxation is politically and economically survivable in a large capitalist democracy — yet the tax never collected more than a small share of dynastic wealth, because avoidance channels were left open by design. Its subsequent forty-year dismantling, culminating in today's eight-figure exemption, is the American instance of the erosion pattern in [abolitions.md](abolitions.md).

## The arc at a glance

| Period | Regime | Top rate / exemption |
|---|---|---|
| 1797–1802 | Stamp duty on legacies (naval war with France) | Modest fixed duties; repealed 1802 |
| 1862–1870 | Civil War inheritance tax, then estate elements | Graduated by kinship, up to 5%; repealed 1870 |
| 1898–1902 | Spanish-American War estate tax | Up to 15%; repealed 1902 |
| 1916 | **Permanent federal estate tax** (Revenue Act of 1916) | 10% top, $50,000 exemption |
| 1917–1924 | WWI and post-war escalation | 25% (1917), 40% (1924); gift tax added 1924 |
| 1926 | Mellon-era retrenchment | Cut to 20%; gift tax repealed |
| 1932–1941 | Depression/New Deal escalation | 45% (1932), 60% (1934), 70% (1935), **77% (1941)** |
| 1941–1976 | High plateau | 77% top rate throughout; $60,000 exemption from 1942 |
| 1976 | Tax Reform Act: **unification** | Estate and gift taxes merged into one cumulative schedule; generation-skipping tax added; top 70% |
| 1981 | ERTA (Reagan) | Top rate phased to 50%; unlimited marital deduction; exemption toward $600k |
| 2001 | EGTRRA phase-out | Rate to 45%, exemption rising, full **repeal for 2010 only**, sunset to 2001 law in 2011 |
| 2010–2017 | Restoration at high exemption | 35% then 40% rate; $5m exemption, indexed, spousal portability |
| 2017 | TCJA | Exemption doubled to ~$11.2m per person |
| 2025–present | Current law | $13.99m exemption (2025); the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (July 2025) set $15m from 2026, permanent and inflation-indexed; 40% rate |

Sources for the legislative chronology: [1][2][7][8].

## The temporary duties: war taxes with a moral rider

All three pre-1916 episodes were war-finance measures, repealed once the emergency passed [1]. Their significance is constitutional and rhetorical: the Supreme Court upheld death duties ([Scholey v. Rew, 1875](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/90/331/); [Knowlton v. Moore, 1900](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/178/41/)) as excises on the *transfer*, not direct taxes on property — the doctrinal foundation everything since rests on, and a fact worth holding against the modern litigation threats surveyed in [../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md](../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md) [2].

## 1916 and the escalation

The Revenue Act of 1916 made the estate tax permanent, proximately to fund preparedness as tariff revenues fell, but on intellectual ground prepared by a decade of Roosevelt's advocacy ([roosevelt-1906.md](roosevelt-1906.md)) and the redistributive turn of the Wilson coalition [1][2]. Between 1932 and 1941 Congress drove the top rate from 45% to 77%, explicitly citing concentration of wealth; FDR's 1935 message to Congress argued that "the transmission from generation to generation of vast fortunes by will, inheritance, or gift is not consistent with the ideals and sentiments of the American people" [3]. The 77% top rate then stood essentially untouched under Roosevelt, Truman, Eisenhower, Kennedy, Johnson, Nixon and Ford — three Republican and four Democratic presidencies — until 1976.

## Mid-century operation: what it collected, and what escaped

The high plateau's record is double-edged and must be reported honestly.

- **Yield was real but modest**: federal estate and gift taxes peaked at 9.7% of federal receipts ($379m) in fiscal 1936, then settled to on the order of 1–2% of federal receipts through the 1940s–1960s [1][2].
- **Effective rates fell far below 77%.** The marital deduction (1948), charitable deduction, life-insurance structuring, valuation discounts, and above all **generation-skipping trusts** — fortunes parked in trust for grandchildren, taxed once per two generations or less — meant the largest fortunes paid effective rates a fraction of the statutory schedule [2][4].
- The canonical verdict is George Cooper's 1979 Brookings study, *A Voluntary Tax? New Perspectives on Sophisticated Estate Tax Avoidance*, which concluded that the tax "serves no purpose other than to give reassurance to the millions of unwealthy that entrenched wealth is being attacked" — in practice, a voluntary tax for the well-advised [4].

Distributionally, the high-rate decades coincided with the great mid-century compression of US wealth concentration — the top 0.1% wealth share falling from a peak near 25% in 1929 to about 7% by 1978 on the Saez–Zucman series (the estate tax was one cause among several, alongside depression, war, and income-tax progressivity) [5]. Piketty treats this compression as the central natural experiment; see [piketty.md](piketty.md).

## The dismantling, 1976–present

The 1976 unification was technically sound (it closed the lifetime-gift gap Mill had identified in 1848 — [mill-1848.md](mill-1848.md)) but arrived just as the political tide turned. The 1981 ERTA cuts, the 2001 EGTRRA phase-out with its farcical one-year repeal — 2010, the year heirs of billionaires dying that year (George Steinbrenner most famously) owed nothing, while the law was scheduled to snap back in 2011 — the 2017 exemption doubling and the 2025 act's permanent $15m exemption have left a tax that now touches roughly 0.1% of decedents (0.07% of 2019 deaths produced a taxable return; about 0.14% projected for 2023) and raises under 1% of federal revenue ($32bn, or ~0.8%, in FY2024) [7][9]. The "death tax" messaging campaign that drove the 2001 act is the best-documented framing war in tax history and is dissected in [../06-palatability/framing.md](../06-palatability/framing.md) and [../10-opposition/media-narratives.md](../10-opposition/media-narratives.md) [6].

## The companion scandal: step-up in basis

Throughout the whole arc, IRC §1014 has granted heirs a **stepped-up basis**: unrealised capital gains held until death escape income tax entirely, for everyone, forever. As the estate tax shrank, the combined regime became actively *pro*-dynastic — the "buy, borrow, die" strategy lets appreciated fortunes pass with neither capital-gains nor estate tax [2][5]. Canada's deemed-disposition alternative ([abolitions.md](abolitions.md)) taxes exactly what §1014 forgives. Any US implementation of this project's proposal must repeal step-up as a precondition; see [../15-transition/legislative-path-us.md](../15-transition/legislative-path-us.md).

## What the high-rate decades prove — and don't

**Prove:** that a ~77% statutory top rate on estates can persist for a generation in the world's largest capitalist economy, under both parties, without capital strike, growth collapse, or constitutional crisis — the core feasibility datum against the objections in [../04-case-against/capital-flight.md](../04-case-against/capital-flight.md) and [../09-objections/economic.md](../09-objections/economic.md).

**Don't prove:** that such rates were ever *effectively* collected at the top. The mid-century tax was estate-based, trust-permeable, and gift-leaky until 1976; its statutory ferocity coexisted with structural porousness. The correct inference is not "high rates work as legislated" but "high rates are survivable, and their leaks are identifiable and closable" — which is precisely the task of [../02-proposal/avoidance.md](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) and [../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md).

## What this lends the present proposal

- **Feasibility precedent** for rates near 100% at the margin, sustained across decades and parties.
- **A complete catalogue of failure modes** — estate-based structure, trust permeability, unindexed exemptions turned over-indexed, repeal-by-exemption — each answered point-by-point in [../02-proposal/README.md](../02-proposal/README.md).
- **The step-up lesson:** transfer taxation cannot be designed in isolation from income-tax basis rules.
- **A political warning:** the tax died not by argument but by framing and erosion; the counter-strategy is the endowment pairing ([../02-proposal/endowment.md](../02-proposal/endowment.md)) and the communications programme in [../14-communications/naming-and-language.md](../14-communications/naming-and-language.md).
