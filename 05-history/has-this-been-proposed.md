# Has This Exact Proposal Been Made Before?

**Executive summary.** Yes — more nearly than this project previously acknowledged. The exact mechanism (0% below a per-recipient lifetime quota, 100% above) was proposed by the philosopher D.W. Haslett in 1997, and a structurally identical package — recipient-based lifetime accessions cap, 100% marginal rate above it, revenues hypothecated to a universal citizen's capital grant — was published by Ingrid Robeyns in 2024, differing from this proposal only in its numbers (~$100k cap; grant at adulthood rather than 25). What no prior proposal, advisory note, or operating regime supplies is the *calibration and survival engineering*: a threshold set generously at ~P90 of lifetime receipts, indexed to the higher of wage and house-price growth so it cannot drift downward, and an endowment sized and timed to function as the tax's political bodyguard. The honest claim is therefore not novelty but pedigree: every load-bearing component has named proponents from Paine to a 2024 peer-reviewed twin, and this project's contribution is the assembly, the calibration, and the transition and entrenchment design.

## Findings

- **The mechanism has named academic ancestors.** Haslett (1986) argued large gifts and bequests "should be made illegal" with spousal, dependent, and charitable exceptions; by 1997 he had restated this as **a lifetime quota of inheritances and gifts per recipient with 100% taxation above the cap** — explicitly contrasting himself with Mill, who wanted only progressive taxation above his quota [1][2][3].
- **The full package has a 2024 twin.** Robeyns' "accessions cap" (*Politics, Philosophy & Economics*, 2024) is recipient-based, lifetime-cumulative, 0/100 at the cap, and hypothecates the revenue to "a citizen's stake — a capital grant endowed to all citizens when they reach adulthood." Her ballpark cap is $100,000 (noting 90% of Americans inherit under $60k) [3].
- **The legal literature got close in 1990.** Ascher's "Curtailing Inherited Wealth" (*Michigan Law Review*) proposed selling all property at death to the state above six exemptions — including a universal $250,000 exemption per decedent and charity capped at 20% — but on a *decedent* basis, not a per-recipient lifetime ledger [4].
- **The 100% rate has an older pedigree than the repo previously recorded**: Rignano's scheme (1901–24) reaches 100% by *generational age of wealth* (⅓ at first descent, ⅔ at second, all at third) and was engaged by Fisher, Dalton, Stamp, Pigou, and the Colwyn Committee's minority [5][6][7]; Hugh Dalton separately held that "above a specified amount, one hundred per cent of the inheritance or gift ought to be deducted" — the closest pre-war statement of the cap — but as Chancellor never acted on it [7].
- **Confiscation above a threshold has even been *implemented*** — once, badly: Soviet Russia's decree of 27 April 1918 abolished inheritance above a ~10,000-ruble/household-property exemption, and was reversed within four years (specifics verified only at secondary level — see honest limits) [8]. Huey Long's Share Our Wealth (1934) demanded a $5m inheritance cap and 100% capital levy on fortunes above $100m, *paired with a universal $5,000 "Household Estate"* — a mass-movement, estate-side cousin of the tax-plus-endowment pairing [9][10].
- **The closest full-package cousins remain Atkinson (2015) and the French CAE Note 69 (2021)** — the latter now pinned precisely: lifetime-receipts base, exemption-closing worth €12–19bn, and a "garantie en capital pour tous" of up to €40,000 paid at 18, funded from the additional revenue [11][12]. Piketty (2019) is closer on rate (90%) but further on architecture (no specified lifetime-recipient ledger; relies on a paired annual wealth tax) [13].
- **Reception evidence is sobering and instructive**: Ecuador's 2015 bill (top rate 77.5% for indirect heirs — well short of 100%) triggered mass protests and was withdrawn within ten days [14][15]; Abi Wilkinson's 2017 Guardian case for a 100% inheritance tax drew furious cross-spectrum pushback [16]. Both led with the tax, not the benefit — the framing error the whole of [`../06-palatability/`](../06-palatability/README.md) exists to avoid.
- **The mechanism is now live in a G7 legislature's opposition programme**: La France Insoumise's *héritage maximal* — €12m (100× median net wealth), 100% above, on a lifetime-receipts basis, funding a youth guarantee [19] (§3; [`../11-geographies/france.md`](../11-geographies/france.md)).

## 1. The exact mechanism: Haslett and Robeyns

**D.W. Haslett** ("Is Inheritance Justified?", *Philosophy & Public Affairs* 15(2), 1986 [1]) proposed that large gifts and bequests be made illegal, with estates sold on the open market at death (named parties holding first rights to buy at appraised value; family businesses aided by favourable long-term state loans), subject to three exceptions: unlimited spousal transfer; support for dependents ceasing at "a certain age, say twenty-one"; and unlimited charitable giving. In his 1994 book *Capitalism with Morality* and the 1997 restatement [2], the design matured into **a per-recipient lifetime quota with 100% taxation above it** — the mechanism of this proposal, a decade before this project's other modern sources. Haslett never specified the quota's level and paired no universal grant; his argument was framed, notably, as a defence of *capitalism's own values* — the same premise as [`../00-capitalism/`](../00-capitalism/README.md).

**Ingrid Robeyns** ("Limiting lifetime inheritances and gifts," *Politics, Philosophy & Economics*, 2024 [3]) states four components: the tax is "payable by the recipients"; it is "a combined lifetime tax on inheritance and gifts"; there is "a 100% marginal tax rate above the cap"; and revenues fund "a citizen's stake — a capital grant endowed to all citizens when they reach adulthood." This is this project's architecture, independently derived in the philosophical literature, with a much lower illustrative cap (~$100,000). The differences that remain are calibration (P90-generous vs deliberately tight), the grant's timing (25 vs adulthood), and everything in the survival column — indexation, entrenchment, transition engineering.

## 2. Near misses in the literature

- **Ascher (1990)** [4]: 100% escheat above six exemptions, including a universal $250,000 per-*decedent* exemption — generous, but donor-side, so a single heir of four decedents could take $1m untouched while four heirs of one decedent split $250k. The per-recipient lifetime ledger is what separates that design from this one. His epigram belongs in the debate toolkit: "All men are created equal, except the children of the wealthy."
- **Rignano (1901–24)** [5][6]: the rate escalates with the *age of the wealth* — his own schedule ⅓/⅔/1 across three descents — rather than with the amount received. Endorsed in variants by Dalton and the Colwyn minority; wounded by Stamp's administrative critique and Wedgwood's tracing objections; never legislated anywhere. Halliday's 2018 book is its modern revival ([`../13-movement/outreach-academics.md`](../13-movement/outreach-academics.md)).
- **Dalton's aside** [7]: alongside his Rignano advocacy, Dalton held that above a specified maximum, 100% of an inheritance or gift should be taken — then, as the Chancellor best placed in history to enact it (1945–47), concluded it was "politically very difficult," deferred it, and lost office: the clearest historical warning that intellectual assent without movement infrastructure achieves nothing ([`../13-movement/`](../13-movement/README.md)).

## 3. Confiscation attempted in the world

- **Huey Long, Share Our Wealth (1934)** [9][10]: cap fortunes (initially $50m, later $5–8m), cap annual income at $1m, **cap inheritance at $5m per person**, levy 100% above $100m — and pay every family a $5,000 Household Estate plus a guaranteed minimum income. Millions joined; the movement died with Long in 1935, but it pressured FDR toward the 1935 Wealth Tax Act. It is the one *mass-political* precedent for pairing confiscatory caps with a universal capital grant — estate-side and demagogic, but proof the pairing can mobilise.
- **Soviet decree, 27 April 1918** [8]: inheritance abolished above a modest exemption; restored by the 1922 Civil Code. The only implementation of "confiscation above a floor" on record — under revolutionary expropriation, without a recipient ledger, and reversed within four years. Useful mainly to opponents; the distinction (this proposal universalises private capital rather than abolishing it) is drawn in [`../09-objections/slippery-slope.md`](../09-objections/slippery-slope.md).
- **French Revolutionary succession law (1790–94)** [17]: primogeniture abolished; the law of 17 Nivôse An II imposed equal division among all children regardless of will, retroactive to 1789, limiting the testator's free portion to one-tenth. An attack on dynastic transmission by *mandatory dispersal* rather than taxation — the state took nothing — and the ancestor of the Code civil's forced heirship. Evidence that democratic revolutions treat inheritance law as constitutional-order material ([`../03-case-for/democratic-renewal.md`](../03-case-for/democratic-renewal.md)).
- **Ecuador (June 2015)** [14][15]: Correa's bill — progressive to 47.5% for direct heirs and 77.5% for indirect, above a lowered ~$35,400 threshold — met protests reaching ~400,000 people and 72% disapproval, and was withdrawn within ten days (enacted only in weakened form in 2016). The lesson is not that recipient-based inheritance taxation is impossible; it is that a *tax-first, benefit-absent* launch in a low-trust polity is how it fails ([`../06-palatability/case-studies.md`](../06-palatability/case-studies.md)).
- **La France Insoumise (2022–present)** [19]: the one major-party live proposal of the mechanism — *L'Avenir en commun* commits, verbatim, to "créer un héritage maximal de 12 millions d'euros (soit 100 fois le patrimoine net médian)" — a €12m maximum inheritance, 100% above, affecting ~0.01% of heirs — alongside a separate plank taxing the largest successions on **all gifts and inheritances received over the whole of life** (a lifetime-receipts basis), with Mélenchon framing the cap as funding a monthly youth *garantie d'autonomie*. A G7-country party with a fifth of the legislature's left bloc is programmatically committed to cap + lifetime receipts + youth funding; the enforcement drafting is unspecified, and the packaging (cap on a class enemy, benefit as welfare flow rather than universal capital) differs instructively from this project's offer-first design ([`../11-geographies/france.md`](../11-geographies/france.md)).

## 4. The full-package cousins

The closest published relatives, ranked by architecture (the full component matrix is in §5):

1. **Atkinson (2015)** — recipient-based, lifetime-cumulative, gift-integrated, tax-funds-minimum-inheritance loop; progressive schedule rather than a kink ([atkinson.md](atkinson.md)).
2. **Robeyns (2024)** — the mechanism twin (§1), lower cap, thinner transition/entrenchment design [3].
3. **CAE Note 69 (2021)** — Dherbécourt, Fack, Landais & Stantcheva for France's official economic advisory council: tax "the entirety of sums received by a person over their lifetime," close the exemptions (assurance-vie, business assets, dismembered gifts; €12–19bn), and fund a **capital guarantee for all of up to €40,000 at 18** [11][12]. Keeps progressive rates; no generous universal threshold; no entrenchment design — but it is the nearest any *government-commissioned* body has come to this architecture.
4. **Piketty (2019)** — 90% top rates and a €120k endowment at 25, but funded jointly with an annual wealth tax and without a specified recipient-lifetime ledger ([piketty.md](piketty.md)) [13].
5. **Resolution Foundation (2018) / Stuart White** — lifetime receipts tax (£125k allowance, 20%/30%) funding a £10k citizen's inheritance at 25: the same loop at one-fifth scale ([`../11-geographies/uk.md`](../11-geographies/uk.md)).
6. **Batchelder (2020)** — inheritances taxed as income above a lifetime exemption, with a **$500,000 variant matching this project's threshold exactly**; no endowment ([`../15-transition/legislative-path-us.md`](../15-transition/legislative-path-us.md)).
7. **Ireland's CAT** — the operating statutory proof of the recipient-lifetime machinery, at 33% with tiered thresholds and no indexation ([`../11-geographies/ireland.md`](../11-geographies/ireland.md)).

## 6. What is actually new here — the honest claim

Reading the matrix column-wise: **C1–C3, C5, and C7 all have prior occupants**, and the C1+C2+C3 combination — the confiscatory rate on a recipient-lifetime ledger — exists in Haslett (1997) and Robeyns (2024). Two things have no prior occupant:

1. **C4 as calibration**: no earlier confiscatory proposal sets its cap *generously* — at ~P90 of lifetime receipts, sized to turn 90% of heirs into beneficiaries or bystanders. Haslett left the quota unstated; Robeyns deliberately sets it tight (~$100k); Ascher's $250k is donor-side. The generous cap is not timidity — it is the coalition design of [`../06-palatability/coalition.md`](../06-palatability/coalition.md) expressed as a parameter.
2. **The entire C6 column**: no prior proposal, advisory note, or statute indexes its threshold against downward drift (max of wages and house prices), or theorises the endowment as the tax's political bodyguard, or engineers the transition against the abolition record ([abolitions.md](abolitions.md)). Ireland's discretionary threshold swings are the standing demonstration of what its absence costs.

The right framing for the manifesto is therefore the opposite of a novelty claim: *this has been proposed, repeatedly, for two hundred years, by philosophers, economists, a US senator with millions of followers, France's official economic advisers, and a 2024 peer-reviewed twin — what has never been built is the version engineered to survive.* Pedigree is the asset; survival engineering is the contribution.

## Honest limits

- Haslett's 1986 small-gift allowance figure and his 1994 quota level are unverified; his 1997 mechanism is characterised via Robeyns' peer-reviewed account [3] and the Springer chapter record [2].
- Fisher's 1919 AEA address is cited from consistent secondary transcriptions; the primary text was not fetched (JSTOR-gated) — degree of endorsement vs sympathetic citation of Rignano remains soft [18].
- The Soviet decree's text is quoted from a source located by search whose page could not be fetched directly; the 1922 restoration terms are unverified.
- Piketty's C2/C3 cells are genuinely unresolved: whether *Capital and Ideology*'s inheritance tax is assessed on the heir's cumulative receipts is not specified in reachable sources.
- Wedgwood's own positive programme (beyond his scepticism and Dalton-variant) is not fully characterised; the French law of 7 March 1793 and any Saint-Just fragments were not verified and are not cited.
- Lewis Goodall's reported 2025 100% advocacy is secondary-only (Dale's rebuttal); his original statement was not located [16].
