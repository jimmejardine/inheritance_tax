# UK Death Duties: Estate Duty (1894) to Inheritance Tax (1986–)

**Executive summary.** Britain ran the West's most aggressive death duties — top rates around 80% for three post-war decades — and they had real distributional teeth: they helped break up the great landed estates in a way no other peacetime policy ever has. But the duty taxed only what was left at death, never properly integrated lifetime gifts, and accreted reliefs until its successor now touches under 5% of estates — the fullest domestic illustration of the self-destruction pattern in [abolitions.md](abolitions.md).

## 1894: Harcourt's Estate Duty

Chancellor Sir William Harcourt's Finance Act 1894 consolidated a century's patchwork (probate, account, legacy and succession duties) into a single graduated **Estate Duty** on the principal value of all property passing at death, with graduated rates from 1% (estates over £100) to 8% (estates over £1 million) [1][7]. The measure provoked aristocratic fury and Rosebery's misgivings, but passed; Harcourt's earlier remark "We are all socialists now" ([House of Commons, 11 August 1887](https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/William_Harcourt), on the Labourers' Allotments Bill) captured the sense that graduated taxation of wealth had entered the mainstream of *Liberal*, not socialist, politics [1]. Structurally, however, Harcourt taxed the **estate** — the corpse's last act — rather than the recipient, declining the Millian architecture ([mill-1848.md](mill-1848.md)) that his own tradition had produced. Every subsequent pathology follows from that choice plus one other: gifts made sufficiently long before death (initially three months, eventually seven years) escaped entirely.

## Escalation: two wars and a social democracy

| Date | Top rate | Context |
|---|---|---|
| 1894 | 8% | Harcourt's consolidation |
| 1919 | 40% (over £2m) | Post-WWI debt |
| 1930 | 50% (over £2m) | Snowden |
| 1939–46 | 60% (1939), 65% (1940), 75% (1946) | War finance |
| 1949 | **80%** | Cripps; over £1m |
| 1969 | **85% marginal** over £750,000 (total duty capped at 80% of the estate) | Jenkins |
| 1975 | CTT top 75% on death transfers | Healey; lifetime gifts integrated |
| 1986– | IHT flat 40% | Lawson; lifetime gifts largely exempt again |

Rate chronology: [7][8].

At its peak the duty raised a meaningful share of revenue — in the interwar years death duties contributed on the order of 8–10% of central government tax receipts (unverified; a King's College London working paper on the death duties 1859–1930 reports their share of revenue rising from 16% to 24% between 1918 and 1930, though on a different — apparently Inland-Revenue — base) [2] — a far higher fiscal weight than any transfer tax today.

## Real distributional consequences: the break-up of the estates

The strongest evidence that death duties *can* decompose dynastic wealth is British. Estate Duty, compounding with agricultural depression and wartime losses of heirs, drove the great sell-off of aristocratic land: contemporaries estimated that between 1918 and 1922 alone something like a quarter of England changed hands — a land transfer contemporaries compared to the dissolution of the monasteries. The figure originates with a December 1921 *Estates Gazette* estimate and is endorsed by Cannadine ("some six to eight million acres, one-quarter of the land of England, was sold by gentry and grandees"), but Beckett and Turner's re-examination of the sales data in the *Agricultural History Review* finds "much less than 25 per cent" — nearer 6.5% for 1918–21 — so the claim should be used with that caveat [3][9]. The twentieth century saw over a thousand country houses demolished; the V&A's 1974 exhibition *The Destruction of the Country House* memorialised the toll [3]. The National Trust's Country Houses Scheme (1937) and the acceptance-in-lieu system arose precisely to manage the flow of houses and art surrendered against duty [3]. The top 1%'s share of UK personal wealth fell from about 70% in 1914 to roughly 16% by 1980 (Alvaredo–Atkinson–Morelli series, building on Atkinson–Harrison), with death duties one identifiable cause among several [4].

This record matters doubly for this project: it proves transfer taxation at scale is not merely symbolic ([../03-case-for/economic-efficiency.md](../03-case-for/economic-efficiency.md)), and it supplies the opposition's most emotive imagery — the lost country house — whose modern deployment is anticipated in [../10-opposition/media-narratives.md](../10-opposition/media-narratives.md).

## The avoidance culture

Because Estate Duty never reached lifetime gifts made outside the survivorship window, and never looked through offshore structures, avoidance became a national professional industry:

- **The gift with strings.** Give the estate to the heir, survive seven years, keep living in the house — a pattern only partly checked by "reservation of benefit" rules.
- **Discretionary trusts and offshore settlements.** The Vestey family's Paris trust (settled December 1921) sheltered one of Britain's largest fortunes from direct taxation for two generations, surviving legal challenge into the 1980s (*Vestey v Inland Revenue Commissioners*, decided by the House of Lords in 1979) and becoming the byword for immovable avoidance [5].
- **Agricultural and business assets** attracted growing reliefs, capitalising into asset prices.

The period's epitaph is the quip attributed to Roy Jenkins in the March 1986 Commons Budget debate — inheritance tax is, "broadly speaking, a voluntary levy paid by those who distrust their heirs more than they dislike the Inland Revenue" [5] — aimed at the rebranded IHT, but true of the whole death-only lineage. Note the transatlantic rhyme with Cooper's "voluntary tax" verdict on the US regime ([us-estate-tax.md](us-estate-tax.md)) — the same structural leak, the same epitaph.

## CTT, IHT, and the relief-creep endgame

Labour's **Capital Transfer Tax (1975)** was the honest repair: a cumulative tax on lifetime gifts *and* death transfers — the closest Britain has come to the integrated design this project requires. It lasted eleven years. Lawson's 1986 reform renamed it **Inheritance Tax** and re-exempted lifetime gifts (as "potentially exempt transfers" surviving seven years), restoring the old leak in a single stroke [1][6]. Since then the pattern has been relief-creep and threshold politics:

- 100% Business and Agricultural Property Relief from 1992, turning farmland and unlisted business assets into inheritance-tax shelters and distorting asset markets (AIM portfolios, farmland prices) [6].
- Nil-rate band frozen at £325,000 since 2009; the 2017 residence nil-rate band added a distortionary house-specific allowance rather than indexation.
- Result: 4.39% of UK deaths in 2021–22 resulted in an IHT charge (27,800 estates) [10]; effective rates on the very largest estates are *lower* than on moderately large ones, because reliefs scale with sophistication [6].
- The 2024 Autumn Budget's £1m allowance for combined APR/BPR at 100% relief (50% thereafter), effective 6 April 2026, is the first counter-move in decades and has generated the farm-protest politics anticipated by the historical pattern [11].

## The lesson

The UK arc is the cleanest demonstration of a compound theorem: **high rates without gift integration self-destruct through avoidance; and any transfer tax without automatic indexation and relief discipline self-destructs through legitimacy loss** — first it fails to catch the top, then it drifts onto the middle, then it is hollowed or killed. Each clause maps to a numbered failure mode in [abolitions.md](abolitions.md) and to a design response in [../02-proposal/mechanism.md](../02-proposal/mechanism.md) (lifetime cumulation), [../02-proposal/thresholds.md](../02-proposal/thresholds.md) (max-of-two indexation) and [../02-proposal/carveouts.md](../02-proposal/carveouts.md) (principled deferral instead of open-ended relief).

## What this lends the present proposal

- **Proof of distributional force.** Britain's landed dynasties were actually broken up, in significant part by death duties — the historical answer to "these taxes never touch the truly rich."
- **The gift-integration imperative.** Ninety years of Estate Duty and forty of IHT show that a death-only tax is a tax on the unadvised; CTT's eleven-year life shows integration is legislatively achievable and politically reversible. Entrenchment strategy: [../15-transition/sequencing.md](../15-transition/sequencing.md) and [../15-transition/legislative-path-uk.md](../15-transition/legislative-path-uk.md).
- **Relief-creep as the death channel.** BPR/APR is the live domestic case study for the carve-out discipline in [../02-proposal/carveouts.md](../02-proposal/carveouts.md).
- **The UK as first-adopter candidate.** Deep administrative experience, existing IHT machinery, centralised fiscal state — assessed in [../11-geographies/uk.md](../11-geographies/uk.md).
