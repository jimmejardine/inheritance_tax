# Polling: What the Public Actually Thinks

**Executive summary.** Inheritance tax is consistently among the least popular taxes in the UK, US, and Germany — yet the experimental literature shows it is also the tax where opinion is *most movable* by simple information and framing treatments. The unpopularity is real but shallow: it rests on misperception of who pays, and it inverts when thresholds are explained, when the tax is framed per-recipient, and above all when it is paired with a visible universal benefit.

## Key findings

- Every major UK tracker (YouGov, Ipsos) has repeatedly found inheritance tax rated the "most unfair" or least popular major tax, ahead of income tax, VAT, and council tax (pattern to verify against current trackers) [1][2].
- US polling in the repeal era (Gallup, Pew, NPR/Kaiser/Harvard) found majorities — including majorities of low-income respondents — favouring estate-tax repeal, despite fewer than 2% of estates then paying it (to verify) [3][4].
- The unpopularity coexists with mass misperception: large majorities substantially overestimate the share of estates that pay, and underestimate the exemption threshold (to verify) [4][5].
- **The estate tax is the tax where information moves opinion most.** Kuziemko, Norton, Saez & Stantcheva (2015) found that a brief information treatment on who actually pays roughly tripled support for expanding the estate tax — by far the largest treatment effect in their experiment, dwarfing effects on income-tax or minimum-wage preferences (magnitudes to verify) [5].
- Source-of-wealth matters: Fisman, Gladstone, Kuziemko & Naidu (2020) found Americans favour markedly higher taxes on inherited wealth than on identical wealth that was earned or saved, once the source is made explicit [6].
- Bastani & Waldenström (2021), in Sweden — a country that *abolished* its inheritance tax — found that informing respondents about the prevalence of inherited wealth and its role in inequality of opportunity raised support for inheritance taxation substantially (to verify) [7].
- Endowment-side polling is favourable: Resolution Foundation/Demos found majority support for a "citizen's inheritance" at 25 when described concretely (see [`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md)) [8].
- **Nobody has seriously polled a 100% marginal rate above a generous threshold.** This is the single largest evidence gap for this project.

## 1. The baseline: an unpopularity that is real, broad, and cross-national

| Country | Typical finding | Houses / sources |
|---|---|---|
| UK | IHT routinely ranked the most unfair tax; pluralities favour abolition even among voters who will never pay it | YouGov fairness trackers; Ipsos; Fairer Share/Tax Policy Associates commissioned polls (to verify) [1][2] |
| US | Majorities backed estate-tax repeal c. 1998–2006, including ~half of respondents in the bottom income tercile | Gallup; NPR/Kaiser/Harvard 2003; Pew (to verify) [3][4] |
| Germany | Erbschaftsteuer persistently among the least popular taxes despite low effective incidence; strong "family property" objection | Allensbach, Forsa series (patterns to verify) [9] |
| Sweden | Abolished 2004 with cross-bloc support and little public defence — see [`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md) | Bastani & Waldenström retrospective work [7] |

The cross-national consistency matters: this is not a quirk of one media ecosystem. Whatever explains it must be psychological and structural, not merely propagandistic — which is the subject of [framing.md](framing.md).

## 2. The three paradoxes

**Paradox 1: People oppose a tax they will never pay.** Fewer than ~4–5% of UK estates currently pay IHT (to verify against HMRC statistics), and low-single-digit percentages of *recipients* would be affected by this project's £500k threshold ([`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md)). Yet opposition runs at 50–70%. Survey work consistently finds respondents believe the tax reaches far further down the wealth distribution than it does (to verify) [4][5].

**Paradox 2: The same people support the tax's *purpose*.** Majorities in the same populations say wealth inequality is too high, that the rich should pay more, and — critically — that people should get ahead by effort rather than birth. Fisman et al. show that when wealth is explicitly labelled as inherited, punitive tax preferences emerge [6]. The opposition is to the *frame* ("tax on death, on family, on Dad's house"), not to the *function* (breaking dynastic transmission).

**Paradox 3: "Keep it in the family" beats abstract inequality.** When the question pits a concrete family (mine) against a statistical inequality (everyone's), family wins. This is the finding the citizen's endowment is engineered to reverse: it gives every family a concrete stake on the *receiving* side. See [`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md) and [framing.md](framing.md).

## 3. The experimental evidence: opinion is soft where it looks hard

| Study | Treatment | Effect on estate/inheritance-tax support |
|---|---|---|
| Kuziemko, Norton, Saez, Stantcheva (2015, AER) | Information on how few estates pay | Largest effect in the study; support for expansion roughly tripled from a low base (to verify) [5] |
| Fisman, Gladstone, Kuziemko, Naidu (2020, J. Pub. Econ.) | Vary the *source* of wealth in vignettes | Respondents tax inherited wealth significantly more heavily than earned wealth [6] |
| Bastani & Waldenström (2021, Economica) | Information on inherited wealth's prevalence and link to opportunity | Substantial increase in Swedish support for inheritance taxation (to verify) [7] |
| Stantcheva (2021, QJE, "Understanding Tax Policy") | Open-ended reasoning elicitation | Estate-tax attitudes driven by fairness reasoning and misperceived incidence more than self-interest [10] |
| German survey experiments (e.g. Gross, Lorek & Richter, 2017) | Recipient- vs estate-based framing | Recipient framing and explicit thresholds raise stated support (to verify) [11] |

Two implications. First, the movement's core communications task is *information delivery*, not value change — a far easier task (see [`../14-communications/narratives.md`](../14-communications/narratives.md)). Second, the effect sizes are large enough that opponents know it too: their strategy depends on keeping incidence vague and salience low until a repeal window opens ([`../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md`](../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md)).

## 4. Polling on the benefit side: endowments and baby bonds

- Resolution Foundation (2018, *A New Generational Contract*) and follow-up work found majority support for a citizen's inheritance at 25, rising when the funding source was named (to verify) [8].
- IPPR's Commission on Economic Justice (2018) reported majorities for a Citizens' Wealth Fund paying universal dividends (to verify) [8].
- US baby-bond polling (post-2018, following the Booker proposal and Hamilton–Darity work) finds net-positive but partisan-split support (to verify) [12].
- The Alaska Permanent Fund Dividend is the strongest revealed-preference evidence: universal cash from a common asset is, once established, politically untouchable across party lines.

The design lesson is already embedded in [`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md): lead with the endowment. The polling lesson here is narrower — *no published survey has tested the full package* (100% above £500k per recipient + £50k at 25) as a single instrument.

## 5. What we still do not know

1. **100%-rate polling does not meaningfully exist.** Published instruments test rate rises within conventional ranges (40% → 50–60%) or abolition. Whether a *binary* 0/100 design with a generous threshold polls better or worse than a high progressive rate is unknown — and plausibly better, since the 0% side ("first £500k completely tax-free, guaranteed") is a stronger promise than any current regime makes.
2. **Per-recipient lifetime framing** has thin direct evidence outside a handful of European survey experiments [11].
3. **Package effects.** Does pairing with the endowment move opposition, or only intensity? Does hypothecation language ("your £50k is funded by...") outperform generic "public services"?
4. **Durability.** Information effects in Kuziemko et al. partially decayed in follow-ups (to verify) [5]. One-shot persuasion is not the same as a stable coalition — which is why [`../13-movement/theory-of-change.md`](../13-movement/theory-of-change.md) matters more than any single poll.
5. **Salience risk.** Raising the issue's salience historically helped *opponents* (see [case-studies.md](case-studies.md)). The conditions under which salience helps reformers are untested.
