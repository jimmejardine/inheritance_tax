# Polling: What the Public Actually Thinks

**Executive summary.** Inheritance tax is consistently among the least popular taxes in the UK, US, and Germany — yet the experimental literature shows it is also the tax where opinion is *most movable* by simple information and framing treatments. The unpopularity is real but shallow: it rests on misperception of who pays, and it inverts when thresholds are explained, when the tax is framed per-recipient, and above all when it is paired with a visible universal benefit.

## Key findings

- Every major UK tracker (YouGov, Ipsos) has repeatedly found inheritance tax rated the "most unfair" major tax: YouGov's biannual fairness tracker (2019–2026) consistently finds majorities calling it unfair (61% in September 2023), its 2015 comparison found it the most unfair of eleven taxes across supporters of all parties, and Ipsos (June 2023) found it the most unfair of the taxes it tested (43% unfair vs 23% fair) [1][2].
- US polling in the repeal era (Gallup, NPR/Kaiser/Harvard) found large majorities — including large majorities of low-income respondents — favouring estate-tax repeal: 60% favoured eliminating the "death tax" (54% when called the "estate tax") in the 2003 NPR/Kaiser/Kennedy survey, and a 2001 poll found 76% support for repeal among those with incomes under $40,000 — despite only around the wealthiest 2% of decedents then paying it [3][4].
- The unpopularity coexists with mass misperception: in the 2003 NPR/Kaiser/Kennedy survey, 49% believed "most people have to pay" the estate tax and a further 18% did not know; in the UK, 31% of Britons expect inheritance tax to apply to the assets they leave — several times the actual share of estates that pay [1][4][5].
- **The estate tax is the tax where information moves opinion most.** In Kuziemko, Norton, Saez & Stantcheva (2015), the information treatment (covering inequality, top tax rates, and how few estates pay the estate tax) roughly tripled support for increasing the estate tax — from 17% in the control group to around 53%; the paper reports that the treatment "triples the share of respondents supporting an increase" (the abstract states it "more than doubles" support). A standalone estate-tax slide more than doubled support, and even a four-sentence purely factual version raised it by ~50%. This was by far the largest treatment effect in the experiment, dwarfing effects on income-tax or minimum-wage preferences (the preferred top-1% tax rate moved by less than 1 percentage point) [5].
- Source-of-wealth matters: Fisman, Gladstone, Kuziemko & Naidu (2020) found Americans favour markedly higher taxes on inherited wealth than on identical wealth that was earned or saved, once the source is made explicit [6].
- Bastani & Waldenström (2021), in Sweden — a country that *abolished* its inheritance tax — found that informing respondents about the large aggregate importance of inherited wealth and its link to inequality of opportunity significantly increased support for inheritance taxation [7].
- Endowment-side polling is thinner than often assumed: the Resolution Foundation proposed a £10,000 "citizen's inheritance" at 25 [8], but no published poll directly testing majority support for it could be located (unverified); the nearest evidence is Demos's finding that 76% prefer keeping IHT revenue for public services or debt reduction over abolishing the tax (see [`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md)) [13].
- **Nobody has seriously polled a 100% marginal rate above a generous threshold.** This is the single largest evidence gap for this project.

The cross-national consistency matters: this is not a quirk of one media ecosystem. Whatever explains it must be psychological and structural, not merely propagandistic — which is the subject of [framing.md](framing.md).

## 2. The three paradoxes

**Paradox 1: People oppose a tax they will never pay.** Only 4.62% of UK deaths resulted in an IHT charge in 2022–23 (31,500 taxpaying estates, per HMRC) [14], and low-single-digit percentages of *recipients* would be affected by this project's £500k threshold ([`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md)). Yet opposition runs at 50–70%. Survey work consistently finds respondents believe the tax reaches far further down the wealth distribution than it does: 31% of Britons expect IHT to apply to the assets they leave [1], and in 2003 49% of Americans believed "most people have to pay" the estate tax [4][5].

**Paradox 2: The same people support the tax's *purpose*.** Majorities in the same populations say wealth inequality is too high, that the rich should pay more, and — critically — that people should get ahead by effort rather than birth. Fisman et al. show that when wealth is explicitly labelled as inherited, punitive tax preferences emerge [6]. The opposition is to the *frame* ("tax on death, on family, on Dad's house"), not to the *function* (breaking dynastic transmission).

**Paradox 3: "Keep it in the family" beats abstract inequality.** When the question pits a concrete family (mine) against a statistical inequality (everyone's), family wins. This is the finding the citizen's endowment is engineered to reverse: it gives every family a concrete stake on the *receiving* side. See [`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md) and [framing.md](framing.md).

## 3. The experimental evidence: opinion is soft where it looks hard

| Study | Treatment | Effect on estate/inheritance-tax support |
|---|---|---|
| Kuziemko, Norton, Saez, Stantcheva (2015, AER) | Information including how few estates pay | Largest effect in the study; support for increasing the tax roughly tripled, from 17% to ~53% (+35.7pp); a standalone estate-tax slide more than doubled support [5] |
| Fisman, Gladstone, Kuziemko, Naidu (2020, J. Pub. Econ.) | Vary the *source* of wealth in vignettes | Respondents tax inherited wealth significantly more heavily than earned wealth [6] |
| Bastani & Waldenström (2021, Economica) | Information on inherited wealth's aggregate importance and link to opportunity | Significant increase in Swedish support for inheritance taxation [7] |
| Stantcheva (2021, QJE, "Understanding Tax Policy") | Open-ended reasoning elicitation | Estate-tax attitudes driven by fairness reasoning and misperceived incidence more than self-interest [10] |
| German survey experiments (e.g. Gross, Lorek & Richter, 2017) | Vignettes varying heir, asset type and bequest size | Preferred "fair" tax rates fall for close kin and for family homes/family firms, and rise with bequest value and the heir's income [11] |

Two implications. First, the movement's core communications task is *information delivery*, not value change — a far easier task (see [`../14-communications/narratives.md`](../14-communications/narratives.md)). Second, the effect sizes are large enough that opponents know it too: their strategy depends on keeping incidence vague and salience low until a repeal window opens ([`../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md`](../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md)).

## 4. Polling on the benefit side: endowments and baby bonds

- Resolution Foundation (2018, *A New Generational Contract*) proposed a £10,000 citizen's inheritance at 25, funded by replacing IHT with a lifetime receipts tax [8]; the claim that its polling found majority support for the policy, rising when the funding source was named, could not be confirmed (unverified).
- IPPR's Commission on Economic Justice (2018) proposed a Citizens' Wealth Fund paying a universal capital dividend of £10,000 to 25-year-olds [8]; published polling showing majorities for that specific instrument could not be confirmed (unverified).
- US baby-bond polling (post-2018, following the Booker proposal and Hamilton–Darity work) is mixed rather than uniformly net-positive: Data for Progress found sharp splits by education and race (college-educated white voters opposed a $5,000-at-birth "baby bond" 53% to 28%), while a 2023 ACLU-commissioned YouGov poll found majority support among Black (73%), Latine (64%) and white (54%) voters and majorities of Democrats, independents and Republicans [12].
- The Alaska Permanent Fund Dividend is the strongest revealed-preference evidence: universal cash from a common asset is, once established, politically untouchable across party lines.

The design lesson is already embedded in [`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md): lead with the endowment. The polling lesson here is narrower — *no published survey has tested the full package* (100% above £500k per recipient + £50k at 25) as a single instrument.

## 5. What we still do not know

1. **100%-rate polling does not meaningfully exist.** Published instruments test rate rises within conventional ranges (40% → 50–60%) or abolition. Whether a *binary* 0/100 design with a generous threshold polls better or worse than a high progressive rate is unknown — and plausibly better, since the 0% side ("first £500k completely tax-free, guaranteed") is a stronger promise than any current regime makes.
2. **Per-recipient lifetime framing** has thin direct evidence outside a handful of European survey experiments [11].
3. **Package effects.** Does pairing with the endowment move opposition, or only intensity? Does hypothecation language ("your £50k is funded by...") outperform generic "public services"?
4. **Durability.** The estate-tax effect in Kuziemko et al. was unusually persistent — 58% of the effect size remained at the one-month follow-up, still more than doubling support — but the wider literature they review finds information effects typically dissipating within days or weeks [5]. One-shot persuasion is not the same as a stable coalition — which is why [`../13-movement/theory-of-change.md`](../13-movement/theory-of-change.md) matters more than any single poll.
5. **Salience risk.** Raising the issue's salience historically helped *opponents* (see [case-studies.md](case-studies.md)). The conditions under which salience helps reformers are untested.

## 6. Reading the polls correctly: three methodological cautions

Before any headline number from this file is deployed, three distinctions must be enforced:

1. **Support vs salience.** IHT scores terribly on fairness ratings but is nowhere near the top of "most important issue" lists: taxation as a whole registers only around one-in-ten mentions in Ipsos's unprompted Issues Index even at Budget-period peaks, far behind immigration, the economy and the NHS, and IHT does not feature as a standalone issue [2]. Ipsos likewise found IHT ranked only sixth as a priority for tax cuts (14%) despite topping its unfairness table [2]. Low-salience opposition is cheap to hold and cheap to flip; the danger zone is *high-salience opposition*, which historically appears only when the tax visibly reaches ordinary homes ([case-studies.md](case-studies.md), UK 2007) or when an organised campaign spikes it (US repeal).
2. **Question-wording sensitivity is the finding, not a nuisance.** Demos found that 55% initially say inheritances should always be completely tax-free, but when shown specific amounts only 21% still say all inheritance should be tax-free — 75% choose a specific tax threshold instead [13]. That gap is not measurement error — it *is* the measurement. Any commissioned poll must carry both framings in split-sample, or its result is an artefact of the commissioner's politics.
3. **Stated preference vs coalition behaviour.** A respondent persuaded in a survey has not joined anything. The literature's decay findings [5] mean polls measure the *persuadable surface*, while passage requires the organised depth built in [`../13-movement/`](../13-movement/README.md). Treat every favourable poll as an option to exercise, not an asset in hand.

| P10 | UK riches line — "where does too rich to inherit begin?" | Replicate the Dutch riches-line vignette instrument [16]: households described concretely (house, second home, savings, cars, holidays), respondents classify from "just enough" to "extremely rich"; add a receipts variant asking at what *inherited* amount taxation should reach 100% | Locates the public's own threshold and tests whether £500k sits below, at, or above where voters place "too rich to inherit" ([`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md)) |

P10 imports a proven instrument rather than inventing one. Robeyns, Buskens, van de Rijt, Vergeldt and van der Lippe measured where a representative Dutch sample (n=2,561, LISS panel) places the "riches line" — the wealth level at which a household counts as extremely rich — and found striking consensus: almost no one rated vignettes below €1m extremely rich, a majority had crossed the line by a little over €2m (geometric mean €2.2m; the pivotal vignette featured a villa, a second home in southern France and €1m in savings) [16]. Two campaign-relevant lessons travel with the method: the public *can* place a consensual line when asked concretely rather than abstractly, and — in the same study — support for limiting wealth was low in the abstract but rose significantly when concrete cases and vulnerable beneficiaries were in frame, the exact pattern of the Demos threshold finding in §6 [13][16].

The two claims marked (unverified) — polled majority support for the Resolution Foundation citizen's inheritance and for the IPPR Citizens' Wealth Fund — could not be confirmed and must not be used in manifesto material without further verification.
