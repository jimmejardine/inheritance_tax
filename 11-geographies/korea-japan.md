# Korea and Japan: The High-Rate Incumbents

**Executive summary.** South Korea and Japan operate the highest inheritance-tax rates in the OECD (top statutory rates of 50% and 55%, 60% on Korean controlling stakes [1][2]) with accepted state capacity and, in Korea's case, the world's most aggressive enforcement against controlling-shareholder dynasties — making them the countries that have travelled furthest toward this proposal's rate, though not its structure. Both prove that heavy inheritance taxation can coexist with advanced capitalism and strong family norms; neither is currently trending toward a 100% rate, and Korea's live reform debate is about *cutting*.

## Korea: dynasty politics as the engine

### The Samsung saga as the world's largest inheritance-tax event

The death of Lee Kun-hee (2020) produced an inheritance-tax assessment on the family of about ₩12 trillion — roughly $8bn, the largest such bill in Korean history — paid over five years in six instalments, partly through share sales and the donation of over 23,000 artworks to the state, with the final instalment completed in May 2026 [4]. Add the earlier merger-and-succession manoeuvres that put Lee Jae-yong in court, and Korea offers the clearest real-world demonstration that (a) very large fortunes *can* be made to pay at scale, and (b) the attempt to avoid succession tax is itself what corrupts — the governance abuses prosecuted in the chaebol cases were substantially succession-tax avoidance schemes ([`../01-problem/political-capture.md`](../01-problem/political-capture.md), [`../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md`](../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md)).

### Structure and sentiment

- Top statutory rate 50% above ₩3bn [5], with a 20% surcharge on controlling ("largest shareholder") stakes in large companies pushing the effective top rate to 60% [1] — the only regime that explicitly taxes *control premia*, an anti-dynastic instrument this proposal should study.
- Public sentiment is strongly anti-dynastic: the "gold spoon / dirt spoon" (*geumsujeo/heuksujeo*) class discourse maps exactly onto [`../01-problem/meritocracy-legitimacy.md`](../01-problem/meritocracy-legitimacy.md).
- **Counter-current:** the mid-2020s policy debate ran toward *reduction* — the government's 2024 bill cutting the top rate to 40% was rejected by the National Assembly in December 2024, and its 2025 bill converting from an estate basis to a recipient-acquisition basis (targeted for 2028) stalled in the Assembly in December 2025 [3][6]. The conversion half is convergence with this proposal's structure; the rate half is retreat. Business lobbies argue the current burden forces firm sales to private equity or triggers relocation ([`../04-case-against/family-business.md`](../04-case-against/family-business.md)).

### Confucian family norms cut both ways

Obligation to transmit the family enterprise intact fuels resistance; but the same ethic supplies the *anti*-dynastic argument's force — unearned third-generation control violates the meritocratic-examination tradition, and chaebol heirs' scandals (the "nut rage" genre) are read as moral failure of inherited position. Korean public opinion simultaneously supports taxing chaebol succession heavily and worries about "excessive" burdens on ordinary firms — the same split the threshold design in [`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md) is built to exploit.

## Japan: capacity plus demography

- Top rate 55% above ¥600m per statutory heir; the 2015 reform *broadened* the base by cutting the basic deduction from ¥50m + ¥10m per statutory heir to ¥30m + ¥6m, raising the share of deaths incurring tax from 4.4% (2014) to 8.0% (2015) and above 10% by 2024 [2] — a rare rich-democracy example of inheritance-tax *expansion* passing without major backlash.
- The Japanese computation (aggregate estate notionally divided among statutory heirs, tax computed per share) is already half-way to a recipient basis.
- The dankai generation's savings — a household financial-asset stock of ¥2,286tn as of September 2025 (BOJ flow-of-funds) [7] — is transferring now; the state actively runs early-gifting incentive schemes to accelerate it, showing transfer timing is already a live policy lever ([`../07-implementation/gifts-and-timing.md`](../07-implementation/gifts-and-timing.md)).
- Obstacles: consensus politics without a radical-reform tradition in peacetime; weak housing grievance; an endowment would compete with acute pension/care spending claims; and low-salience politics — inheritance tax is accepted, not contested, and nobody is campaigning to raise it.

## Structural comparison

| Dimension | Korea | Japan | This proposal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Basis | Estate (recipient-acquisition switch pending, targeted 2028 [3]) | Hybrid: estate aggregated, computed per statutory heir | Recipient, lifetime-cumulative |
| Top statutory rate | 50% (+ controlling-share premium) | 55% | 100% marginal |
| Effective threshold | ~₩1bn via lump-sum (₩500m) plus minimum spouse (₩500m) deductions [8] | ¥30m + ¥6m per heir basic deduction [2] | £500k/$750k per recipient, indexed |
| Business succession relief | Contested; narrower than Europe | Deferral-based *jigyō shōkei* regime — 100% deferral on unlisted shares since 2018 [2] | 30-year deferral, state equity option |
| Revenue destination | General budget | General budget | Hypothecated universal endowment |
| Anti-avoidance | Real-name finance, aggressive audit | Gift-timing rules, 7-year clawback (extended from 3 years by the 2023 reform, effective 2024 [9]) | Full spine in [`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) |

The instructive asymmetry: both countries reached near-maximal *rates* without ever adopting the *pairing* — no endowment, no hypothecation — and consequently both taxes survive on inertia and enforcement rather than on a constituency. They are stress-tests of rate tolerance, not previews of the political economy this proposal builds.

## What to watch

- **Korea:** the fate of the recipient-basis ("inheritance acquisition tax") conversion bill and any top-rate cut — submitted May 2025, stalled in the Assembly in December 2025, still pending [3][6]; the next chaebol succession event and its tax structuring; Democratic Party positioning on chaebol governance after each scandal cycle.
- **Japan:** further unification of gift and inheritance taxation (the direction of the 2023 reform [9]); whether ageing-driven fiscal packages reach for the inheritance base again as in 2015; LDP tax commission (*zeichō*) annual outlines, where all real Japanese tax policy is written.
- **Both:** any bilateral or OECD-level interest in transfer-tax coordination — as the highest-rate incumbents, Korea and Japan are the natural *demandeurs* for a floor that stops their wealthy relocating to Singapore ([unlikely-and-havens.md](unlikely-and-havens.md), [`../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md`](../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md)).

## Campaign use of the East Asian record

- **Against "no advanced economy could sustain this":** Korea and Japan have run 50–55% top rates for decades while leading the world in industrial sophistication — the single most economical rebuttal available ([`../14-communications/debate-toolkit.md`](../14-communications/debate-toolkit.md)).
- **Against "the rich are untouchable":** the Samsung assessment shows a determined administration collecting ten-figure sums from the most powerful family in the country, in instalments completed in May 2026, without capital flight or corporate collapse [4] (post-payment record is material for [`../08-capital-flight/evidence.md`](../08-capital-flight/evidence.md)).
- **For the anti-dynastic frame:** chaebol succession scandals supply the narrative library for [`../01-problem/political-capture.md`](../01-problem/political-capture.md) — concrete, prosecuted, non-hypothetical cases where inheritance preservation corrupted governance.
- **Caution:** do not oversell — both taxes are estate-structured, endowment-free, and politically embattled; they prove rate tolerance and enforcement capacity, nothing more.

## Analytic judgement

Korea is the more probable adopter of something *recognisably* like this proposal — its anti-dynastic politics could, under a reformist government and a chaebol scandal window ([crisis-windows.md](crisis-windows.md)), push a recipient-based restructure upward rather than downward, and its threshold politics would follow the same "protect ordinary families, cap dynasties" line as [`../02-proposal/`](../02-proposal/README.md). Japan is the more probable *quiet convergence* case: incremental base-broadening and rate creep under fiscal pressure, without manifesto politics. Neither will pair the tax with a universal endowment unaided — that idea has no local lineage, and exporting it is a communications task ([`../14-communications/audience-messaging.md`](../14-communications/audience-messaging.md)).
