# The Unlikely and the Havens

**Executive summary.** Switzerland, Singapore, the Gulf states, Monaco, and the smaller haven jurisdictions will not adopt this proposal in any foreseeable window — by referendum verdict, by business model, or by constitutional identity — and the campaign should say so plainly rather than pretend universality. Their significance is systematically overrated: havens matter only to the degree that big economies leave the doors open, and the proposal's dual-nexus, exit-tail, and coordination architecture ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md), [`../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md`](../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md)) is designed precisely so that a handful of refusers cannot veto everyone else.

## Switzerland: the referendum verdict

Switzerland is the definitive consensus-institution case. Inheritance taxation is cantonal; spouses are exempt in every canton that levies the tax, direct descendants are exempt in virtually all cantons (Appenzell Innerrhoden, Neuchâtel, and Vaud are the exceptions), Schwyz levies no inheritance tax at all, and cantons compete on the margin [1]. The decisive datum: the 2015 federal popular initiative "Millionen-Erbschaften besteuern" — a 20% federal estate tax above CHF 2m, earmarked for the pension system — was rejected on 14 June 2015 by 71% of voters and all 26 cantons [2]. The 2025 JUSO initiative for a 50% tax on estates and gifts above CHF 50m fared even worse: 78.3% no, again in every canton, on 30 November 2025 [3]. Two readings matter:

1. **The institutional reading.** Even a moderate rate with a high threshold and popular earmarking cannot pass a wealth-holding, referendum-based polity where cantonal treasuries and the private-banking sector campaign against it. This is C4 = 0: the veto is the median voter herself, primed by an asset-management ecosystem.
2. **The campaign reading.** Opponents will cite 2015 as "the people rejected this." The honest answer: Switzerland is the *most* wealth-defensive institutional environment on earth, its vote generalises to nowhere with majoritarian institutions, and even there the initiative put the question on the national agenda ([`../14-communications/debate-toolkit.md`](../14-communications/debate-toolkit.md)).

Switzerland's other role is as destination: the Kamprad precedent ([nordics.md](nordics.md)), lump-sum taxation (*Pauschalbesteuerung*) for wealthy foreigners, and forfait deals make it the default European exit option — the pivotal chair in [`../08-capital-flight/exit-options.md`](../08-capital-flight/exit-options.md).

## Singapore, the Gulf, Monaco: refusal as business model

- **Singapore** abolished estate duty for deaths on or after 15 February 2008 [4], with growing the wealth-management industry an explicit part of the case; its family-office regime (13O/13U incentives) actively recruits the fortunes other countries might tax. State capacity is world-class; the *choice* is the point. Domestic inequality politics (HDB-vs-landed divide) occasionally surfaces wealth-tax talk from establishment figures — MAS managing director Ravi Menon floated a property-gains or inheritance tax in his 2021 IPS lectures [5] — but transfer taxation would contradict the national development model.
- **Gulf states (UAE foremost)** run zero-personal-tax models with golden-visa recruitment of the globally wealthy; Sharia forced-heirship rules govern succession for Muslims, an entirely different legal frame. Adoption probability is effectively zero and no campaign resource should be spent pretending otherwise.
- **Monaco, Liechtenstein, Channel Islands, Caribbean territories** are constitutionally *constituted* as havens; several are dependencies of larger states (UK, France), which is the actual pressure point — haven policy is metropolitan policy at one remove ([uk.md](uk.md), [`../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md`](../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md)).

## Why their non-adoption matters less than assumed

The instinctive objection — "the money will just go to Monaco" — misreads where enforcement power lives. Four structural points, argued fully in [`../08-capital-flight/`](../08-capital-flight/README.md):

1. **The tax follows persons and situs, not flags.** Under the dual-nexus rule ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md)), transfers to a resident of an adopting country are taxable wherever the assets sit, and domestic real property is taxable whoever inherits it. A haven helps only if the *recipient* genuinely relocates their life — and the 10-year exit tail prices that.
2. **Havens hold accounts, not lives.** The great wealth transfer's assets are overwhelmingly housing, pensions, and operating businesses physically inside the big economies ([crisis-windows.md](crisis-windows.md)); these cannot be moved to Singapore, only their owners can, and observed migration responses to taxation are small for settled populations, though concentrated in specific mobile groups ([`../08-capital-flight/evidence.md`](../08-capital-flight/evidence.md); Kleven et al. 2020 [6]).
3. **The CRS/FATCA precedent.** Automatic exchange of financial-account information was imposed *on* the havens by G20 market power within a decade of being dismissed as utopian; a transfer-tax floor rides the same rails ([`../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md`](../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md)).
4. **Defection has a natural ceiling.** A haven absorbing exiled dynasties imports their political capture problem ([`../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md`](../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md)); jurisdictions selling stability face pressure to cap fugitive-wealth intake when reputational cost rises — though the post-2022 record is mixed: the UAE absorbed a multi-billion-dollar Russian influx while on the FATF grey list [7], then tightened its anti-money-laundering regime and exited the list in 2024 [8].

The honest residual: a world where the US refuses ([anglosphere.md](anglosphere.md)) is a world where the haven question stays live, because the largest fortunes have a large domestic-market refuge. Haven-proofing is downstream of big-economy coordination, not of haven conversion.

## Haven typology and pressure points

Not all refusers are alike; each type has a distinct lever ([`../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md`](../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md) develops the coordination mechanics):

| Type | Examples | What they sell | Pressure point |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sovereign consensus-democracy | Switzerland | Stability, banking, lump-sum deals | G20 market access; the CRS precedent — Swiss bank secrecy fell to US/EU pressure, not domestic conversion |
| Sovereign city-state | Singapore, UAE (Dubai) | Residence, family-office regimes | Reputational tiering, treaty access, financial-infrastructure dependence on big-economy clearing |
| Metropolitan dependency | Jersey, Guernsey, IoM, Cayman, BVI; Monaco (French customs/currency union) | Legal wrappers, trusts, zero-tax residence | Direct: the metropole legislates or withholds — the UK's SAMLA 2018 s.51 duty to impose public beneficial-ownership registers on non-compliant OTs by Order in Council [9] |
| Micro-sovereign | Liechtenstein, Andorra | Foundations, anstalts | EEA/EU adjacency; both capitulated on banking transparency post-2008 |
| Internal havens | South Dakota/Nevada trusts; Madrid's bonifications | Perpetuities, situs shopping *inside* federations | Federal supremacy — the cheapest coordination problem to solve and the most neglected ([anglosphere.md](anglosphere.md), [continental-europe.md](continental-europe.md)) |

The typology's punchline: the majority of the world's haven capacity is *not sovereign*. It is dependencies of the very states this section ranks as likeliest adopters, plus internal arbitrage within them. A UK-led move ([uk.md](uk.md)) carries its Crown Dependencies and Overseas Territories with it or forfeits its own base — which is why haven policy is a chapter of adopter policy, not a separate diplomatic project.

## What to watch

- Swiss cantonal politics: any canton reintroducing direct-descendant taxation, or further JUSO-style federal initiatives — each loses but each re-normalises the question (the 2025 initiative lost 78.3% to 21.7% [3]).
- Singapore's family-office incentive reviews: MAS tightened the 13O/13U qualifying criteria in July 2023 [10] and strengthened money-laundering defences for single family offices after the S$3bn 2023 laundering case — six of whose figures were linked to tax-incentivised family-office funds [11][12] — showing even the model haven prices reputation.
- UK policy toward Crown Dependencies and Overseas Territories on beneficial-ownership registers — the live test of metropolitan leverage.
- UAE golden-visa and zero-tax positioning as EU/OECD lists evolve; any Gulf state joining CRS-successor frameworks.
- US internal-haven politics: state legislation on perpetual trusts and any federal interest in a minimum rule against situs shopping ([anglosphere.md](anglosphere.md)).

## Campaign framing

- Do not debate haven hypotheticals in the abstract; name the mechanism — "your inheritance is a Surrey house and a UK pension; Monaco cannot hold either" ([`../14-communications/debate-toolkit.md`](../14-communications/debate-toolkit.md)).
- Use the CRS/FATCA arc as the standard reply to "the havens will never cooperate": they said that about bank secrecy, and bank secrecy is dead ([`../14-communications/inoculation.md`](../14-communications/inoculation.md)).
- Treat the Swiss 2015 result as an institutional datum, not a popularity datum, and pre-empt its citation accordingly ([`../09-objections/whataboutery.md`](../09-objections/whataboutery.md)).

## Analytic judgement

Write these jurisdictions off as adopters and price them in as adversaries: expect Swiss private banking, Singaporean family-office promoters, and dependency governments to fund the international opposition case ([`../10-opposition/astroturf-and-lobbying.md`](../10-opposition/astroturf-and-lobbying.md)). The campaign posture is containment — dual nexus, exit tails, situs rules, metropolitan pressure on dependencies, and a G20-level floor — rather than persuasion. One reframing worth deploying: every generation of tax history has had its refusers, and the refusers did not stop the income tax, VAT, or CRS from becoming the world's norm; they merely arrived last.

The ranking consequence for [README.md](README.md): these jurisdictions occupy the table's floor not because the analysis is incomplete but because refusal is, for each of them, either constitutionally entrenched or commercially constitutive — the honest treatment is containment, and the containment tools already exist.
