# Exit Options: Where Would They Actually Go?

**Executive summary.** The flight objection assumes an abundant supply of attractive destinations, but the realistic list is short: a handful of micro-jurisdictions and preferential regimes, each with binding capacity constraints, high entry prices, and real costs in culture, distance, family, and rule of law. The aggregate carrying capacity of the world's tax havens is a small fraction of the population that would be affected by the proposed tax — scarcity of exit is itself a structural defence (developed further in [game-theory.md](game-theory.md)).

## Headline findings

- Only a few jurisdictions combine zero-or-negligible death taxation with genuine liveability for a UK/US family; each is small, expensive, and in several cases actively rationing entry [1][2].
- The EU has moved against golden-passport and golden-visa schemes — Cyprus's programme was abolished from 1 November 2020, Malta's citizenship-by-investment was ruled contrary to EU law by the CJEU on 29 April 2025 (Case C-181/23), and Portugal closed NHR to new applicants from 2024 [3][4][6][8].
- The regimes that remain (Swiss forfait, Italian flat tax, UAE residence) tax-shelter the *migrant's* worldwide income — they do nothing about UK/US-*situs* assets, which under the proposal's dual nexus remain in charge regardless of where the owner or heir lives ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md)).
- Emigration solves the donor's problem only if the *heirs* also emigrate and remain non-resident past the 10-year tail — the compound requirement analysed in [friction-and-anchors.md](friction-and-anchors.md).

## The realistic destination list

| Jurisdiction | What it offers | What it costs | Capacity constraint |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Monaco** | No personal income tax since 1869; no inheritance tax on direct-line transfers for Monaco-situs assets; Mediterranean, francophone, secure | Residency requires proof of accommodation plus a reference from a Monaco bank — no statutory minimum, but banks typically require a deposit of around €500k, sometimes €1m+ [9] — in the world's most expensive housing market; ~2 km²; French nationals excluded from the tax benefit by treaty | Population ~38,000; effectively full — new supply is land reclamation; waiting lists for property and schools [1] |
| **Dubai / UAE** | No personal income, inheritance, or wealth tax; golden-visa residence; world-class logistics and services | Distance from European family; summer climate; civil-law/Sharia interaction historically required DIFC wills for non-Muslim succession; thin common-law protections outside financial free zones; reputational discount | Absorptive capacity larger than Monaco but the expatriate elite ecosystem is still a single city; geopolitical and reputational risk priced in [2] |
| **Singapore** | Estate duty abolished 2008; strong rule of law; premier Asian wealth hub | 60% additional buyer's stamp duty on foreign residential purchases (doubled from 30% on 27 April 2023) [7]; tightened family-office qualification rules; strict social regime; 10,000+ km from UK family | City-state actively managing inflow; signalled it does not want to be a pure haven [2] |
| **Switzerland (forfait)** | Lump-sum ("expenditure-based") taxation for foreigners without Swiss employment; most cantons exempt direct descendants from inheritance tax; stable, central, high rule of law | Forfait abolished in Zurich (2009 referendum) and several cantons; minimum deemed-expenditure bases raised; a 2014 federal initiative to abolish it failed but keeps it politically contested; high cost of living | Cantonal discretion means entry is negotiated and rationed; political tolerance for wealthy foreigners is a live referendum issue [1][5] |
| **Italy (flat-tax regime)** | Substitute flat tax (€100k, doubled to €200k for arrivals after 10 August 2024) [10] on foreign income for new residents, up to 15 years; inheritance tax low (4% direct line, €1m allowance); culture, climate, proximity | Italian-situs and (for the UK-origin migrant) UK-situs assets not sheltered; administrative friction; regime is young and politically revisable — the 2024 doubling shows repricing risk | No hard cap, but the regime's own instability is the constraint [10] |
| **Portugal (NHR)** | The 2009–2023 Non-Habitual Resident regime offered 10-year exemptions on much foreign income | Closed to new applicants from January 2024 after housing-cost backlash; successor regime (IFICI, "NHR 2.0") narrowed to scientific-research, innovation, and highly qualified professions [8] | Effectively closed as a general wealth-migration route — a live demonstration that haven regimes get repealed under domestic political pressure [8] |
| **Caribbean CBI (St Kitts, etc.)** | Citizenship by investment from low six figures; no direct taxes in several islands | A travel document, not a life: small societies, limited healthcare and schooling, hurricane exposure; visa-free-access revocations — the UK imposed visa requirements on Dominica and Vanuatu in July 2023, citing "clear and evident abuse" of their CBI schemes [11] | Trivial absorptive capacity for actual residence; most buyers never move [4] |
| **Malta / (formerly Cyprus)** | EU access via investment migration; Malta remittance-basis taxation | CJEU ruled Malta's citizenship-by-investment contrary to EU law (29 April 2025, Case C-181/23) [4]; Cyprus abolished its programme from 1 November 2020 after an undercover-investigation scandal [6]; sustained Commission pressure | EU is closing this class of route in real time [4] |
| **Israel** | 10-year exemption on foreign income/reporting for new immigrants; no inheritance tax | Available only via aliyah eligibility; regional security; distance | Structurally limited to a specific population [2] |

## The haven set is repricing against the migrant

The destination list is not static: over the past decade nearly every route has become more expensive, narrower, or closed. Betting a family's multi-decade domicile strategy on the stability of a haven regime is itself a risk the folk model ignores.

| Year | Event | Effect on the exit market |
|---|---|---|
| 2009 | Zurich abolishes the Swiss forfait by referendum; other cantons follow or raise minimums | Flagship Swiss route narrowed; political risk demonstrated |
| 2014 | Swiss federal initiative to abolish forfait nationwide fails — but keeps abolition permanently on the ballot | Regime survives under standing threat |
| 2020 | Cyprus closes citizenship-by-investment after undercover-investigation scandal | EU passport route closed |
| 2023 | Singapore raises additional buyer's stamp duty on foreign residential purchases to 60% [7]; tightens family-office rules | Premier Asian route repriced sharply upward |
| 2023–24 | Portugal ends general NHR after housing backlash [8] | Most popular mid-tier European route closed |
| 2024 | Italy doubles its flat-tax charge for new residents €100k → €200k [10] | Unilateral repricing mid-regime; signals revisability |
| 2025 | CJEU rules Malta's investor-citizenship scheme contrary to EU law [4] | Last EU golden passport closed |
| 2025 | UK abolishes non-dom status | The UK itself exits the haven-lite market |

The direction is uniform. Destination-country electorates dislike inbound wealth migration (housing costs, inequality optics), and the EU institutional machinery is actively hostile to citizenship-and-residence sales — *why* courting jurisdictions' own voters keep repealing these regimes, and the referendum record on both sides, is the subject of [poaching-and-haven-politics.md](poaching-and-haven-politics.md). The wealthy family planning a thirty-year avoidance strategy must bet that its chosen regime — typically a political concession sustained by a thin domestic constituency — outlasts three decades of this trend. Portugal's NHR lasted fourteen years.

## Who actually uses these routes

The realistic user is not the settled UK business owner but the already-multi-jurisdictional family office: liquid wealth, no operating business tying them down, children in international schools, existing residences in three countries. This population is real but small, is precisely the Moretti–Wilson high-responsiveness tail ([evidence.md](evidence.md) §3), and is the design target of the exit charge and trailing tail in [exit-tax-design.md](exit-tax-design.md) — not the marginal case for the regime, because much of its wealth is already outside any single jurisdiction's net under *current* law. The proposal's incremental loss from this group is correspondingly modest; what matters is preventing the *settled* wealthy from joining it, which is the anchor analysis of [friction-and-anchors.md](friction-and-anchors.md).

## Three structural observations

### 1. Havens shelter people, not situs assets

Every regime above operates on the migrant's *residence*. None can re-situs a Mayfair freehold, a UK-registered company, a Delaware holding of US real assets, or an operating business with a UK workforce. Under the proposal's dual nexus (recipient residence *or* asset situs — [`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) §5), moving to Monaco changes the tax on UK-situs inheritances not at all. The genuine escape requires liquidating domestic assets and re-deploying them abroad — a taxable, visible, decades-long project analysed in [friction-and-anchors.md](friction-and-anchors.md).

### 2. Capacity is small and rationed

Monaco's population would fit inside a mid-sized London borough. Switzerland's forfait population was about 4,600 at the end of 2018 (4,557 people, paying CHF 821m in tax — fewer than 0.1% of Swiss taxpayers) [5]. The population potentially affected by a £500k-per-recipient threshold is orders of magnitude larger than the combined absorptive capacity of every jurisdiction in the table. Havens also self-ration: Singapore's stamp-duty wall, Swiss cantonal referenda, Portugal's NHR repeal, and EU action against golden passports all show destination-country politics turning against inbound wealth migration — the haven side of the market is *shrinking* while transparency infrastructure grows ([international-coordination.md](international-coordination.md)).

### 3. The price is paid in life, not money

The table's "costs" column is the under-weighted term in the folk model. Moving to escape a death tax means spending one's final decades — precisely the years of grandchildren, familiar healthcare, and accumulated social position — in a jurisdiction chosen for its tax code. Sweden's celebrity exiles were working-age entrepreneurs with global businesses; the marginal 70-year-old UK testator is a different case entirely. The full anchor analysis is in [friction-and-anchors.md](friction-and-anchors.md); the positive retention case is in [retention-attraction.md](retention-attraction.md).

## What would change this assessment

The exit-options analysis is falsifiable, and honest monitoring should watch for: (a) a large, credible, rule-of-law jurisdiction — a hypothetical "Switzerland at scale" — deliberately building capacity for inbound wealth migration rather than rationing it; (b) the UAE or Singapore reversing their current repricing trend and courting UK/US retirees specifically; (c) remote-work normalisation materially cutting the career cost of relocation for heirs (the step-5 constraint in [friction-and-anchors.md](friction-and-anchors.md)); or (d) an EU member successfully defending a new golden-visa regime against Commission pressure. None of these is currently observed; each would warrant revisiting the capacity-constraint argument in [game-theory.md](game-theory.md).

## Research gaps

- Swiss forfait minimum deemed-expenditure bases per canton, and headcounts more recent than the 2018 federal figure; Monaco bank-deposit practice is documented only by relocation-industry sources [9] and warrants confirmation against Monegasque official guidance.
- Track the post-2024 Italian flat-tax uptake and any further repricing (a rise to €300k for new arrivals was reported in 2025–26 budget coverage — confirm enactment); it is the most credible European competitor regime.
- Quantify the combined annual absorptive capacity (housing stock, school places) of the top five destinations against the UK/US population above the proposed threshold.
- Monitor EU infringement actions on remaining golden-visa schemes and the post-CJEU status of Malta's programme.
